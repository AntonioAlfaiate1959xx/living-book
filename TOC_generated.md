
## Contents — The One Hundred Questions

**Part I — Philosophical Foundations (Why Do We Educate?)**
- Q1 — What is the purpose of education in a world where machines can know, reason, and create?
- Q2 — If AI can answer any question, what does it mean to "learn" something?
- Q3 — Does human knowledge lose or change its value when it is no longer scarce?
- Q4 — What is the difference between knowledge, understanding, and wisdom — and which of these can AI possess or transmit?
- Q5 — How does the human relationship with truth change when answers are generated rather than discovered?
- Q6 — What is irreducibly human in the act of teaching, and what is irreducibly human in the act of learning?
- Q7 — Should education aim to make humans complementary to AI, resistant to it, or independent of it?
- Q8 — How does AI reshape our concept of intelligence itself — and the way schools measure and reward it?
- Q9 — What happens to intellectual struggle, effort, and "productive failure" when friction can always be removed?
- Q10 — Is there a moral difference between learning with AI, learning from AI, and outsourcing learning to AI?

**Part II — Ethical Dimensions**
- Q11 — Who is responsible when an AI teaches something false, biased, or harmful to a student?
- Q12 — How should student data collected by AI systems be governed, and who owns the learning profile of a child?
- Q13 — Does personalized AI tutoring risk creating algorithmic determinism — locking students into predicted trajectories?
- Q14 — How do we prevent AI from amplifying existing inequalities of class, language, geography, and disability in education?
- Q15 — Is it ethical to expose children to systems whose long-term cognitive and emotional effects are unknown?
- Q16 — What consent can a minor meaningfully give to AI-mediated education?
- Q17 — How do we preserve academic integrity when the boundary between assistance and authorship dissolves?
- Q18 — Should there be a "right to a human teacher," analogous to a right to human medical care?
- Q19 — How do we address the cultural and ideological biases embedded in models trained predominantly on certain languages and worldviews?
- Q20 — What ethical obligations do AI companies have toward education systems they disrupt but do not serve?

**Part III — AI’s Role in Teaching**
- Q21 — What can AI tutors do better than human teachers, and where do they categorically fail?
- Q22 — How should the teacher's role be redefined — from transmitter of knowledge to what, exactly?
- Q23 — What pedagogical models (Socratic, constructivist, mastery-based) does AI enable at scale for the first time?
- Q24 — How can AI support teachers (planning, assessment, feedback) without deskilling or replacing them?
- Q25 — What does effective human–AI co-teaching look like in a real classroom?
- Q26 — How should teacher training change so educators can critically use, evaluate, and challenge AI tools?
- Q27 — Can AI meaningfully teach non-cognitive skills — empathy, collaboration, resilience, character?
- Q28 — How do we assess learning authentically when AI can complete any traditional assessment?
- Q29 — What is the right age and developmental stage to introduce AI tools into learning?
- Q30 — How do we design AI systems that strengthen rather than substitute students' thinking (avoiding cognitive offloading)?

**Part IV — The Learner’s Experience**
- Q31 — How are students actually using AI — and how far does practice diverge from institutional intention?
- Q32 — What happens when students form relationships with AI — emotionally, socially, developmentally?
- Q33 — How do learners evaluate AI-generated information, and can epistemic vigilance be taught?
- Q34 — What is AI literacy, and should it become a universal core competence like reading and numeracy?
- Q35 — How does AI change learning for students with disabilities and diverse learning needs?
- Q36 — What does AI do to student motivation, agency, and the experience of ownership over learning?
- Q37 — In a world of instant answers, what still needs to be memorized, practiced, and internalized?

**Part V — Institutions and Systems**
- Q38 — Is the industrial-era school model (grades, classrooms, standardized curricula) still viable — and what replaces it?
- Q39 — How should curricula be redesigned when factual content is instantly accessible and rapidly obsolete?
- Q40 — What is the future of universities, credentials, and degrees when skills can be learned and demonstrated outside institutions?
- Q41 — How can public education systems keep pace with private AI development without surrendering governance to tech companies?
- Q42 — What policies and regulations should govern AI in schools — and who should set them: states, markets, or communities?
- Q43 — How do developing nations avoid a new "AI colonialism" in education while leapfrogging infrastructure gaps?

**Part VI — Work, Economy, and Society**
- Q44 — Which human capabilities will the labor market actually reward in 10, 20, and 50 years — and how do we teach for an unpredictable future?
- Q45 — How should education respond to a possible decoupling of learning from employment, if AI displaces large categories of work?
- Q46 — What is the role of education in a society where economic productivity no longer requires most human labor — education for meaning rather than for jobs?
- Q47 — How do we manage the transition for current workers whose skills are being obsoleted faster than retraining systems can respond?
- Q48 — How does AI in education affect democracy — the formation of citizens capable of judgment, deliberation, and resistance to manipulation?
- Q49 — What shared human culture and values should education transmit when AI fragments information into personalized realities?
- Q50 — Looking back from 2050: which decisions of this decade about AI and education will we most regret or celebrate — and how do we choose wisely now?

**Part VII — The Evidence Base: What Research Actually Shows**
- Q51 — What does current peer-reviewed evidence show about the effects of AI tutoring systems on learning outcomes, and how strong is that evidence?
- Q52 — What does research reveal about how students actually use generative AI when unsupervised — as distinct from how they report using it?
- Q53 — What is currently known from cognitive science about AI's effects on memory formation, retention, and transfer of learning?
- Q54 — What does the evidence show about AI's impact on writing skill development, from early literacy through academic and professional writing?
- Q55 — What have studies found about AI feedback compared with human feedback — in accuracy, usefulness, and student receptivity?
- Q56 — What does research show about the reliability of AI-detection tools, and what have institutions learned from relying on them?
- Q57 — What evidence exists on how AI use affects different learner profiles — high vs. low performers, younger vs. older students, novices vs. experts?
- Q58 — What do studies reveal about teachers' actual adoption patterns, workload effects, and attitudes toward AI tools?
- Q59 — What does current evidence show about AI's effects on student motivation, engagement, and persistence — and how do these findings differ from marketing claims?
- Q60 — Which widely repeated claims about AI in education lack solid evidence, and which early findings have failed to replicate?

**Part VIII — Field Experiences: Case Studies from Real Classrooms**
- Q61 — What have large-scale national AI-in-education initiatives (across different countries and income levels) achieved, and what have they abandoned?
- Q62 — What lessons emerge from schools and universities that banned AI, then reversed course — or embraced it, then restricted it?
- Q63 — What do documented deployments of AI tutors in low-resource and developing-world contexts show about what transfers and what fails?
- Q64 — How have universities restructured courses, assessments, and honor codes in response to generative AI, and with what results?
- Q65 — What have K–12 schools learned from integrating AI into daily instruction — the successes, failures, and unexpected consequences?
- Q66 — What experiences have emerged from AI use in vocational, technical, and professional training (medicine, law, engineering, trades)?
- Q67 — What can be learned from teachers who have become exemplary AI practitioners — what do they do differently?
- Q68 — What documented harms — from data breaches to well-being incidents to learning setbacks — have occurred in real AI deployments, and what changed as a result?

**Part IX — Policy and Governance in Practice**
- Q69 — Which national and regional AI-in-education regulations currently exist, and how do their approaches compare?
- Q70 — What guidance have major international bodies (UNESCO, OECD, EU, and others) issued on AI in education, and how is it being implemented?
- Q71 — What does an effective institutional AI policy contain, and what distinguishes policies that work from those ignored in practice?
- Q72 — How are education systems regulating student data privacy in AI tools, and which legal frameworks apply to minors?
- Q73 — How should institutions handle procurement of AI tools — evaluation criteria, vendor accountability, contract terms, and exit strategies?
- Q74 — How are examination and credentialing bodies redesigning high-stakes assessment for the AI era?
- Q75 — What academic-integrity frameworks have institutions adopted to replace binary "cheating/not cheating" models, and how are they working?
- Q76 — How are teacher unions, professional bodies, and accreditation agencies responding to AI, and what standards are they setting?
- Q77 — What funding models and public investments in AI for education exist, and what do early returns suggest about their effectiveness?

**Part X — Practical Navigation: Guides for Teachers, Students, and Leaders**
- Q78 — How should a teacher decide, lesson by lesson, when AI use helps learning and when it undermines it — is there a practical decision framework?
- Q79 — What are the current best practices for designing AI-resilient and AI-inclusive assignments and assessments?
- Q80 — How can teachers use AI effectively for planning, differentiation, feedback, and administration while safeguarding their professional judgment?
- Q81 — How should students use AI as a study partner in ways that demonstrably strengthen rather than weaken their learning?
- Q82 — What should students know about the limits, failure modes, and biases of AI systems before trusting their outputs?
- Q83 — How should students disclose and document their AI use in academic work, and how can they build integrity habits that also serve them professionally?
- Q84 — What practical steps allow school and university leaders to move from prohibition or paralysis to structured adoption — a realistic implementation roadmap?
- Q85 — How should institutions structure professional development so teachers gain real AI competence rather than one-off training?
- Q86 — How can schools communicate with and involve parents and communities in decisions about AI use?
- Q87 — What technical and organizational safeguards (age gates, data controls, human oversight, escalation paths) should any classroom AI deployment include?
- Q88 — How can educators and institutions with minimal budgets or infrastructure still give students equitable access to AI-supported learning?

**Part XI — Measurement and Evaluation**
- Q89 — How should the effectiveness of an AI intervention in education be measured — what outcomes, over what timescales, against what baselines?
- Q90 — What standards of evidence should decision makers demand from edtech vendors before adopting AI products at scale?
- Q91 — How can institutions monitor for unintended effects of AI — skill atrophy, dependency, equity gaps — that standard metrics miss?
- Q92 — What is known about the longitudinal effects of sustained AI use on learners, and how should long-term studies be designed and tracked?
- Q93 — How should learning analytics generated by AI systems be interpreted, validated, and protected from misuse in student evaluation?
- Q94 — What benchmarks and audits exist for evaluating the pedagogical quality, safety, and bias of educational AI models themselves, and what are their limits?

**Part XII — Frontiers: Emerging Territories**
- Q95 — How are multimodal and voice-based AI changing learning beyond text — in languages, arts, sciences, and physical skills?
- Q96 — What roles are autonomous AI agents beginning to play in education — as persistent tutors, course designers, or student proxies — and what new questions do they raise?
- Q97 — What is known and unknown about AI in early childhood education, where developmental stakes are highest?
- Q98 — How is AI reshaping special education and individualized support plans in practice, beyond its general accessibility promise?
- Q99 — How is AI transforming informal and out-of-school learning — self-teaching, homeschooling, communities of practice, and family learning?
- Q100 — What weak signals today — in research labs, startups, and experimental schools — most plausibly indicate what AI in education will look like in ten years?
