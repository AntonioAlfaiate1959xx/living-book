Chapter 1. Philosophical Foundations — Why Do We Educate?

What is the purpose of education in a world where machines can know, reason, and create?

**The Purpose of Education in a World Where Machines Can Know, Reason, and Create**

The question is not merely pedagogical — it is philosophical, and its urgency grows with every advance in machine capability. For most of human history, education was justified by its relationship to knowledge: schools and universities existed to transmit what was known, train minds in reasoning, and form the capacity to produce something new. Each of those three justifications is now under pressure. Generative AI was designed as a general-purpose technology, and some of its most common uses — producing finished work with minimal effort — can stand in direct tension with the purposes of learning. If a machine can retrieve, synthesize, and generate at speed and scale no human can match, the obvious question follows: what is left for education to do?

The least satisfying answer — though not a wrong one — is vocational retraining. AI fluency is now one of the job market's most in-demand skills, and students need the tools and guidance to develop relevant capabilities to thrive in their futures. Governments have responded accordingly: in April 2025, President Trump signed an executive order to create new educational and workforce development opportunities around AI, with the explicit goal of enabling America's young people to gain interest and expertise in AI technology from an early age. Yet workforce preparation has never been a sufficient account of education's purpose, and it is even less so now, when the nature of work itself is being redefined faster than curricula can be revised.

A more searching answer requires returning to what education was always, at its best, trying to do: not fill minds with information, but form the kind of person capable of using information well. Across time and place, education has been expected to cultivate individuals, sustain societies, transmit culture, and shape ethical values — purposes that coexist in tension, and that tension is not a weakness but the very condition that sustains the richness of human knowledge across generations. AI has not dissolved these purposes; it has clarified them by contrast. If artificial intelligence can outperform humans in reasoning, writing, and analysis, what remains uniquely human? The answer, one scholarly account argues, is not intelligence but wisdom — moral judgment, empathy, interpretive depth, and responsibility under uncertainty.

The distinction matters enormously in practice. AI produces outputs but does not interpret meaning; faculty must therefore shift from delivering information to fostering epistemic judgment. Students can now use AI to generate differential diagnoses or draft arguments, yet the real learning occurs when they weigh ethical, cultural, and contextual factors — judgments no algorithm can provide. Wisdom emerges from embodied human experience, relational resonance, ethical judgment, and a commitment to the common good — dimensions AI lacks due to its reliance on curated, syntactic representations of knowledge.

This is why the relational and social dimension of education is increasingly recognized as central rather than supplementary. Education is not only cognitive but also social. Educators and students broadly recognize that human judgment, assessment, and interaction provide irreplaceable value, and that AI alone cannot adequately address the multifaceted demands of education, including social and emotional development, personalized support, and adaptability to individual student needs. Higher education's future lies not in defending what AI does better, but in cultivating what AI cannot replicate: epistemic judgment, belonging, creativity, and wonder — and universities must reimagine themselves as human commons, spaces where meaning-making, ethical responsibility, and imagination thrive.

There is also a structural problem that no amount of philosophical clarification resolves on its own. Unlike traditional educational tools, which functioned purely as mediators through which students engaged with disciplinary content, AI is simultaneously a mediator and an object of learning — and faculty are therefore not simply adopting a new tool in service of existing goals, but renegotiating what those goals are. Assessment systems built around the production of essays or problem sets are visibly obsolete when machines can produce both fluently. The emerging consensus points toward authentic tasks — simulations, reflective narratives, collaborative projects — that emphasize reasoning and adaptability.

What emerges from this debate is not a single new purpose but a reprioritization of purposes that were always present but easy to neglect when information-handling seemed the main challenge. We are giving the next generation extraordinary power without the wisdom to wield it, and the most important question education can ask — the one Socrates never stopped asking — remains the simplest: what is the wise thing to do here? In a world where machines can know and create, education's irreducible task is to cultivate the judgment to decide what is worth knowing and what ought to be created — and to form people who take that responsibility seriously.

If AI can answer any question, what does it mean to "learn" something?

The question sounds deceptively simple, but it strikes at the deepest tension in contemporary education: if an answer is always one prompt away, what exactly is the human learner supposed to do?

The classical cognitive science answer is unambiguous on the distinction that matters most. Understanding means being able to *use* knowledge, as opposed to merely *having* it — and that requires knowledge to be integrated into some kind of mental structure. Cognitive learning, by this definition, is a process in which information is received, processed, and connected to existing knowledge, leading to a change in the learner's mental schemata. AI can supply information instantly, but it cannot perform that integration on behalf of another mind. The schemata either form in a particular brain, through effort and experience, or they do not form at all.

This distinction between possession and integration is not merely academic. Cognitive learning is less about what one knows and more about how one comes to know it — the mental steps taken to make sense of new information, including questioning, analyzing, and connecting it to prior knowledge. A student who asks an AI to explain the French Revolution and reads the answer has acquired a text. A student who argues with a primary source, gets confused, revises a hypothesis, and eventually produces a coherent account has undergone something categorically different: a structural change in how they think about historical causation. The former transaction leaves no lasting trace on the mind; the latter reshapes it.

What is now actively contested is whether AI, in its practical day-to-day use, tends to promote the deeper process or to short-circuit it. The emerging empirical picture is worrying. Research has found a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by increased cognitive offloading. A collaborative study by Carnegie Mellon University and Microsoft Research revealed that too much reliance on AI can hinder the brain's capacity for critical thinking, finding that it risks reducing a user's ability to problem-solve or have independent thought, particularly when AI-generated content is accepted without review. The International AI Safety Report for 2026 notes that one study found that three months after the introduction of AI support, clinicians' ability to detect tumours without AI assistance had dropped by 6% — a concrete instance of how outsourcing a cognitive task erodes the underlying skill.

The developmental stakes are even higher for young people. An adult choosing to offload a task they already understand is making a tradeoff between effort and efficiency — the capacity to do that task independently still exists, and the atrophy is probably recoverable. A child offloading a task they have never learned to perform is not making a choice; they are skipping a developmental step that was never developed, and the foreclosure may be permanent.

None of this means that AI is inherently hostile to learning. The relevant distinction, as one recent analysis puts it, is whether AI operates as a scaffold or a substitute — and scaffolding is characterized by temporariness and empowerment, with the goal of strengthening internal capacities so that the technology becomes progressively less necessary. If students overuse AI-provided answers, their ability to develop independent critical thinking may be impaired; AI implementation must therefore be made in a way that enhances higher-order thinking rather than simply automating lower-order tasks.

What AI's arrival clarifies, then, is something that was always true but easy to obscure: learning has never really been about acquiring answers. Learning is a process whereby knowledge is created and results in a change in mental representations or associations due to experience. The verb that matters is *change*. An AI system does not change when it returns a result; the user who reads that result and does nothing demanding with it does not change either. To learn something, in any sense worth the word, is to be a different thinker afterward — to carry a revised structure in one's mind that was not there before. No tool, however capable, can accomplish that on another person's behalf.

Does human knowledge lose or change its value when it is no longer scarce?

The question of whether knowledge loses its value when it ceases to be scarce turns out to be one of the most consequential questions of the present moment — and the answer depends heavily on which kind of "value" is under discussion.

The economic case seems clear enough at first glance. Abundance lowers costs, and when that happens, the elimination of scarcity changes the economics of how goods and services are produced and distributed. This has always been true of physical commodities, but knowledge has an unusual relationship with scarcity because it was never naturally scarce to begin with. Patent and copyright exist to supply artificial scarcity to what would otherwise be harmfully abundant knowledge — and paradoxically, this artificial scarcity is intended to motivate the production of more knowledge. In other words, much of what looks like knowledge's economic value has always been a legal construction, not an intrinsic property of the knowledge itself. Ideas are made scarce so that they can be brought into the economy and charged for; on the classical view of intellectual property law, a world in which all value resides in information is one that requires IP everywhere, or no one will be paid to create.

When abundance arrives anyway — through the internet, through open-access publishing, and now through large language models — the economic value of holding information does erode. But this is not the same as saying knowledge loses all value. What shifts is *where* value is created. In conditions of abundance, value migrates up the supply chain: it is created through the service of helping people find what they want within the sea of choice. Curation, judgment, and synthesis become the scarce goods. Scarcity of time on the part of the consumer becomes the ultimate limiting factor even when physical constraints are removed.

The deeper question, though, is epistemic rather than economic. Information abundance paradoxically degrades truth discernment, creating new opportunities for entrepreneurial foresight but also new forms of cognitive fatigue. A world drowning in content is not a world that knows more; it may, in important ways, know less reliably. Many prominent scholars have argued that increasing abundance of knowledge resources will lead to substantial and even disruptive transformation of existing political, economic, and social systems — but the direction of that transformation is contested, not settled.

This points to a distinction that philosophy has long maintained between knowledge and wisdom. As Heraclitus observed, abundance in knowledge cannot guarantee wisdom; being knowledgeable means having knowledge, but being wise means much more than that. Some of the most knowledgeable people are not wise: although they have an abundance of very important factual knowledge, they lack the practical know-how that marks a wise person — the capacity to navigate all kinds of situations and all kinds of people. Wisdom, in the formulation of psychologist Robert Sternberg, is "the value-laden application of tacit knowledge not only for one's own benefit but for the benefit of others, in order to attain a common good" — and what matters is not just knowledge, or the intellectual skills one applies to knowledge, but how that knowledge is used.

Abundance, then, devalues a particular kind of knowledge: propositional knowledge, the kind that can be stored, retrieved, and replicated. It does not devalue — and may actually increase the premium on — what philosophers call tacit knowledge: the embodied, experiential, judgment-laden understanding that resists easy transmission. AI technologies are trained on data from the human past, but democratic life often depends on the surfacing of human tacit knowledge and previously unrevealed preferences. Systems that reward speed over critical reflection risk encouraging learners to shift from inquiry-based learning toward the reproduction of polished, preformed answers — and unless explicitly addressed, this epistemic conditioning risks undermining the cultivation of ambiguity tolerance, intellectual humility, and reflective skepticism.

The honest verdict is this: when knowledge loses scarcity, its *commodity* value collapses, but its deeper value — as understanding, as judgment, as the hard-won capacity to act wisely under uncertainty — does not. What changes is the ease with which that deeper value can be counterfeited by the mere appearance of information. The scarcity that matters most in an age of abundance is not factual but attentional and epistemic: the scarce resource is the human capacity to distinguish knowledge from noise, and to use what one knows well.

What is the difference between knowledge, understanding, and wisdom — and which of these can AI possess or transmit?

The three concepts — knowledge, understanding, and wisdom — are often used as rough synonyms in ordinary speech, yet philosophy has long insisted they occupy distinct rungs on an epistemic ladder, and the ascent from one to the next is neither automatic nor guaranteed.

**Knowledge**, in the most venerable philosophical tradition, is justified true belief: one knows a proposition when it is true, when one believes it, and when one has adequate grounds for that belief. Knowledge primarily concerns the accumulation of facts, information, and understanding about specific subjects or phenomena. It is, in this sense, propositional and additive — a stockpile of verified claims about the world. A person can have unbounded knowledge and not have wisdom; the reverse, however, is not the case — no one can have wisdom without first having knowledge. Knowledge is thus necessary but not sufficient for the higher states.

**Understanding** is epistemologically more demanding and more contested. Recent epistemology has emphasized that understanding is not reducible to knowledge. Understanding involves grasping explanatory connections — seeing *why* things are as they are, not merely *that* they are. Where knowledge can consist of isolated, disconnected facts, understanding requires that one successfully grasp how one's beliefs in the relevant propositions cohere with other propositions one believes. The analytic tradition is genuinely divided here: some philosophers argue that understanding is simply a species of knowledge, while others oppose the reduction entirely, arguing that understanding demands more than knowledge. What is widely accepted across both camps is that understanding requires something like integration — the capacity to move between ideas, trace dependencies, and model how one thing would change if another changed.

**Wisdom** sits at the apex and is the most resistant to formal definition. It is not merely the possession of knowledge, but the judicious application of it, coupled with experience, intuition, and an ethical compass. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy identifies at least five major philosophical approaches, from wisdom as epistemic humility to wisdom as a kind of deep rationality; these include wisdom as epistemic accuracy, wisdom as knowledge, a hybrid theory, and wisdom as rationality. Aristotle's concept of *phronesis* — practical wisdom — remains the most influential formulation: it is the intellectual virtue concerned with action, with knowing how to act correctly in particular circumstances. Wisdom, then, is irreducibly action-oriented and evaluative. It is the ability to predict the consequences of one's choices and to make choices based on those predicted consequences — always in conditions of genuine moral and practical uncertainty.

---

The question of what AI can possess or transmit across these three tiers maps almost precisely onto the difficulty of each concept.

**At the level of knowledge**, AI systems perform strikingly well. Some AI systems hold superior epistemic positions in specific areas of application, producing outputs that consistently reduce false positives and false negatives beyond human capability. Machine learning models trained on vast datasets can detect patterns and correlations that no human could reasonably discern. The philosophical question of whether AI *possesses* this knowledge, or merely processes information that tracks truth reliably, remains genuinely open. Whether AI agents can be thought of as epistemic agents in the strict sense is still disputed; some scholars claim that AI systems do not possess justified beliefs and communicative intentions in the same way humans do. Whether or not one resolves that metaphysical question, AI can certainly *transmit* knowledge effectively to human users.

**At the level of understanding**, matters become thornier. A system might produce correct outputs without its processing exhibiting the structural features associated with understanding why those outputs are correct; it might generate coherent explanations without those explanations being grounded in actual causal mechanisms; and even if both outputs and explanations are correct, the system might fail to exhibit understanding if it cannot reliably distinguish contexts where its patterns apply from those where they fail. Whether large language models exhibit genuine understanding or a sophisticated simulation of it is one of the central unresolved debates in philosophy of mind and AI research.

**At the level of wisdom**, the current scholarly consensus is skeptical. Cognitive technologies such as generative AI have demonstrated impressive capabilities, yet their capacity to generate wisdom remains limited — wisdom emerges from embodied human experience, relational resonance, and ethical engagement. This is not merely a matter of computing power or training data. Wisdom, as Aristotle conceived it, is inseparable from having skin in the game — from facing genuine consequences, experiencing time's passage, and caring about outcomes in a way grounded in one's own life. An AI system has none of these in any straightforward sense.

Yet there is a subtler possibility: even if AI cannot *possess* wisdom, it may help *transmit* it, by surfacing the accumulated practical judgments of thinkers across centuries, modeling the structure of wise deliberation, and prompting human reflection. This transformation is not merely practical but philosophical: AI systems impact not only beliefs, but belief revision itself. That influence can run in either direction — toward deeper reflection or away from it. Whether AI becomes a catalyst for human wisdom or a substitute for the effortful work of acquiring it may be one of the defining cultural questions of the coming decades.

How does the human relationship with truth change when answers are generated rather than discovered?

## Generated Truth: A New Epistemic Condition

For most of human history, truth was something one went to find — through observation, argument, experiment, or testimony from a witness who had themselves gone looking. The answer, when it arrived, carried a traceable lineage: here is the evidence, here is the method of reasoning, here is the person who stands behind the claim. What generative AI introduces is something categorically different: an answer that is assembled rather than retrieved, produced rather than possessed.

These systems operate not through deliberation, interpretation, or hermeneutic engagement but through probabilistic reasoning, pattern recognition, and anomaly detection — what might be called computational inference rather than human judgment. The architecture underlying large language models makes this especially stark. Popular LLMs such as ChatGPT and Gemini are text-generation engines designed to predict which string of words comes next in a piece of text, fine-tuned via reinforcement learning from human feedback to make their outputs more human-like, persuasive, and useful. The output is optimized for plausibility, not for accuracy in the classical sense — a distinction that cuts to the heart of the epistemic problem.

This creates a peculiar vulnerability in the human receiver. AI's fluency and confident tone align with cognitive preferences for easily processed content, and existing work shows that users form trust in AI based on fluency, tone, and perceived authority, often overlooking accuracy when corrections are absent. In other words, the psychological conditions for accepting a generated answer as true are largely decoupled from the conditions that would traditionally warrant that acceptance. Our vulnerability to treating LLMs as human-like truth tellers is uniquely worrying.

The consequences are not merely individual. Algorithmic truth is neither neutral nor universal; it is embedded with normative assumptions, data-driven biases, and institutional logic that carry profound implications for epistemic authority, public trust, and democratic discourse. When a system functions as an arbiter of credibility at scale, the errors it makes propagate differently than the errors of any individual expert. Recent 2025–2026 studies confirm that even advanced models like GPT-4o and Claude 3.7 still exhibit 15–20% hallucination rates on factual citation tasks, rising sharply on niche or recent topics — and in medical and legal domains, rates can exceed 28% without grounding.

The phenomenon of hallucination — in which a model produces information that seems authentic yet is ultimately fabricated — is not simply a technical bug awaiting a patch. As long as the core next-token prediction mechanism remains, hallucinations persist as an ongoing technical challenge and an epistemic risk. Philosophers and computer scientists have begun to argue that the goal must therefore shift: systems must be oriented towards epistemic integrity — a commitment to generating outputs that are not only plausible, but also verifiable, context-aware, justified, and appropriately uncertain — including modelling and communicating internal confidence levels, and recognising when to defer or abstain.

Yet there is a genuine and contested question about whether generated answers might, under the right conditions, actually extend human knowledge rather than merely simulate it. In several recent cases, AI systems have genuinely contributed to human knowledge, leveraging fast processing of big data and neural network pattern detection — including in biomedical research, especially drug discovery, and in astronomy and cosmology. By analyzing extensive scientific literature, LLMs can uncover gaps in existing knowledge and propose novel hypotheses that may not be immediately apparent to human researchers — and have been applied to propose drug combinations for breast cancer treatment, some of which were later validated in laboratory experiments.

This is the sharpest tension in the current moment. The same generative process that fabricates a nonexistent legal citation can, it seems, sketch a genuinely productive hypothesis. This signals a basic epistemological shift: from the collaborative construction of knowledge to a framework that privileges immediate, polished output. What changes, fundamentally, is not just where answers come from — but who bears the burden of verification, and whether that burden, quietly and incrementally, is being laid down.

What is irreducibly human in the act of teaching, and what is irreducibly human in the act of learning?

**What Is Irreducibly Human in Teaching and Learning**

The question presses hardest precisely when it seems most answerable by technology. An AI tutor can be patient without limit, responsive without fatigue, available at midnight, and calibrated to the exact pace of any learner. And yet something resists substitution — not as a sentimental preference for the old ways, but as a structural feature of what teaching and learning actually are.

Begin with the teacher. The most obvious candidates for the irreducibly human — warmth, encouragement, the ability to notice a student's confusion — are real but insufficient. What is deeper is the teacher's own history as a learner. When a learner works with an expert teacher, they are not simply gaining access to the teacher's knowledge, but also benefiting from the teacher's memories of learning it themselves. This is not a trivial advantage. A teacher who has genuinely struggled with a concept carries in their body and speech a kind of phenomenological map of the confusion — where it hurts, where the wrong turn is attractive, what it feels like to suddenly see through it. No AI technology learns something the way a human learns it, and then helps another human learn accordingly. The machine's "knowledge" is statistically derived, not experientially won, and this distinction leaves a mark on every explanation it can give.

The teacher also brings something harder to name: the capacity for genuine moral witness. If education is ultimately about cultivating judgment, empathy, curiosity, and the ability to engage thoughtfully with others, then meaningful human encounters cannot be an occasional feature of learning. The teacher who perceives a student's shame, or excitement, or dawning self-doubt is not performing pattern recognition on behavioral data — a human is uniquely placed to sense what another human is cognitively experiencing at any moment, and respond accordingly. That responsiveness is partly emotional and partly ethical: it involves caring about this particular person's formation, not just their performance on a task. Teachers believe that the complex nature of teaching requires human intervention, that AI alone cannot adequately address the multifaceted demands of education, including social and emotional development, personalized support, and adaptability to individual student needs.

There is also the matter of intellectual authority — not the authority of hierarchy, but the authority of genuine commitment. A teacher vouches for what they teach. They stake something of themselves on the claim that this material matters, that it is worth the student's time and suffering. A system that generates plausible explanations cannot vouch for anything. Close reading, cultural nuance, and the encounter with soulless machine translations all point to the same belief: that yearning is a human act, and that learning involves recognizing and reclaiming what is irreducibly human.

Now consider the learner. Here the irreducibly human is less about relationship and more about stakes. To learn genuinely — not to acquire information but to be changed by it — is to submit to a process that disrupts prior certainty. Education does not begin when information is presented; it begins when assumptions are challenged by new people and experiences. This kind of challenge is not merely cognitive. Cognition is shaped not merely by the brain but also by the interactions between the body and the environment, and conceptual knowledge can be acquired and understood through bodily experiences, with cognition grounded in sensorimotor activities. The learner who grasps a concept is not filing a new proposition into memory; the understanding often inhabits posture, gesture, and sensation.

More fundamentally, the learner brings vulnerability — the willingness to not yet know. This is existentially costly in a way machines cannot replicate or appreciate. Social-emotional competencies developed through human interactions are something generative AI technologies cannot currently replicate. The courage to be confused in front of others, to revise one's self-understanding, to remain uncertain while pushing forward — these are not skills in the ordinary sense. They are modes of being that emerge through the encounter with other people who have themselves survived not knowing.

What remains irreducibly human in teaching, then, is the transmission of hard-won experience, the act of moral witness, and the vouching of one mind for the value of what it knows. What remains irreducibly human in learning is the vulnerability of genuine transformation — the willingness to be remade by contact with difficulty, other minds, and the world as it resists our expectations. As AI becomes increasingly capable of providing information, the purpose of education might become even clearer: information is abundant. What is scarce is the human willingness to be changed by it.

Should education aim to make humans complementary to AI, resistant to it, or independent of it?

**The Three Orientations and Their Stakes**

The question of what education should make of AI — a partner, an adversary, or an irrelevance — is not merely pedagogical. It is a question about what kind of minds a society wishes to cultivate, and what version of human agency it is prepared to defend.

The dominant institutional answer, at present, is complementarity. Domain accomplishment is said to hinge on complementary human-AI roles, wherein AI provides scalability and humans maintain contextual rigor. This framing has been embraced by bodies ranging from individual universities to international organizations. UNESCO released AI competency frameworks for students and teachers in 2024, built on the idea that AI should support, rather than replace, human decision-making and intellectual development. The appeal is obvious: rather than positioning technology as a threat to be resisted or a crutch to be avoided, the complementarity model imagines a productive division of cognitive labor. AI offers consistency, vast knowledge, and real-time adaptivity, while human teachers provide the irreplaceable elements of inspiration, ethical grounding, and emotional connection — together forming, in this account, a powerful alliance.

The labor market lends this view empirical weight. The World Economic Forum projects that AI will displace 92 million jobs while creating 170 million new ones — roles that are not simply "AI jobs" but existing roles transformed by AI integration — and the IMF emphasizes that students need "cognitive, creative, and technical skills that complement AI and help them use it rather than compete with it."

Yet the complementarity consensus conceals a serious empirical worry. AI is creating an increasing dependence that reduces critical thinking skills and human agency; people are relinquishing "old" skills — independent decision-making, critical thinking, face-to-face social skills — before acquiring the new ones necessary for using AI, and a vicious cycle of dependence and loss of confidence in one's own judgment has begun. The risk is particularly acute for the young. The downside of adolescents growing up delegating to AI is a generation that was never sharp to begin with, making the protection of the space children need to develop foundational thinking skills a non-negotiable. Research on younger learners bears this out: for primary school children, reliance on AI may interfere with the "automation" of foundational skills such as syntax or calculation, whereas for secondary adolescents the risk shifts toward the erosion of self-efficacy and academic identity.

This is where the case for resistance — or at least for AI-free cognitive development — gains its most serious purchase. Resistance here need not mean Luddism; it means protecting developmental phases during which unassisted thinking must be practiced if it is to consolidate. Educators and philosophers argue that we need to put effort into learning the profound limitations of AI, take into account its broader impacts on human rights and social justice, and — perhaps most importantly — learn how to think critically. The French philosopher Éric Sadin, speaking at UNESCO in late 2025, went further, warning against the consequences of the "civilizational rupture" represented by generative AI — a concern that UNESCO echoed in its insistence that education remain human-centred.

Independence — the third orientation — is often conflated with resistance, but it is subtly different. Where resistance is reactive, independence is constitutive: it asks that certain capacities be cultivated in students without reference to AI at all, so that those students can then engage AI from a position of genuine agency rather than habituated reliance. Human agency can be safeguarded by designing AI systems to complement rather than replace human capabilities, and emerging perspectives frame human-AI interaction as a partnership in which each side's strengths mitigate the other's limitations — but this only works if the human side of the partnership has something genuine to contribute. A more balanced educational approach is necessary to break out of the cycle of dependence and fill the void with renewed skills to navigate the new technological reality.

What emerges from the current evidence is not a clean answer but a developmental sequence. The three orientations are not mutually exclusive; they are appropriate at different stages and in different domains. Foundational education — early literacy, numeracy, the practice of sustained argument — demands a form of independence that AI presence can undermine. Later, in professional and higher education, a well-grounded complementarity becomes not only defensible but necessary. And throughout, some capacity for resistance — the ability to interrogate, distrust, and override AI outputs — must be deliberately cultivated, because threats such as hallucination-induced errors, academic fraud, and a decline in critical thinking remain serious in the absence of metacognitive scaffolding and ethical regulation. The honest answer is that education must pursue all three orientations in sequence: independence first, so that complementarity is real rather than illusory, and resistance always, so that the partnership never becomes surrender.

How does AI reshape our concept of intelligence itself — and the way schools measure and reward it?

**Intelligence Reconsidered: What AI Changes About the Mind and the Classroom**

For most of the twentieth century, the dominant model of intelligence was essentially computational: the smartest person in the room was the one who could store the most information, retrieve it fastest, and process it with fewest errors. Schools were built in that image — examinations rewarded recall, speed, and the disciplined application of known procedures. Then came machines that can do all of those things vastly better than any human being, at any scale, at negligible cost. The encounter is not merely a technological disruption; it is a conceptual one. It forces a reckoning with what intelligence actually is, and what schooling is therefore for.

The reckoning is already underway in research and policy. Where the steam engine redefined labour and the computer redefined calculation, AI is redefining thought itself — and unlike earlier technologies that multiplied individual workers' power, large language models multiply what might be called the collective intelligence of humanity. This shift puts pressure on a long-standing assumption: that the value of a trained mind lies chiefly in what it holds and can produce in isolation. A 2025 framework proposed by Moldoveanu and Siemens argues for a core shift — that skills are now exercised interactionally and dialogically before they are individually mastered, and that cultivating what they call "interactional intelligence" means integrating meta-cognitive capacities such as understanding how we think *with* AI, evaluating model appropriateness, and designing task architectures that distribute work between human and machine.

That redistribution raises an uncomfortable empirical question: does offloading cognitive work to machines degrade the human capacity for independent thought? The evidence is genuinely mixed and should not be simplified. A study by Gerlich (2025) found a negative correlation between frequent AI usage and critical-thinking abilities, with one contributing factor being cognitive offloading — AI users engaging less in deep, reflective thinking and preferring quick AI-generated solutions. An MIT Media Lab study divided subjects into three groups writing essays using ChatGPT, a search engine, or nothing at all; EEG recordings found that generative AI users had the lowest brain engagement and consistently underperformed at neural, linguistic, and behavioural levels. Yet the picture is not uniformly bleak. Cognitive Load Theory suggests that AI has the potential to eliminate redundant work and allow students to focus on more important cognitive operations — but that over-reliance risks reducing the kind of effortful engagement needed for deep learning and higher-order thinking, and that adaptive AI must promote effortful engagement rather than passive reception.

Unlike earlier tools such as calculators and spreadsheets, which made specific tasks easier without fundamentally altering the ability to think, AI is reshaping the way people process information and make decisions, often diminishing reliance on their own cognitive abilities. Whether that diminishment becomes permanent depends significantly on how schools respond — and here the institutional picture is one of belated, uneven reform.

Australia's national higher-education regulator, TEQSA, released a landmark guidance document in September 2025 acknowledging that AI detection tools cannot guarantee integrity and that structural redesign of assessment is the only sustainable response. Schools, vocational providers, and universities alike are grappling with the same question: how to design assessments that are credible, authentic, and future-facing in the age of AI. The answer emerging from multiple quarters is to shift emphasis from what students can produce toward what they can judge, question, and direct. The World Economic Forum's *Future of Jobs Report 2025* projects that nearly 40% of the skills required by the global workforce will change within five years, and that preparing students means going beyond traditional curricula. The skills that curriculum designers now emphasise include algorithmic thinking and prompt engineering alongside the human capacities that AI cannot replicate: empathy, judgment, ethical reasoning, and collaboration.

Prompt engineering — the ability to craft precise, thoughtful inputs for AI tools to produce effective outputs — is quickly becoming an essential skill in modern education; more than a technical trick, it represents a pedagogical shift. There is a genuine irony here: to use a thinking machine well, one must think carefully first, which means the best use of AI may paradoxically demand more rigorous intellectual formation, not less. Policymakers, accordingly, are being urged to expand access to education and training that emphasises complex decision-making skills, ensuring that more people develop the judgment needed to complement AI rather than simply defer to it.

What remains genuinely contested is whether schools are moving fast enough, or in the right direction. The World Economic Forum identifies creative thinking, flexibility, resilience, curiosity, and lifelong learning as competencies expected to rise sharply through 2030 — skills that K–12 schools must intentionally foster. Whether the grading rubric, the standardised test, and the timed examination — all instruments calibrated to a pre-AI conception of mind — can capture those qualities is a question that educational systems have barely begun to answer.

What happens to intellectual struggle, effort, and "productive failure" when friction can always be removed?

**The Friction That Teaches**

There is a well-established paradox at the heart of learning science: conditions that feel difficult in the moment tend to produce the most durable understanding, while conditions that feel easy tend to produce the least. The term "desirable difficulties," coined by cognitive psychologist Robert Bjork, refers to strategies that introduce difficulties, engage learners actively, and may appear to impede performance but which, in reality, create more durable learning than the conditions that, superficially, make learning feel easy. The canonical examples — spacing study sessions, interleaving topics, testing oneself before one feels ready — share a common feature: they make the learner work. Research suggests that while difficult tasks might slow down learning initially, the long-term benefits are greater than with easy tasks. This is not a minor footnote in educational psychology. It is among the field's most robust findings, replicated across age groups, subjects, and cultures.

The related concept of "productive failure," developed by Manu Kapur at ETH Zurich, sharpens the argument further. Productive failure is a learning design that entails creating conditions for learners to persist in generating and exploring representations and solution methods for solving complex, novel problems. The premise is that failing to solve a problem before being taught the canonical solution is not waste — it is preparation. Initial struggle and failure can activate prior knowledge and create fertile ground for subsequent learning, leading to deeper understanding and better retention of concepts. In a University of British Columbia study of first-year biology students, students first struggled to solve complex problems, which primed them to learn the expert solution via a series of interactive activities — and this approach led to significant benefits compared with more conventional instruction.

What happens, then, when a tool exists that can dissolve this friction on demand? The evidence is increasingly worrying. In a quasi-experimental study of 151 first-year computer science students, substantial short-term gains were found in AI-assisted programming tasks, yet weak transfer to independent problem-solving contexts — suggesting that performance improvements do not necessarily translate into long-term learning. In writing contexts, students who relied extensively on AI-generated text exhibited a superficial understanding of their own submissions, often struggling to explain arguments, justify structural decisions, or recall specific claims within their essays. The irony is precise: the tool produces a better-looking output while the mind behind it grows less capable.

Part of the mechanism is what researchers call the illusion of fluency. Cognitive struggle — mental effort that may feel uncomfortable — is essential for deep understanding, long-term retention, and transferable knowledge. Students often misinterpret effort as a sign of poor learning, a bias known as the illusion of fluency, which drives preference for low-effort, superficially satisfying activities. AI tools, by their design, reward exactly this preference. The speed, fluency, and authoritative tone of AI can normalize shortcut-seeking and reduce tolerance for cognitive effort, shifting learners from effortful sense-making toward passive consumption unless pedagogy enforces critique, justification, and desirable difficulties.

The broader concern is not merely academic performance but what one recent paper calls cognitive sovereignty. The durable, transferable competence a student earns through struggle is the individual ground of autonomous thought in an age of AI systems built to supply it on demand — and outsourced at the moment of learning, that ground erodes one effortless task at a time. There is also striking experimental evidence on the magnitude of the effect: an unguarded AI helper left high-school students roughly 17 percent worse on an unaided exam than peers with no tool at all, while the same model rebuilt to withhold answers erased the harm, and a well-engineered tutor roughly doubled learning.

The conclusion the research forces is not that AI assistance is inherently corrosive — it is that design determines everything. Pedagogically aligned AI — designed to manage cognitive load without eliminating struggle, to prompt reflection rather than deliver answers — can strengthen intellectual resilience rather than weaken it. The challenge for educators and designers is to resist the temptation of frictionless automation and instead design for productive struggle, agency, and reflection. Friction, in other words, is not a bug to be optimized away. For minds in the process of becoming, it is the substrate of growth itself.

Is there a moral difference between learning with AI, learning from AI, and outsourcing learning to AI?

The three modes of engaging with AI in learning — learning *with* it, learning *from* it, and outsourcing learning *to* it — are not merely stylistic variations on the same activity. They represent genuinely distinct moral situations, distinguished by what the learner retains, surrenders, and becomes.

The most useful philosophical entry point is the concept of cognitive offloading, which has been in human culture since the first writing tablet. Cognitive offloading — using a physical or digital system to reduce the mental demands of information processing — has expanded so dramatically with AI that it may blur into cognitive *outsourcing*, which previously required trusting other people for judgments about the news, history, or what one should do. That blurring is precisely where the moral stakes become serious.

**Learning with AI** — using it as an interlocutor, a sparring partner, or a Socratic challenger — most closely resembles the use of any good tool: it extends capacity without displacing agency. The learner still frames the problem, interrogates the output, and owns the resulting understanding. The educational value of generative AI depends on how students engage with its outputs, with practices such as negotiated and oppositional reading being essential for maintaining critical engagement. Here the moral picture is relatively benign, subject to the usual caveats about source reliability and epistemic humility.

**Learning from AI** is more ambiguous. When a student turns to an AI tutor for explanation and then verifies, questions, and builds on what it provides, the relationship resembles learning from a textbook or a lecturer — with the significant difference that the AI adapts to the learner in real time. The growing use of AI in personalized learning environments has raised concerns over the erosion of student autonomy; while adaptive algorithms aim to optimize learning efficiency, they may also over-direct students, limiting opportunities for independent exploration and critical thinking. The moral concern here is not dishonesty but a subtler problem: current integrations privilege efficiency over learners' epistemic agency, normalizing quick answer-taking rather than verification and productive struggle; in such "oracle-style" pipelines, learning devolves into a passive, one-way flow of information from AI to humans.

**Outsourcing learning to AI** is a categorically different matter. Recent scholarship has coined a useful distinction between cognitive offloading and what some researchers call *cognitive surrender*: whereas cognitive offloading involves the strategic outsourcing of a narrow, well-defined task while the reasoner retains the executive cognitive process, generative AI enables a qualitatively different pattern — "cognitive surrender" — in which the user adopts an AI-generated output as one's own answer with minimal scrutiny, relinquishing cognitive control rather than delegating a discrete subtask. The moral weight of this distinction lies in what the learner does not build. Studies have consistently revealed a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, with cognitive offloading acting as a mediating factor. More troublingly, cognitive offloading, illusion of competence, authorship ambiguity, and normalized deference to machine outputs are argued to foster moral abdication and erosion of ethical self-regulation, particularly in education and knowledge work.

The moral question turns on whether only the final outcome of an action matters, or whether the way in which one reaches that outcome also matters — which sorts of thinking are only about the destination, and which are also about the journey? This is precisely the question that separates the three modes. A student who outsources an essay to AI may produce a passable text, but has foregone the friction through which understanding is actually formed. Introduced too early, before students have acquired the necessary conceptual foundations, AI becomes counterproductive; just as giving a calculator to a child who cannot yet multiply undermines mental arithmetic, using AI as a shortcut in early learning ultimately holds students back.

What remains genuinely contested is whether this matters only instrumentally — as a failure of self-interest, since one ends up less competent — or whether it also involves a form of self-betrayal with broader ethical implications. The kind of embodied, experiential know-how typified in craftsmanship is deeply formative of thought and character; just as the assembly line dissected craftsmanship into isolated, repeatable activities, algorithmic reasoning can decompose cognitive expertise into separate skills to be automated or outsourced. On this view, outsourcing learning is not merely unwise; it shapes what kind of thinker — and ultimately what kind of person — one becomes. The evidence for catastrophic cognitive harm from AI use remains, as of current research, more theoretical than empirically settled. The quantitative evidence for enhancement is currently far more substantial and systematically documented than the evidence for cognitive dependence, which often remains an identified risk or a theoretical concern. But the moral case for preserving the journey of learning does not depend on empirical proof of harm — it rests on the older claim that the struggle to understand is itself constitutive of intellectual character, not merely a means to it.

Who is responsible when an AI teaches something false, biased, or harmful to a student?

## Who Is Responsible When an AI Teaches Something False, Biased, or Harmful?

The question of accountability when an AI system misleads a student is deceptively simple on the surface and deeply tangled in practice. It implicates at least four distinct parties — technology developers, schools and universities, classroom teachers, and students themselves — and the law, as yet, offers no clean resolution.

### The Nature of the Harm

The harms in question are not hypothetical. AI models frequently "hallucinate," producing false information that students may accept as truth due to the authoritative way AI presents responses. Despite improvements in content filtering, bias remains a concern, with studies showing that chatbots can reinforce stereotypes and mislead young users into believing AI-generated content is factually accurate. As generative AI expands in education, multiple cases have been documented where algorithms have reproduced and amplified pre-existing inequalities — biases that can affect academic assessments, course recommendations, and even the allocation of learning opportunities. For younger students, the risks compound: children tend to anthropomorphize chatbots, seeking emotional support from AI systems that lack true understanding, which can result in distress if responses are inappropriate or unhelpful.

### The Legal Vacuum

Existing law was not built for this situation. Legal and ethical frameworks assume a human-in-the-loop model, where accountability rests with the user, because AI tools are not recognized as legal persons or responsible agents. That assumption breaks down in educational settings, where a child interacting with a tutoring chatbot has no meaningful ability to audit its outputs. Traditional product liability theories, such as design defect and failure to warn, are being tested and redefined in the AI context, but because AI systems' decision-making can be opaque even to their creators, it is inherently difficult to assess liability, assign responsibility, and anticipate the full range of potential harms.

Early court cases have set limited and cautious precedents. In a 2025 ruling against a plaintiff who sued OpenAI for defamation, the court recognized OpenAI's efforts to reduce inaccuracies and provide user warnings, concluding that the legal threshold for defamation was not met — a ruling significant for suggesting that disclaimers and responsible AI design may protect developers from liability, even while the broader question of AI accountability remains unsettled.

### Where Responsibility Currently Falls

In practical terms, responsibility is being distributed — unevenly — across three layers. At the developer level, researchers and ethicists increasingly argue that bias mitigation must be addressed across the AI lifecycle, from data collection and training to inference and system updates, and that developers should incorporate human rights impact assessments to identify risks before deployment. Ultimately, developers must be held accountable for ensuring that AI education technologies foster ethical, inclusive, and pedagogically meaningful innovations.

At the institutional level, schools are being pushed to take responsibility for what they deploy. Institutional accountability requires universities to establish clear standards and monitoring mechanisms to ensure the ethical and responsible use of these tools. Schools need clear agreements outlining liability with AI vendors, and regular monitoring of AI's impact is needed to ensure it supports rather than disrupts learning goals. Legislatively, the pace of regulation is accelerating: during the 2026 legislative session alone, FutureEd is tracking 71 bills across 27 states that address artificial intelligence in classroom instruction.

Teachers occupy a third position, one that the law has barely begun to define. Concerns are looming over transparency and accountability as AI becomes increasingly entrenched in decision-making processes, and the educator who recommends or simply permits the use of a particular AI tool may carry an implicit duty of care that has no established legal precedent.

### The Unresolved Core

What remains genuinely open is whether any single party can or should bear primary responsibility. The lack of transparency and accountability in algorithmic processes fundamentally complicates ethical oversight. Exploiting personal data due to a lack of awareness can go unnoticed without accountability, and problems persist with the opacity of AI systems, including so-called black-box algorithms. The practical consequence is a diffusion of responsibility that benefits no one — least of all the student who simply trusted the machine. The honest answer, for now, is that accountability is shared in principle and enforced by almost no one in particular, a condition that is more a reflection of how quickly the technology arrived than of any considered social judgment about where the duty of care should lie.

How should student data collected by AI systems be governed, and who owns the learning profile of a child?

**The Child as Data Subject: Governing Student Information in the Age of AI**

The question of who owns a child's learning profile has no clean answer in current law, and the gap between what AI systems can do and what governance frameworks permit them to do is widening by the year. The stakes are considerable: the use of AI in education involves the collection and analysis of sensitive student data, including personal information, academic performance, and behavioral patterns. Beyond academic records, AI-powered tools collect vast amounts of sensitive information, including student health records, Social Security numbers, and families' credit card data. When aggregated and processed over years of schooling, this material constitutes something qualitatively new: a continuously updated psychological and cognitive portrait of a developing person.

**The Legal Landscape and Its Gaps**

The primary American statute governing this territory is the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act (FERPA). FERPA governs student data privacy, ensuring that educational institutions maintain strict confidentiality around student records — but it was enacted long before AI became prevalent, leaving gaps in how it applies to modern data practices. The law was designed for a world of paper files and administrative records, not for adaptive learning platforms that track every hesitation, wrong answer, and rereading of a passage. Guidelines like FERPA don't guarantee meaningful protections for students when AI vendors can structure their data practices to fall outside its definitions of "education records."

For younger children, the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) provides a separate layer of protection. The FTC's COPPA amendments, finalized in 2025, require separate parental consent for sharing children's data with third parties, though schools can still authorize educational data collection under existing FTC guidance. The question of who actually holds that consent authority — parent, school, or district — remains practically ambiguous.

**The Ownership Problem**

The question of legal ownership is particularly unsettled. FERPA and similar laws dictate how schools manage student data, yet many AI tools operate in a legal gray area. To ensure compliance, educators and administrators must critically evaluate how AI systems collect, store, and process student information — including who controls the data and whether the school retains ownership or the AI vendor does. In practice, contractual arrangements between districts and edtech companies often determine the answer, and those contracts vary enormously. The law ensures that student data remains under the control of the local educational agency, and the AI provider must agree not to use the data for any purposes other than those specified in the agreement — but enforcement of such provisions is inconsistent and under-resourced.

The training-data question introduces a further complication. AI firms are constantly seeking data to train their models, and while they are not required to say exactly where they get it, they do have to say how they're using customer data, especially when dealing with students. Most major educational AI platforms publicly disclaim training on student inputs — content submitted by teachers and students is not used to train the foundational AI models that underlie Khan Academy's AI tutor, Khanmigo, according to the company's chief learning officer — but there is no independent verification regime to hold vendors to such commitments.

**The Risks of Permanent Profiling**

Perhaps the most consequential and least-discussed risk is what might be called the permanence problem. AI privacy risks in education include the creation of permanent digital records that follow students throughout their lives. A learning profile assembled at age eight — one that might label a child as struggling with reading comprehension or prone to distraction — could inform algorithmic decisions about academic placement, college admissions, or even employment screenings in ways the child never consented to and cannot later contest. A 2024 study found that predictive algorithms widely used by universities tend to underestimate the success potential of Black and Hispanic students while overestimating that of White and Asian students — and this systematic bias skews academic support interventions, creating a risk of self-fulfilling prophecies where students flagged as "high-risk" receive fewer opportunities.

**The Regulatory Response**

Governance is moving, but unevenly. More than half of US states have issued AI guidance for K-12 schools, most of it converging on the same themes: data privacy, academic integrity, equity, and keeping educators in control of AI-assisted decisions. Yet guidance is not law. As of July 2025, only a couple of states had moved beyond recommendations to passing legal mandates: Ohio passed a law requiring all K-12 public schools to adopt AI use policies by mid-2026, and Tennessee mandated that local school boards implement policies regarding the use of AI by students, faculty, and staff. At the federal level, legislation has been proposed that would amend FERPA and create a Golden Seal of Excellence in Student Data Privacy, along with a Privacy Technical Assistance Center at the U.S. Department of Education, though no such comprehensive framework has yet been enacted.

Internationally, the EU AI Act offers a more structured approach. It classifies several education uses of AI as high-risk, including systems that determine admission or assignment to institutions, evaluate learning outcomes, or monitor behavior during tests — and high-risk status brings obligations on risk management, data quality, logging, transparency, and human oversight.

**What Sound Governance Requires**

The contours of a defensible governance framework are becoming clearer even if implementation lags. Transparency in data usage is essential — stakeholders should be informed about what data is being collected, how it will be utilized, and who will have access to it — and institutions should establish clear data governance policies that delineate responsibilities for data handling and outline procedures for responding to potential data breaches. Beyond transparency, the field increasingly recognizes that ownership should vest ultimately in the student — with parents acting as stewards during minority — rather than in vendors or even institutions. A comprehensive, nationwide approach to data privacy in education is needed; until such regulations are in place, the responsibility lies with educators and schools to harness the benefits of AI responsibly, ensuring the protection of student data and upholding the integrity of the learning environment. Whether governments move fast enough to establish that framework before AI-generated learning profiles become deeply embedded in consequential decisions remains, as of mid-2026, an open and urgent question.

Does personalized AI tutoring risk creating algorithmic determinism — locking students into predicted trajectories?

**Algorithmic Determinism and the Personalized Learning Path**

The promise of AI tutoring rests on a seductive logic: that a system continuously observing a student's choices, errors, and pace can construct a learning environment precisely calibrated to that student's needs. AI systems create custom learning paths based on individual data, and as students work with the platform, the system builds an understanding of their strengths, weaknesses, and learning preferences, then adjusts content difficulty and moves more quickly in areas of confidence. In practice, however, this continuous calibration raises a question that the field has only begun to take seriously: does a system that optimizes for where a student *is* inadvertently foreclose where a student might *go*?

The concern is structural. Machine learning algorithms analyze student performance data to customize content difficulty, pacing, and presentation methods according to individual needs. But the model's prediction of what a student needs next is built entirely from what that student has done before. If a learner struggles early with abstract reasoning, the algorithm may route them permanently toward more concrete scaffolding, never presenting the harder material that might have catalyzed a breakthrough. The system mistakes a current state for a fixed trait.

This is not merely a theoretical worry. AI systems trained on biased datasets can inadvertently perpetuate or exacerbate inequalities; some studies demonstrate that AI algorithms may disadvantage students from underrepresented groups by providing less accurate or biased feedback, thus impacting their learning outcomes. The mechanism here is a feedback loop: a student from a historically underserved background arrives at the system with performance data that reflects accumulated disadvantage rather than innate capacity. The AI Index Report (2025) concludes that AI systems trained on unbalanced or incomplete datasets "magnify pre-existing inequalities and reinforce historical patterns of exclusion," and warns that large-scale AI adoption without robust data governance risks institutionalizing bias within access to education.

What makes this especially difficult to remedy is the opacity of the systems themselves. Unexplained path adjustments — observed in 15% of cases in one medical education study — may undermine trust, and researchers have called for the integration of explainability tools to address this. A student, or a teacher, cannot easily contest a trajectory they cannot see. Without effective safeguards, the same technologies that promise to personalize and expand access may also reinforce bias or depersonalize the learning experience; five recurrent themes in the research are data privacy, algorithmic bias, depersonalization of learning, accountability, and transparency.

There is a countervailing body of evidence, however, that complicates an outright verdict of determinism. Research examining AI-generated feedback found that AI-assisted learners exhibited greater autonomy in problem-solving tasks, though the same study cautioned that excessive AI control can reduce student agency, emphasizing the need for a balanced approach where students can choose how and when to interact with AI support. The variable appears to be design: systems that invite student choice produce different outcomes than those that route silently. When implemented responsibly, generative AI can foster student ownership of learning and encourage critical thinking.

Students may struggle to develop self-regulation — planning, monitoring, and adjusting one's own learning — if AI tools provide constant direction without requiring independent goal setting or reflection. This is perhaps the deepest version of the determinism problem: not that the algorithm locks a student into a particular content track, but that it quietly displaces the metacognitive work of deciding how to learn. A student who never has to choose a path never learns to navigate.

The honest conclusion is that algorithmic determinism in AI tutoring is a genuine risk rather than an inevitability. Whether it materializes depends on how transparent the system is, whether human educators retain the authority to override its recommendations, and whether the data on which the model is trained reflects genuine diversity of potential rather than historical patterns of disadvantage. The educational impact of AI is shaped by three intersecting dimensions — personalization, fairness, and access — whose intersections determine whether AI narrows or widens learning inequalities. The technology itself is neutral on this question; the governance around it is not.

How do we prevent AI from amplifying existing inequalities of class, language, geography, and disability in education?

**Preventing AI from Amplifying Educational Inequality**

The optimistic case for AI in education rests on a genuine insight: for the first time in history, something approximating a personal tutor — patient, adaptive, available at midnight — could be within reach of any child with a device and a connection. One of the largest barriers to academic success for low-income students has always been access to high-quality one-on-one tutoring, which most families simply cannot afford; generative AI has the potential to bridge this gap at a fraction of the cost. The problem is that every democratizing technology follows the same bitter pattern. It benefits the already-advantaged first, and risks leaving the gap wider than it found it. Avoiding that outcome requires confronting four distinct, compounding fault lines: class, language, geography, and disability.

**Class and the Infrastructure Floor**

Access is not merely a matter of software licenses. Nine million American students still lack reliable home broadband, and the problem is far graver globally. Physical deficits — unreliable electricity, inadequate facilities — severely limit the feasibility of digital tools, and without investment in that foundational infrastructure, AI adoption risks remaining a distant aspiration rather than a practical solution. Governments are beginning to take note. The UK has announced plans to make AI-powered one-to-one tutoring available to up to 450,000 children from disadvantaged backgrounds, offering personalized learning support to those who cannot afford private tutors. Whether such programs narrow the gap or simply layer a new digital hierarchy on top of existing ones depends entirely on whether the tools reach students with the weakest, not the strongest, prior preparation.

**Language and the Dominance of English**

The linguistic geography of AI is radically uneven. Only about seven percent of the world's roughly 7,000 languages are reflected in published online material, and UNESCO estimates that only around 400 languages are fully accessible online — which means the training data underlying most large language models is a thin slice of human expression. English remains the dominant language in AI development and application, and its widespread use can marginalize non-English languages, limiting access to educational content for indigenous language speakers. The injury is not only one of access. AI output often demonstrates a bias toward a "WEIRD" perspective — Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic — leaving out the perspectives of minority groups and underprivileged communities, effectively reproducing and reinforcing existing social hierarchies.

This bias has a particularly sharp edge in academic integrity enforcement. Research has found that more than 19% of texts produced by non-native English speakers were unanimously misclassified as AI-generated by seven different detectors, and 97% of those texts were flagged as suspicious by at least one detector — because non-native speakers tend to adhere more closely to standard syntax, producing lower perplexity scores that trigger higher rates of false positives. This results in a significantly higher false positive rate for this population, reinforcing structural inequities in education.

**Geography and the Concentration of AI Development**

According to the 2025 Stanford AI Index, global AI activity — from model production to investment — is heavily concentrated in a few regions, primarily the United States, China, and parts of Western Europe. Many non-English-speaking nations lack the technological and economic resources to develop generative AI tailored to their linguistic and cultural needs. A UNESCO analysis has warned that fully digitalizing education in low-income countries could add 50% to the education financing gap those nations already face. The epistemic dimension of this concentration is as troubling as the economic one: whose knowledge gets encoded, and whose gets discarded, is a question with profound consequences for what students in different parts of the world are taught to regard as authoritative.

**Disability and the Normative Design Problem**

AI educational tools carry within them assumptions about what normal learning looks like. Research drawing on Critical Disability Studies finds that AI technologies in education reproduce systemic inequalities by embedding ableist and normative assumptions into their design, marginalizing non-normative ways of learning and knowing. The promise is real — adaptive platforms like Kurzweil 3000 adjust content and pace to identified needs, while text-to-speech and real-time translation tools facilitate course access for students with visual or auditory impairments, and speech recognition can support those with specific learning disorders. But students with disabilities are particularly at risk of AI bias, due to the lack of diverse user input in the training of most models. Students on the autism spectrum face the added indignity that their structured or literal writing styles may resemble AI-generated text, leading in documented cases to false accusations of academic dishonesty.

**What Prevention Actually Requires**

The literature converges on several principles, none of which the market will produce on its own. Investment in foundational infrastructure is essential, as is sustained teacher training to build both technical competence and the critical capacity to evaluate and adapt AI for local pedagogical purposes. Tools must be co-created with the communities they are meant to serve — not optimized by engineers and delivered downstream. Algorithmic bias operates across multiple stages, from data collection to institutional policy; without ethical oversight and accountability at each stage, AI systems actively introduce new, opaque barriers to assessment and opportunity. Crucially, bias can be reinforced through patterns of user interaction, allowing inequities to compound over time rather than dissipate. The danger, in other words, is not a single moment of exclusion but a self-reinforcing drift — one that will require active, sustained, structurally-informed intervention to reverse.

Is it ethical to expose children to systems whose long-term cognitive and emotional effects are unknown?

The question of whether it is ethical to expose children to AI systems whose long-term effects remain unknown sits at the intersection of developmental science, moral philosophy, and technology governance. The honest answer is that the evidence is genuinely unsettled, the stakes are unusually high, and the ethical frameworks society currently possesses were not built with this situation in mind.

The foundational difficulty is one of developmental vulnerability. Because capacities are still forming, AI does not merely serve children's preferences — it actively shapes how curiosity, cognition, and emotion develop. This is not a marginal concern. The plasticity that makes childhood the richest period for learning is precisely what makes it the most susceptible to structural influence by systems whose behavioral logic children cannot evaluate. Children as "digital natives" face cognitive challenges in distinguishing between virtual and real-world ethics, and the imbalance between technological advancement and ethical education has led to cognitive disorientation and ethical confusion.

The empirical picture is ambiguous rather than alarming, but that ambiguity is itself the problem. AI's use among school students raises concerns about its impact on cognitive, emotional, and behavioral domains, necessitating further investigation at various school levels. On one side of the ledger, AI tools can enhance cognitive load management, information processing, and constructive learning by personalizing experiences and providing real-time feedback. On the other, concerns about cognitive overloading, reducing critical thinking, attentional biases, and over-reliance on technology need to be addressed. Emotional AI tools present a parallel duality: social robots and emotion-recognition technologies offer novel learning opportunities but risk undermining relational learning and fostering overreliance, manipulation, or loss of autonomy.

What makes the ethical question especially sharp is the nature of unknown unknowns. Unlike a pharmaceutical trial, where investigators can monitor for adverse events in a defined window, the cognitive and emotional consequences of sustained AI interaction during childhood may only surface years or decades later, in the form of impaired autonomy, stunted empathic capacity, or distorted epistemic habits. Even if AI technologies were capable of fully replicating human interactions, they could introduce unknown effects that disrupt child developmental processes, leading to ethical concerns. The Beijing Principles on AI for Children, one of the few internationally acknowledged frameworks addressing this directly, acknowledge the problem explicitly: attention should be paid to the uncertainty in the long-term application of AI on children, and short-sightedness that might bring in negative impacts on children in pursuit of short-term gains.

The ethical debate maps onto a broader regulatory tension. There is significant controversy about how AI development should be regulated, with those at one end of the spectrum advocating a strict application of the "precautionary principle," limiting unfettered experimentation, while those at the other call for an "innovation principle," promoting development with as little regulation as possible. For adults, the case for experimentation is strong — informed consent is possible and autonomy deserves deference. Children cannot offer meaningful consent, and their guardians cannot give truly informed consent on their behalf when the risks themselves are uncharacterized. This asymmetry tilts the ethical weight toward caution. As a 2025 analysis in *Frontiers in Education* put it, education leaders must adopt the precautionary principle, not allowing themselves to be seduced by marketing, before allowing any AI systems into classrooms or other educational contexts.

This does not resolve into a simple prohibition. Children will experience an increasingly high level of exposure to AI systems over the course of their lives, with impacts in childhood having long-term effects. Refusing engagement altogether may itself carry costs — in preparation, in equity, in educational opportunity. The more defensible position is conditional exposure: only closed, carefully regulated, and transparently implemented AI systems — designed with direct input from child development specialists — can be justified. Ethicists further argue that the governance architecture must be fundamentally redesigned. Standard fairness metrics offer only snapshots and cannot capture the cumulative impact of repeated exposure to biased systems; to address such long-term consequences, frameworks of developmental justice are needed that could help AI designers and deployers anticipate impacts on identity, aspiration, and capability over time.

The most intellectually honest conclusion is this: the current situation — widespread, largely unmonitored child exposure to AI systems designed primarily for engagement or efficiency, without longitudinal safety data — does not meet the ethical bar that society would demand in any other domain of child welfare. That does not mean the technology is inherently harmful. It means the burden of proof has not yet been satisfied, and proceeding as though it has is itself an ethical choice, not a neutral one.

What consent can a minor meaningfully give to AI-mediated education?

The question of what consent a minor can meaningfully give to AI-mediated education cuts across philosophy, developmental psychology, and a rapidly shifting legal landscape — and it resists any tidy answer.

**The structural problem of consent**

The most fundamental difficulty is not procedural but philosophical. One prominent line of criticism questions whether it is at all possible for students to consent to AI-powered educational systems, given that they are minors and schools wield disproportionate power compared to them. A student who must use a school-mandated platform — or who faces academic disadvantage for not doing so — is not in a position to refuse freely. Consent obtained under institutional compulsion is consent in name only. Some privacy policies are purposefully written in confusing ways, so accepting them should not be considered genuine consent; and even well-intentioned policies, being lengthy and difficult to comprehend, are often accepted carelessly to save time.

**The developmental dimension: assent, not consent**

A more nuanced position distinguishes between legal *consent*, which minors cannot give, and *assent* — a meaningful, if partial, expression of willingness by someone who lacks full legal capacity. Although a younger child is not legally able to give consent, the child has the freedom to assent or dissent after being informed in age-appropriate language. This distinction matters practically. Facilitating meaningful child assent — where children receive age-appropriate information and the ability to opt out of data collection — reinforces ethical engagement; and revisiting assent processes at key developmental milestones can further support children's evolving cognitive abilities, ensuring that they gradually develop an understanding of privacy and data rights. The complicating factor is that young children have limited metacognitive competence and are still developing an understanding of social contexts, personal rights, and roles, making it difficult for them to assess whether they have provided informed assent or whether they have the right to withdraw.

**The legal scaffolding: where law steps in**

Because genuine minor consent is so difficult to establish, legal frameworks largely substitute other mechanisms for it. In the United States, COPPA requires parental consent for data collection from children under 13, with 2025 amendments requiring separate consent for third-party data sharing and mandatory data retention policies. In Europe, GDPR establishes digital consent ages ranging from 13 to 16, depending on jurisdiction. Schools themselves occupy an ambiguous intermediary role: tools may comply with COPPA when used through a school — where the school acts as the parent's agent for consent — with the school signing a data processing agreement with the vendor and the vendor treating student data accordingly. Legislatively, the landscape is accelerating: if operators know a user is a minor, pending legislation would require them to disclose that the user is interacting with AI, provide periodic reminders that the interaction involves AI, and institute additional protections.

Yet legal compliance and ethical adequacy are not the same thing. A 2024 Center for Democracy and Technology survey found that 42% of school districts using AI tools had not executed data processing agreements with their AI vendors, and 31% of administrators could not identify which federal law governs student data privacy. Research also reveals that 60% of school apps send student data to third-party advertising platforms, and only 14% of schools enable parents to consent to technology use.

**The case for dynamic, graduated consent**

The most intellectually serious proposals move away from a single moment of consent — a box clicked, a form signed — toward something ongoing and developmental. Dynamic and flexible forms of informed consent could be adopted to maximize transparency, and by including methodological advancements and ethical developments in the research process, the ethical soundness of AI involvement with children and adolescents could be maintained. This approach treats consent not as a threshold event but as a relationship: one that evolves as the child matures, as the technology changes, and as its consequences become clearer. In K–12 contexts, where minors may lack the capacity to assess risks and implications, parents must be fully informed about the presence, functions, and impacts of AI tools, including transparency regarding data collection practices, algorithmic profiling, and potential privacy risks.

What emerges from the current state of evidence is that no minor — and arguably no adult — can give truly informed consent to AI systems whose data practices are opaque and whose long-term developmental effects remain unknown. Generative AI is transforming education while research on long-term effects is still needed; there are identified risks concerning the cognitive and socio-emotional development of children and adolescents. The honest position is that the consent frameworks available today are placeholders — morally imperfect approximations — designed to distribute responsibility among parents, institutions, and regulators in the absence of anything more robust.

How do we preserve academic integrity when the boundary between assistance and authorship dissolves?

**The Dissolution of Authorship and the Problem of Accountability**

The question of academic integrity in the age of generative AI is not, at its core, a question about technology. It is a question about what authorship means, and why it has ever mattered. Authorship has traditionally implied a cluster of things that travel together: intellectual origination, accountability for error, and the capacity to stand behind one's claims. When a language model drafts the sentences a scholar submits under their name, that cluster begins to come apart — and institutional life has not yet caught up with the consequences.

The current institutional response has settled, for now, on two principles. Authors are held solely responsible for all content, AI tools cannot be listed as authors or co-authors, and human judgment and verification are treated as indispensable. This position is now universal across major academic publishers: Elsevier, Springer Nature, Wiley, Taylor & Francis, and SAGE Publishing all explicitly prohibit listing generative AI or large language models as an author or co-author. The justification is straightforward: the fundamental responsibilities of academic authorship — taking full accountability for a work's integrity, managing legal agreements, approving the final manuscript — can only be fulfilled by humans or legal persons.

Transparency through disclosure has become the dominant governance mechanism. The Committee on Publication Ethics published formal AI authorship guidelines in 2023, updated in 2025, requiring that AI tools cannot be listed as authors and that their use must be disclosed in the methods section. As of January 2025, approximately 70% of journals had established AI usage guidelines, and the trend in academic publishing is continuing to expand toward accepting AI tools while requiring disclosure. Yet disclosure requirements, however well-intentioned, face a fundamental enforcement problem: despite the high prevalence of AI policies, academic writing shows a significant increase in AI content from 2023 onward, and journals with and without AI policies show parallel growth trends — suggesting that publishing an AI policy has not meaningfully affected researchers' use of AI writing tools.

This enforcement gap has pushed institutions toward automated detection, with results that are troubling. The ability to detect AI-generated content is currently not 100% reliable, and unlike traditional plagiarism detection — which can document the original source of plagiarized text — no such evidence currently exists for AI detection platforms. The inequity introduced by these tools is especially concerning: detectors are more likely to flag non-native English writers due to linguistic patterns that resemble AI output, raising serious concerns about fairness and inclusivity in academic assessment. One study found that over half of TOEFL essays were wrongly flagged as AI-generated across all tested detectors. The strongest argument against detection-based enforcement is epistemological: probabilistic tools should not make high-stakes determinations about academic conduct, and a 2024 study documented systemic harm from enforcement based primarily on detection scores.

The more durable response may lie not in surveillance but in redesigning what is assessed and how. While using generative AI for essays may not constitute plagiarism in the classical sense, it can reasonably be determined to be "false authorship," since the student did not write the essay themselves — functionally similar to having a peer write it. The 2025 AWAC Statement on AI and Writing Across the Curriculum recommends separating responsible "supporting" use from unethical "substitution," advising students to retain primary authorship by revising and personalizing automated suggestions. That distinction — support versus substitution — is philosophically sensible but practically elusive, which is precisely why assessment design matters more than detection. When a detector flags an assignment, a more defensible approach is a structured conversation: asking the student to explain key paragraphs, justify sources, and describe how the draft developed — shifting from "gotcha" enforcement to evidence-based integrity review.

Underlying all of this is a deeper unresolved question. Existing authorship models are arguably inappropriate for AI contributions, and revised criteria are needed that account for the ethical and legal dimensions of accountability and intellectual property. A 2025 survey of U.S. university students found that students' personal ethical beliefs — not institutional policy — are the strongest predictors of whether they use AI in prohibited ways, suggesting that institutional energy may be better invested in ethical education and policy clarity than in detection-based enforcement. Academic integrity, in other words, has always depended less on catching dishonesty than on cultivating the conditions under which honesty seems worthwhile. The technology has changed; that underlying logic has not.

Should there be a "right to a human teacher," analogous to a right to human medical care?

**Should There Be a "Right to a Human Teacher"?**

The question is less fanciful than it sounds. As AI tutoring systems grow more capable and economically attractive, the pressure to substitute them for human educators will intensify — in underfunded school districts first, then more broadly. Whether students have, or should have, a legally enforceable claim to human instruction is now a live policy question, and it connects in revealing ways to debates already unfolding in medicine.

The structural analogy to healthcare is imperfect but illuminating. Philosophers of medicine have argued that moral agency belongs only to humans, on the grounds of autonomy and sentience that AI systems lack, and have used this to argue against fully automated systems in clinical settings. The principle of patient autonomy — which entails the right to receive reasonable explanations and to withhold informed consent — has been invoked to reject what some call "black-box medicine." Similar reasoning applies to education: if a learner cannot interrogate the reasoning behind an assessment or a pedagogical choice, something morally significant has been lost. When AI performs teacher-like functions such as diagnosing student understanding, planning learning pathways, and adjudicating correctness, no expert may be able to examine the basis of those judgments, and intervention becomes impossible when they are incorrect.

The rights-based case for a human teacher draws on existing international frameworks. The United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child establishes key rights for children — including the rights to education, dignity, autonomy, privacy, and protection from discrimination and economic exploitation. In the context of AI and education, this implies that tasks such as teaching, assessment, and accreditation should not be delegated to AI systems unless it can be demonstrated that doing so does not violate children's dignity; and even when that high standard is achieved, human teachers must remain central to these processes, as they are better equipped to understand and respond to the emotional and psychological needs of children.

UNESCO has been the most prominent institutional voice on this question. Its position, endorsed by the Santiago Consensus at the World Summit on Teachers, is that teachers are irreplaceable and that AI must support, not replace, educators. A UNESCO Teacher Task Force paper argues that while AI can support teaching by automating certain tasks and providing data-driven insights, it cannot replicate the relational, ethical, and human dimensions of teaching — and that framing teachers as replaceable risks undermining professional identity and reducing education to a purely technical process.

Yet the analogy to medicine also reveals a tension that advocates of a formal right must confront. In healthcare, AI has demonstrably outperformed human specialists in certain diagnostic tasks. AI applications have begun to diagnose some types of cancer better than doctors, identify heart rhythm abnormalities like cardiologists, and diagnose various eye diseases as well as ophthalmologists. If we would not insist on a human radiologist when an algorithm is more accurate, the argument for mandatory human involvement must rest on something beyond mere competence — on the intrinsic value of the human relationship itself. In education, that value is more plausibly central. Effective teaching requires empathy, judgment, and the ability to respond to diverse student needs — capabilities that cannot be automated. Teachers often play a role in shaping students' moral and ethical values by discussing complex issues and encouraging reflection, and AI systems lack the capability to provide such guidance.

There is also a distributional dimension that gives the rights question additional urgency. There is unprecedented federal attention in the United States to accelerating the deployment of AI in K-12 schools, and at the same time growing evidence that this expansion is related to increased risks to students. The danger is not that wealthy private schools will replace their teachers with chatbots — they will not — but that AI substitution will be imposed on precisely the students who already receive the least human attention. A right to a human teacher, in this light, is partly a proxy for a right against educational neglect dressed in technological clothing.

Where current consensus lands is not quite at a codified right, but at something close to a rebuttable presumption: human teachers should remain central, AI should be deployed only where it demonstrably serves learners without violating their dignity, and the burden of proof falls on those proposing automation. As AI becomes more embedded in education systems, it raises pressing questions about rights to privacy, work, culture, autonomy, and the right to be heard. Whether those concerns eventually crystallize into a justiciable right to human instruction — as the right to a human clinician may yet do in medicine — remains genuinely open.

How do we address the cultural and ideological biases embedded in models trained predominantly on certain languages and worldviews?

## The Embedded Worldview Problem in AI Language Models

Every language model is, in a philosophical sense, a theory of the world — and like any theory, it inherits the assumptions of those who built it and the evidence they chose to include. The cultural and ideological biases embedded in large language models are not incidental impurities that better engineering will simply remove. They arise from structural facts about how these systems are built, trained, and evaluated, and addressing them requires confronting questions that are as much political and epistemological as they are technical.

The empirical picture is now well documented. The abstract concern about cultural homogenization in multilingual models is supported by a growing body of empirical work: benchmarks comparing Arab and Western cultural contexts demonstrate that both multilingual and Arabic monolingual models exhibit systematic bias toward Western-associated entities, disproportionately generating Western responses even when prompted in Arabic about food, beverages, or personal names. The problem runs deeper than surface vocabulary. This bias persists even in models specifically fine-tuned for Arabic — and, strikingly, a model may appear culturally competent when evaluated in English but reveal significant cultural blind spots when operating in the target language itself.

The ideological dimension is equally concrete. Research has documented what some analysts term "information cocoons" in multilingual AI, where the same query produces different factual claims depending on the query language — with Hindi queries yielding India-aligned responses and Chinese queries producing China-aligned responses on contested border disputes, suggesting that multilingual models may encode multiple inconsistent worldviews rather than any unified factual understanding. This is not merely a matter of cultural flavor; it is a problem of epistemic integrity.

Underlying these phenomena is a structural imbalance in training data. AI systems are only as good as the data they consume, yet the datasets driving machine learning are far from neutral — they reflect histories of exclusion, erasure, and cultural dominance. From natural language processing to facial recognition, the question of whose knowledge gets encoded is increasingly unavoidable, and to address it is not just a technical problem but a cultural and political challenge that demands a rethink of authorship, representation, and participation in AI design. Many AI systems, rooted in Western epistemologies, fail to account for the linguistic diversity and socio-cultural realities of communities in the Global South.

The standard technical response — collecting more diverse data and fine-tuning models to reduce measured bias — is necessary but insufficient. Current LLMs display far less preference variation than humans across cultural and political lines, reinforcing an "algorithmic monoculture" that overlooks human value diversity. The alignment process itself is part of the problem: while value alignment has become a central topic in AI research, existing studies have largely focused on generic safety or alignment to a single core value — leaving the challenge of pluralistic value alignment underexplored, even though human values are diverse and context-dependent.

A more promising direction is what researchers are increasingly calling *pluralistic alignment* — designing systems that can recognize, hold, and navigate genuinely competing value frameworks rather than collapsing them into a dominant consensus. Alignment is not monolithic: cultural value systems differ and often conflict. Recent work shows that LLMs internalize culturally specific preferences that influence downstream reasoning, and researchers propose addressing this through culture-conditioned approaches or agentic debates that reconcile competing value targets — demonstrating that cultural priors shape model outputs and that single-objective alignment can obscure equity concerns in cross-cultural, high-stakes contexts.

On the data infrastructure side, community-driven initiatives point toward a different model of production. The Masakhane Project, a grassroots research collective across Africa, develops open-source natural language processing datasets, models, and tools for African languages, addressing a field in which most mainstream NLP systems privilege English and a handful of global languages, sidelining hundreds of millions of speakers. Such projects demonstrate that decolonizing AI is less about "fixing" biased systems from the outside and more about designing infrastructures that begin from different worldviews.

What none of these approaches fully resolves, and what remains genuinely open, is the question of legitimacy: who decides which cultural values get encoded, and through what process? Contemporary AI models, founded on Global North notions and data, frequently yield biased outcomes that exacerbate inequalities, and fair algorithms require that local knowledge and cultural values be taken into account, with community input being key to decision-making. The honest answer, as of mid-2026, is that the field has compelling diagnoses and promising early interventions, but no settled solution — and the temptation to mistake technical mitigation for genuine cultural pluralism remains one of the deeper hazards ahead.

What ethical obligations do AI companies have toward education systems they disrupt but do not serve?

**What Ethical Obligations Do AI Companies Have Toward Education Systems They Disrupt but Do Not Serve?**

The question contains a hard premise worth examining first: AI companies frequently disrupt education without genuinely serving it. The distinction is not trivial. To disrupt is to alter the conditions under which teaching and learning occur — rewriting what counts as student work, reshaping the value of credentials, and recalibrating the skills an economy demands. To serve is to bear the costs, meet the needs, and accept accountability for the populations affected. These two things are not the same, and the gap between them is where the most serious ethical obligations reside.

The intersection of AI power and education policy has become increasingly direct: in July 2025, OpenAI released an "Economic Blueprint" for Australia, a document containing recommendations and advice for Australian government policy across multiple sectors, including education. This pattern repeated globally — in the US, a June 2025 "Pledge to America's Youth" on AI education was signed by over sixty organizations, more than half representing big tech companies or their venture capital funders, including Amazon, Apple, Google, Meta, Microsoft, and OpenAI. These companies have, in effect, taken it upon themselves to determine the future of AI in American education. There is an obvious asymmetry here: the entities shaping the terms of educational disruption are the same ones standing to profit from it.

The most concrete harm this produces is inequity. The impact of generative AI on digital equity depends on its implementation: if equitable access to these technologies is not guaranteed, the digital divide could widen. A growing equity concern is that advanced, expensive AI tools threaten to create a new dramatic educational divide, with critics warning that students who can afford this technology may gain "almost superhuman academic capabilities" compared to peers without access. Meanwhile, adoption is uneven, especially between urban and rural districts. Key unresolved questions remain: how should institutions respond to generative AI companies seeking a role in education while offering tools at unaffordable prices, and why are academic-use AI models not currently supported or subsidized more broadly?

A second cluster of obligations concerns bias and the integrity of pedagogical systems. Despite advances in dataset auditing and fairness tools, algorithmic discrimination remained a significant challenge in 2025, with models used in education continuing to mirror historical inequalities. Cognitive dependence is also a relevant concern, where excessive use of generative AI can inhibit the development of critical thinking skills and autonomy in students. These are not technical glitches awaiting a patch — they are structural features of how these systems were built, trained, and deployed, which makes them the developer's responsibility rather than the teacher's problem.

On the question of accountability, current practice falls short of what the moment demands. As privately developed AI systems shape more of daily life, transparency must move beyond technical descriptions to show how accountability works — including who makes decisions, how ethical issues are escalated, and what remediation paths exist when things go wrong. Clear responsibility for harms or breaches should be identifiable in practice, not just in principle. The ACM warned about the manipulative potential of chatbots interacting with minors, and UNICEF emphasized that AI systems impacting children must be designed with explicit safeguards, transparency, and accountability to prevent exploitation and undue influence.

Some companies have responded with pledges. xAI committed $10 million to provide AI education for underserved youth, and OpenAI announced plans to certify 10 million workers by 2030 through a new skills platform. These gestures are real but also instructive in what they reveal: they are voluntary, unilateral, and difficult to enforce — which is precisely the corporate responsibility model that critics find inadequate. The digital divide is rapidly becoming an AI divide, and pledges calibrated in marketing cycles cannot substitute for structural remedies.

What would genuine obligation look like? Scaling AI literacy is a shared responsibility, and companies working with educators, governments, and communities can help create a generation of learners who engage with AI critically, ethically, and confidently. But "shared responsibility" cannot be a euphemism for diffused responsibility — the arrangement in which everyone is nominally responsible and no one is actually held to account. Maintaining educational integrity amidst growing commercial influence requires, at minimum, that AI companies accept binding obligations of transparency, submit to third-party audits, provide meaningful access to underserved populations as a condition of market entry, and acknowledge that the choice between investing in AI platforms that could reach millions and hiring the 44 million additional teachers the world still needs is a genuine dilemma — not one Silicon Valley should resolve alone. The ethical minimum, in short, is proportionality: those who profit most from disruption owe the most to those who bear its costs.

What can AI tutors do better than human teachers, and where do they categorically fail?

**AI Tutors vs. Human Teachers: Genuine Advantages and Categorical Limits**

The question of what AI tutors do better than human teachers is not, at present, merely speculative. A body of controlled research now exists, and the results are striking enough to demand serious attention — and sober qualification.

**Where AI tutors excel**

The most documented advantage is raw instructional efficiency on well-defined content. A randomized controlled trial at Harvard found that students learned twice as much in less time with AI tutoring, with effect sizes ranging from 0.73 to 1.3 standard deviations — a magnitude that dwarfs most educational interventions. A separate large-scale trial produced comparable findings: a 2025 randomized controlled trial found a custom AI tutor outperformed in-class active learning on knowledge gains and time efficiency, and raised engagement.

What drives this? AI tutors possess capabilities that no human instructor can match at scale. They can identify and operate within each student's zone of proximal development, instantly detecting whether a task is too easy or too hard — based on error patterns and response latency — and adjusting difficulty in real time. They are also constitutionally patient and omnipresent: AI tutors are available 24/7, infinitely patient, and adapt in real time to each student's needs, eliminating scheduling constraints and removing the social anxiety that some students experience with human tutors. For procedural, knowledge-based subjects — mathematics, coding, grammar, pronunciation — AI tutors work especially well, while the complexity and variability of humanities or creative fields make developing an effective system considerably harder.

**Where the evidence is mixed**

A 2025 meta-analysis found, more modestly, no significant differences in learning outcomes between AI and human feedback — suggesting that in many standard instructional contexts the two approaches are approximately equivalent rather than AI being categorically superior. The impressive RCT results may partly reflect the weakness of the comparison condition (passive lectures) rather than the particular genius of AI pedagogy.

**Where AI tutors categorically fail**

The failures are not mere inconveniences; some are structural. The most damaging finding comes from dependency research. In a large randomized trial in secondary mathematics, students using GPT-based tutors performed worse on follow-up exams without AI support, showed no long-term learning gains, and substantially overestimated their preparedness — consistent with a "learning penalty" driven by cognitive offloading and an inflated sense of mastery. This is not a marginal side effect: evidence suggests that reliance on generative AI can hinder independent performance and distort self-assessment, with one randomized controlled trial demonstrating that students using a basic GPT tutor performed 17% worse on subsequent non-AI exams compared to their peers.

Compounding this is the hallucination problem. AI generates hallucinations — confidently presented misinformation — and performs inconsistently across tasks, what researchers describe as "a jagged and unpredictable frontier" of capabilities. The danger is that students are poorly equipped to detect these errors: medical trainees achieved only 55% accuracy detecting AI hallucinations overall, dropping to 44.2% in complex clinical scenarios — and as cognitive demands increased, detection accuracy decreased, meaning AI assistance is least reliable precisely where it is most needed.

Then there is the dimension of human formation that no current system touches. Teachers' emotional support is crucial for student motivation and engagement, something that AI technologies have not yet been able to replicate. Over-reliance on AI risks reducing face-to-face interactions that help foster social skills and build rapport, both of which are vital for holistic development. There is also a more unsettling social risk on the horizon: OpenAI has cautioned that human-like voice interactions could lead users to anthropomorphize the AI tutor and develop emotional dependency, with early testing surfacing risks that extended interaction could change social norms and cause some users to prefer engaging with an AI bot over human interaction.

The honest summary is this: AI tutors are genuinely superior at delivering structured, measurable content gains efficiently and equitably — democratizing access to patient, personalized instruction. But they are categorically unreliable as guarantors of deep learning, honest information, emotional development, or the cultivation of independent thought. Routine use of AI tools can hinder the development of metacognitive skills, independent thinking, and intellectual agency, as students begin to outsource decision-making to the machine and risk becoming passive recipients of information rather than critical participants in the learning process. The technology is powerful enough to matter and flawed enough to be dangerous without careful human oversight.

How should the teacher's role be redefined — from transmitter of knowledge to what, exactly?

**From Transmitter to What, Exactly?**

The phrase "from transmitter to facilitator" has become so common in education discourse that it risks meaning almost nothing. It is worth pressing harder on that "what, exactly" — because the answer turns out to be multiple, layered, and genuinely contested.

The transmitter model rested on a specific theory of scarcity: the teacher possessed knowledge the student lacked, and the essential act of schooling was the transfer of that content across the gap. Traditional education focused on memorization and the reproduction of knowledge; with the widespread application of AI, mechanical and repetitive tasks are increasingly handled by technology, meaning learning must shift toward developing abilities that cannot be replaced by algorithms. When information becomes abundant and freely accessible, the transfer model does not merely become inefficient — it becomes structurally unnecessary for a large class of content. The teacher's irreplaceability must therefore be located elsewhere.

One answer, now close to orthodoxy in policy circles, is the facilitator. UNESCO has described the teacher's role as having transitioned from knowledge transmitter to facilitator of learning, a collaborative knowledge producer, and a guide to citizenship in the era of AI. That formulation is richer than it first appears: it implies not one role but three simultaneously — pedagogical, intellectual, and civic. Teachers must now embrace a multifaceted role that includes being facilitators, mentors, and digital pedagogues.

A second, more technically precise answer comes from researchers working at the intersection of AI and pedagogy. The integration of generative AI demands a reconceptualization of instructional design as a process of co-authorship between educators and intelligent systems; this dimension views educators as *epistemic designers* who collaborate with AI to curate, interpret, and adapt AI-generated resources to promote scientific reasoning and inquiry. The teacher as epistemic designer is a meaningfully different figure from the teacher as facilitator: design implies intentionality, judgment about what is worth knowing, and accountability for the architecture of learning experiences — not merely the lubrication of a process students lead themselves.

A third answer concerns what AI conspicuously cannot do. While AI enhances instructional efficiency, personalization, and access to education, it cannot fully replicate the emotional intelligence, empathy, and social interactions inherent in effective teaching. This points toward the teacher as a *relational* and *moral* presence. The social and collaborative aspects of learning require a learning environment that supports interaction and cooperation between students; the teacher as facilitator also plays an important role in providing emotional support and constructive feedback, contributing to students' emotional well-being. On this view, what teachers uniquely offer is not content, and not even method, but presence — the kind of attentive witness to a student's development that no system can simulate.

There is also the question of identity and will. While many teachers are actively redefining their roles as learning designers and embracing reflective, student-centered pedagogies, their ability to fully realize this shift is shaped by institutional support, curriculum demands, and access to professional development; the transition is not merely a technical adjustment but a profound pedagogical and identity shift — one that requires agency, collaboration, and systemic alignment. The redefinition, in other words, cannot be mandated from above; it has to be inhabited.

The core objectives of future student development should emphasize cultivating the ability to ask meaningful questions, think critically, and drive innovation. If that is what students need, then the teacher's redefined role is ultimately that of someone who models exactly those capacities — not by lecturing about them, but by exercising them visibly and in relationship with students. The best short answer may be: the teacher moves from being the source of answers to being the most experienced asker of questions in the room.

What pedagogical models (Socratic, constructivist, mastery-based) does AI enable at scale for the first time?

**Pedagogical Models AI Enables at Scale**

For most of recorded educational history, the most powerful models of teaching have been the least scalable. The Socratic method requires a patient interlocutor willing to withhold answers, ask probing questions, and tailor each query to the particular confusion of a particular student. Constructivism demands that learners actively build knowledge through exploration rather than passive reception. Mastery-based learning insists that no student advance until genuine competence is demonstrated. All three ideals share the same structural problem: they are labour-intensive, individualized, and therefore expensive. A well-resourced university tutorial, a one-on-one Montessori session, a personalised mastery curriculum — these have historically been luxuries. AI is now dismantling that constraint, though with important caveats about what is genuinely enabled versus what is merely promised.

**The Socratic method** is perhaps the most dramatic case. The classic Socratic move is to respond to a student's question with another question — not obstruction, but redirection toward self-generated insight. Socratic tutoring, rooted in the philosophies of Socrates, emphasises dialogue and questioning to nurture critical thinking; by engaging students in structured conversations, it fosters deeper understanding and encourages learners to articulate their reasoning, developing higher-order skills such as analysing, evaluating, and synthesising information. The trouble is that a human tutor employing this method can work with only one student at a time. AI systems now implement the approach through deliberate design constraints. Khanmigo, Khan Academy's AI tutor built on GPT-4 and fine-tuned across 842 courses, operates on a core design principle: it never gives the answer. In a parallel UK classroom trial, Google's LearnLM was prompted to draft concise, Socratic responses aimed at guiding students to self-correct specific misconceptions without revealing the answer. A 2025 randomised controlled trial run across UK schools found that such AI-delivered Socratic tutoring was pedagogically effective, and a Georgia Tech initiative demonstrated that AI can even scale the traditionally resource-prohibitive viva-style oral exam to measure reasoning depth across large cohorts.

Yet the limits are real. If a student does not bring genuine cognitive effort to the conversation, the Socratic method collapses — leaving a chatbot asking questions to someone who has checked out. The method's power depends on a kind of intellectual reciprocity that AI cannot compel.

**Constructivism** — the view, associated with Piaget and Vygotsky, that knowledge must be built rather than transmitted — maps naturally onto what adaptive AI systems do best. Adaptive content sequencing, learner-controlled pacing, and feedback personalisation, all commonly implemented in AI systems, are conceptually grounded in constructivist learning theory, which views learning as an active process of knowledge construction shaped by prior experience and contextual interaction. In practice, this means an AI platform can scaffold a student's encounter with new material so that each concept is an extension of something already understood, rather than an arbitrary external imposition. The ability to analyse individual learning patterns and adapt content accordingly ensures that students are engaged at an optimal level of challenge, promoting deeper learning and retention. What is genuinely new is the simultaneous personalisation of this process for thousands of learners at once — something no human teacher or fixed curriculum could attempt.

**Mastery-based learning** may be where AI's structural advantage is clearest. The classical criticism of time-based schooling — that it advances students by calendar rather than by competence — is well-documented. Mastery models, which insist on demonstrated understanding before progression, solve this problem in theory but create an administrative and logistical burden that overwhelmed most institutional attempts. AI-powered adaptive learning systems offer a bespoke learning experience that dynamically adjusts based on each student's unique learning style, pace, and comprehension levels, preemptively identifying areas of difficulty and offering targeted resources to ensure mastery of content. If a student struggles with fractions but excels at geometry, the system identifies this discrepancy and adjusts instructional materials accordingly, providing additional practice or alternative explanations specifically targeting the gap. A systematic review of empirical studies found that adaptive systems can reduce learning time by 30–50% while improving learning outcomes by 15–25% compared to traditional instructional methods.

Across all three models, an honest accounting must acknowledge the problems that accompany the promise. Critical challenges persist in model interpretability, data privacy, and scalability, especially in low-resource settings. Traditional educational approaches constrained by practical limitations of classroom instruction and resource availability have historically struggled to provide truly individualised learning experiences at scale — and AI does not automatically dissolve every one of those constraints. Equity of access, algorithmic bias, and the risk Dartmouth researchers call an "illusion of mastery" — the danger of cognitively outsourcing all thinking and learning to AI without genuinely learning anything — remain live concerns that the evidence has not resolved. What is nonetheless historically significant is that for the first time, the structural ceiling preventing these models from reaching millions of learners simultaneously has been lifted. Whether institutions and educators seize that opening intelligently is a separate, and still open, question.

How can AI support teachers (planning, assessment, feedback) without deskilling or replacing them?

## AI as Pedagogical Partner: Supporting Teachers Without Supplanting Them

The question of how artificial intelligence can enhance teaching without hollowing it out is not merely technical; it is a question about the nature of professional expertise itself. The evidence accumulated over the past few years points toward a clear, if demanding, answer: AI is most valuable when it absorbs the mechanical and most time-consuming dimensions of a teacher's work, freeing cognitive and emotional energy for the tasks that are genuinely irreplaceable.

**Planning.** The administrative burden of lesson design has long consumed hours that teachers might otherwise spend on students. Platforms such as MagicSchool.ai and Eduaide.AI now offer customizable lesson content aligned to learning goals, and one of AI's most practical strengths is its ability to generate well-structured, standards-aligned lesson plans — teachers can input objectives and instantly connect them to state or national standards, with AI systems highlighting skill gaps and providing actionable insights without hours of manual research. Crucially, however, these outputs are strong starting points but may need refinement to match a school's specific pacing guides or frameworks — a reminder that professional judgment remains the final editor of anything a machine produces.

**Assessment.** The case for AI in formative assessment is particularly compelling, because speed is of the essence. Exit tickets — brief end-of-class checks for understanding — are one of the most evidence-backed formative strategies, with a 2019 meta-analysis finding they improve learning outcomes by an average of 0.4 standard deviations; the obstacle has always been analysis speed, since a teacher with 90 students across three sections receives 90 exit tickets per lesson, and reading and categorizing them traditionally takes 30–45 minutes. AI tools can now compress that analysis to seconds. Teachers using AI-powered evaluation tools save approximately 44% of their time on administrative tasks. Yet the limits matter equally: AI is most reliable for formative assessment of constrained tasks; using it to grade final examinations in philosophy, for instance, is straightforwardly inappropriate.

**Feedback.** Timely feedback is among the most powerful interventions in learning, and AI dramatically shortens the lag between submission and response. Students can receive detailed feedback the same day they submit their work, supporting quicker improvement because corrections arrive while the material is still fresh. Tools like Gradescope accelerate this further: it uses AI to group similar student answers together so that a teacher can write feedback once for a common mistake, and that note applies automatically to every student who made the same error. Even so, caution is warranted — a 2025 MIT study found that students who relied heavily on AI during the writing process showed less cognitive engagement, and teachers on the ground report that AI grading accuracy hovers around 50–55% when a rubric is provided, dropping to roughly 33% without one.

**The deskilling risk.** The danger is real and should not be minimised. Many AI tools replace or stand to replace human activity, and the more AI replaces valuable human activity, the more it risks deskilling humans of their capacities. In education specifically, the risk is not simply automation as such, but pedagogical deskilling through disuse: when teachers are less involved in core instructional decisions, the knowledge and judgment those practices sustain may gradually erode. A subtler threat identified in recent scholarship is what one researcher calls "reverse adaptation" — where educational ends are adjusted to fit machine-readable means rather than the other way around, conditioning teachers to anticipate what the system will approve rather than what the situation calls for.

**What protects teacher expertise.** The UNESCO Teacher Task Force's 2025 position paper offers the clearest institutional answer to this problem: AI can help automate tasks, support learning, and improve efficiency, but it cannot replicate the relational, ethical, and creative dimensions at the heart of teaching. Protecting those dimensions requires treating AI literacy not as a bonus skill but as a professional obligation. Teachers must be able to identify algorithmic biases and maintain ethical integration to prevent alienation — and they must develop the discernment to know when AI complements or undermines their intellectual autonomy. Governance matters too: assessment tools should be designed and implemented alongside teachers, with teachers empowered to review results to ensure validity and with teacher-centred design treated as essential for effectiveness and uptake.

A 2024 World Economic Forum report found that 78% of education experts believe AI will augment teachers' work rather than replace them, and research surveying university teachers and students found consensus that AI cannot replace the human qualities essential for students' competency development and personal growth. That consensus is encouraging, but it is not self-fulfilling. The outcome depends on whether schools invest in professional development — 50% of teachers received professional development on AI in fall 2025, up from just 13% in 2023 — and whether that training cultivates critical judgment rather than passive adoption. The teachers least at risk of being diminished by AI are those who understand it well enough to know, at any given moment, exactly what it should and should not be allowed to decide.

What does effective human–AI co-teaching look like in a real classroom?

Effective human–AI co-teaching is not a single model but a continuum of arrangements, and the evidence from real classrooms — rather than design proposals — reveals a picture that is promising, uneven, and still being worked out in practice.

The most documented form of co-teaching assigns a clear division of labor: the AI system handles tasks that require speed, scale, or data-intensive pattern recognition, while the human teacher retains authority over judgment, relationships, and the moral texture of the room. AI has made significant inroads into intelligent recommendation, automated grading, and learning analytics, and is increasingly penetrating core aspects of teaching, including instructional design, classroom management, and student assessment. Yet in practice, teachers rarely hand these tasks over wholesale. Teachers engage in ongoing interpretive work, evaluating AI outputs, reconciling them with classroom contexts, and adapting them to students' diverse needs. The AI, in other words, proposes; the teacher disposes.

One of the more carefully studied implementations is the Culturally Responsive Lesson Planner, a GPT-powered tool observed in science classrooms. Rather than issuing generic outputs, the system engages teachers in a structured dialogue in which the AI asks targeted, context- and culture-specific questions, and teachers respond with their content knowledge, classroom expertise, and understanding of students' cultural assets — an iterative process that activates pedagogical decision-making, cultural reasoning, and anticipatory judgment as teachers refine the AI's drafts. The cognitive demands on the teacher are in fact heightened, not reduced: critiquing AI drafts, generating cultural examples, evaluating scientific coherence, adjusting difficulty levels, and confirming conceptual accuracy demonstrate that AI-assisted teaching is not a passive activity.

At the junior-high school level, a case study examining a hybrid intelligence classroom found a more tentative equilibrium. Involving forty students and one teacher, the study found that the order degree between the human teacher and AI machines remained at a moderate level while undergoing dynamic changes — a finding that captures the essential instability of early co-teaching arrangements. The partnership is not static: teachers and systems learn from each other across weeks and months, and AI integration is a dynamic process in which constructs such as confirmation, satisfaction, and continuance behavior likely evolve as teachers gain experience and as systems are updated.

A persistent structural challenge is that AI tools designed outside the classroom frequently misalign with the realities inside it. A persistent challenge in AI evolution is the disconnect between design innovations and their continuous practical application within the classroom; their integration often falls short when they are not meaningfully aligned with pedagogical objectives or the realities of classroom dynamics. Teachers in field studies report that a minority of AI suggestions are inconsistent or overly generic — a complaint that matters enormously when a teacher must decide, in the flow of a lesson, whether to trust a system's recommendation.

The question of teacher agency is therefore central to whether co-teaching works at all. Teachers' incorporation of diverse AI tools relies on teacher professional agency, which is seriously lacking in most educational development. Where institutional support is strong and teachers feel genuine ownership over the technology, the results shift markedly: empirical data show that teacher–AI collaboration significantly and positively predicts teaching engagement.

What emerges from the collective evidence is a portrait of effective co-teaching as a practice of continuous negotiation rather than a fixed configuration. Emerging models of hybrid human–AI pedagogy frame AI as a collaborator that expands human capacity rather than a replacement. The teacher sets the values, reads the room, and holds the relationship; the AI extends the teacher's reach into data, personalization, and feedback at scale. Neither works well without the other — and neither, on current evidence, works perfectly with the other either.

How should teacher training change so educators can critically use, evaluate, and challenge AI tools?

**Reforming Teacher Training for the Age of AI**

The arrival of generative AI in classrooms has exposed a structural gap in how teachers are prepared. The urgency is real: many professional development initiatives focus primarily on technical skills rather than pedagogical integration, leaving teachers uncertain about meaningful incorporation into their teaching practice. This imbalance produces educators who can operate AI tools but cannot reason about them — a fragile competence in a landscape where the tools themselves change every few months.

The most persuasive emerging consensus holds that teacher training must be reorganized around three interlocking demands: technical understanding, critical evaluation, and ethical reasoning. This responsibility necessitates that educators themselves possess a certain degree of AI literacy to effectively integrate these technologies into their pedagogical practice and curriculum while maintaining critical perspectives on their use. The critical dimension is frequently underemphasized. AI literacy requires the ability to critically evaluate AI outputs and tools, discerning their quality, appropriateness, and potential biases; this involves understanding ethical principles, data privacy, potential biases in AI systems, and the broader social and environmental impacts — all of which are essential competencies for teachers aiming to integrate AI meaningfully into their practice.

The international policy community has begun to codify what these competencies should look like. The AI Competency Framework for Teachers, published by UNESCO in August 2024, addresses technological advancements in AI and their impact on education. The framework defines the foundational principles, values, knowledge, and critical skills that teachers should develop to understand the role of AI in education and to utilize it in an ethical, effective, safe, and responsible way. Specifically, the framework encompasses fostering a human-centered mindset; understanding the ethics of AI, including privacy, data protection, and avoiding algorithmic biases; building technical knowledge of AI foundations; integrating AI tools into pedagogy; and using AI for professional development. Critically, UNESCO warns that AI could soon replace data-driven educational decision-making, presenting the risk that teachers may lose key competencies if they rely too heavily on AI — so the framework emphasises maintaining human agency, ensuring that AI serves as a supportive tool rather than a substitute.

Yet frameworks alone are insufficient if teacher training programs are not redesigned to embody them in practice. Evidence from recent training interventions suggests that structured, hands-on engagement matters enormously. Teacher training programs facilitate controlled integration of new technologies within secure environments and under expert guidance, encouraging the utilization of AI tools while providing feedback and opportunities for self-reflection. A graduate-level program piloted across 2024 and 2025 found that structured engagement increased awareness of both the affordances and risks of AI use in education, and supported candidates' ability to critically evaluate AI-generated outputs, assess alignment with educational standards, and articulate implications for their own instructional practice.

The ethical dimension of this training cannot be treated as an add-on module. Generative AI may produce hallucinations that sound plausible but are incorrect or outdated, and raises critical concerns such as biases in training data, unequal access to advanced models, and privacy issues related to student data storage — all of which require urgent attention. Teachers who do not understand these failure modes cannot protect their students from them.

There is also an ongoing, unresolved tension in the research between depth and adaptability. The nature of AI is always evolving at a rapid pace, and to cope with these technologies, the curriculum from different disciplines needs to emphasize continuous learning and adaptability, and promote a culture of lifelong learning. Research shows that to achieve effective integration of AI educational tools, teachers need systematic training and ongoing technical support; however, the current teacher training system may not be able to meet the needs of this new type of technology application, and limited resources for ongoing professional development constrain teachers from engaging in long-term learning.

The reform required is therefore not merely additive — inserting a module on AI into existing pre-service programs — but structural. Teacher education must cultivate a disposition of productive skepticism: the capacity to use AI tools fluently while questioning their outputs, scrutinizing their embedded assumptions, and retaining the pedagogical authority that no algorithm is equipped to exercise.

Can AI meaningfully teach non-cognitive skills — empathy, collaboration, resilience, character?

**Can AI Meaningfully Teach Non-Cognitive Skills — Empathy, Collaboration, Resilience, Character?**

The question turns on a distinction that the research community has not yet cleanly resolved: the difference between *practising* a non-cognitive skill and *developing* it. AI can plainly scaffold the first. Whether it can reliably produce the second remains genuinely open.

The most credible case for AI in this domain rests on simulation and scale. Building on work in game-based learning, more advanced AI systems can create dynamic, personalised social simulations — offering students diverse scenarios that adapt in real time to their interactions, enabling them to practise empathy and receive immediate feedback. The promise is especially tangible for populations with specific developmental needs: emotion-aware technologies implemented in serious games have shown value in assisting children with Autism Spectrum Disorder in developing social-emotional skills, and as AI grows more capable, such tools increasingly function as teaching instruments in their own right. For neurotypical learners, AI systems can facilitate better emotional regulation and increased focus by providing tailored learning experiences based on students' emotional and cognitive needs, and the evidence suggests that AI-enhanced social-emotional learning frameworks create a more emotionally supportive environment overall.

AI also offers something genuinely scarce in human pedagogy: tirelessness. An AI will not become impatient or burned out, and it can offer around-the-clock support with endless patience — valuable for students who need steady reassurance or a non-judgmental listener. Moreover, AI systems can track a student's emotional patterns over time, potentially flagging declines in engagement or signs of distress that a busy teacher might miss, enabling earlier interventions. A systematic review using virtual reality, chatbots, sentiment analysis, and wearable devices found that integrating AI technologies into social-emotional learning programs offers unique opportunities to personalise learning, provide real-time feedback, and support students' socio-emotional growth in a scalable manner.

Yet the honest assessment of the literature is that these are mostly proximal gains, not evidence of deep character formation. Research exploring AI as a moral mentor critiques its limitations: diminished human autonomy, insufficient mechanisms for deep moral revision, and the neglect of emotional and transformative experiences. Teaching, in this view, must model the intellectual and moral virtues that AI cannot supply. A particularly acute concern is what one research team calls "social deskilling": without intentional design promoting high-fidelity interaction modes such as live video or virtual-reality-based collaboration, students may experience the erosion of their capacity for empathy, negotiation, and non-verbal attunement.

There is also the question of authenticity. Doubts are being raised regarding the validity of empathic responses from machines and how they can influence students' emotional learning — and cross-cultural variations in emotional expression may further affect the suitability of emotional feedback provided by AI, necessitating culturally responsive design. Even among those sympathetic to AI-based social-emotional learning tools, a prevalent psychological concern remains: whether machine-empathy constitutes genuine emotional support.

The emerging consensus among character educators is that the co-curriculum — communities of practice, service, and shared responsibility — must be treated as a central site of formation, because it is in these human contexts that civic and moral virtues are actually exercised and strengthened. AI, on this view, is better understood as a rehearsal space than a formative one. It can help a student practise the moves of empathy or resilience, but character, in the classical sense, is forged through genuine stakes, genuine relationships, and the kind of friction that only other people provide. Empathy remains a fundamental skill whose most effective methods of cultivation are still an open question — and that uncertainty applies with compounded force to any system that cannot itself feel what it claims to teach.

How do we assess learning authentically when AI can complete any traditional assessment?

**Assessing Learning Authentically in the Age of AI**

The question of how to assess learning when AI can complete virtually any traditional assignment is, at its core, a question about what assessment was always supposed to do. The proliferation of AI in education provides a valuable opportunity to reconsider why students are being assessed, what is being evaluated, and how evidence of learning is gathered. What the generative AI moment has exposed is not a new vulnerability but an old one: a great deal of conventional assessment was measuring compliance with a task format rather than the presence of genuine understanding.

The scale of the problem is not in dispute. The rapid adoption of generative AI among university students is reshaping traditional assessment methods, with 40% of surveyed UK students agreeing that AI-generated content could achieve a good grade in their subject. Traditional assessments such as essays and take-home assignments are increasingly susceptible to AI-assisted plagiarism, necessitating a re-evaluation of assessment strategies. The instinctive institutional response — detection tools, prohibition, a return to invigilated paper exams — is understandable but limited. Attempts to block remote access to technology can often easily be bypassed by students, and attempts to use other technology to detect such behaviour are unlikely to be effective or sustainable.

A more principled response begins with a distinction between what AI can produce and what it cannot perform. Authentic assessments differ from traditional tests by requiring students to apply their knowledge in practical, context-rich scenarios which reflect the complexities they will encounter in professional settings. No AI can attend a seminar and defend a position under cross-examination, conduct a supervised clinical consultation, or improvise in a live design critique. Oral examinations in higher education offer a comprehensive method of assessing student learning, and their advantage lies in their ability to evaluate a range of skills and competencies essential in today's rapidly evolving academic and professional landscapes — including depth of understanding and critical thinking in ways that written tests may not.

One of the more rigorous frameworks to emerge from this period is what has been called the "Two-Lane" model, developed at the University of Sydney and adopted by Australia's national quality agency. This validity-centred design recognises that different learning outcomes require different assessment approaches: foundational knowledge and skills that must be verified as genuinely human capability call for supervised environments such as proctored exams, oral presentations, or in-class demonstrations, while professional capabilities involving tool use call for assessments where AI engagement is transparent and the human contribution is measurable. The logic is honest: rather than pretending AI does not exist in professional life, the framework distinguishes between competencies that must be independently held and competencies that will, in practice, be AI-assisted.

A parallel movement emphasises process over product. Universities increasingly cite "authentic assessments (e.g., real-world tasks, projects), process-oriented assessments (e.g., portfolios), and assignments requiring higher-order thinking" as the most frequently mentioned approaches for adapting to the disruption caused by generative AI. There has been a broader shift in the literature toward greater emphasis on learning processes rather than solely on final products, with portfolios, staged drafting, in-class checkpoints, and reflective journals increasingly promoted as ways to protect validity and foster genuine assessment for learning.

Yet caution is warranted about treating any single format as a solution. Portfolio-based assessment, frequently suggested as a forward-looking option, faces the concern that generative AI could also produce outlines, drafts, or reflective commentaries, undermining intended authenticity. While some research appears to support the role of authentic assessment in reducing academic misconduct, the evidence is unclear, and the likely success of this approach will depend heavily on how it is done in its particular context and conditions. Even oral examinations present equity issues: handwritten and oral exams are known to significantly disadvantage students with physical or mental disabilities.

The most intellectually honest position is that there is no single AI-proof assessment format — only a more deliberately designed ecology of assessments that collectively make learning visible. Leading researchers argue that assessment quality, not cheating prevention, should drive AI-era reforms. Rather than investing in AI detection tools, institutions should invest in redesigning assessments to ensure validity. The disruption, in this reading, is not a crisis to be managed but a long-overdue invitation to ask what it actually means to know something — and to design the conditions under which that knowledge must be shown, not merely produced.

What is the right age and developmental stage to introduce AI tools into learning?

There is no single correct answer to the question of when AI tools should enter a child's education, but recent developmental research has begun to map the terrain with enough precision to distinguish wise introduction from reckless early adoption.

The foundational problem is that children are not small adults. As researchers and educators incorporate AI into educational systems, understanding its implications for developing brains is essential, because children's brains exhibit neuroplasticity — the ability to reorganize synaptic connections in response to learning experiences. This plasticity is precisely what makes the timing of AI exposure consequential: the tools do not simply sit atop a finished mind but actively participate in shaping one that is still under construction.

For the youngest learners — roughly ages three to six — the emerging consensus leans toward caution. At this stage, educators are encouraged to use generative AI "back-stage," employing it to generate age-appropriate stories or visual aids that are then delivered through human-centric interaction; direct child-AI dialogue is discouraged unless facilitated by a qualified educator in a structured, supervised setting. The developmental rationale is grounded in neuroscience: the preschool period is characterized by rapid but fragile growth in prefrontal cortex connectivity and executive function, and longitudinal evidence highlights the specific risks of screen exposure during this window.

The picture shifts meaningfully around age six to eight. Several online platforms that teach children about AI start with kids who are eight years old and above, because studies show that by that age abstract thinking develops and children can grasp concepts more easily. At this stage, children can begin exploring logic, prompts, and basic AI thinking through block-based coding and storytelling tools. The key is that the AI serves as an object of inquiry rather than a labor-saving oracle — children learning *about* the technology rather than delegating cognition *to* it.

The middle-school and secondary years represent both the greatest opportunity and the sharpest hazard. Research demonstrates that generative AI can significantly enhance innovation skills and computational thinking in secondary students when they possess adequate prior knowledge and self-regulation. But the adolescent brain is also vulnerable in a specific way: incomplete prefrontal development makes adolescents susceptible to impulsive technology use and cognitive offloading if unsupervised. This concern is not merely theoretical. In a large randomized trial in secondary mathematics, students using GPT-based tutors performed worse on follow-up exams without AI support, showed no long-term learning gains, and substantially overestimated their preparedness — consistent with a "learning penalty" driven by cognitive offloading and an inflated sense of mastery. Critically, younger participants exhibit higher dependence on AI tools and lower critical thinking scores compared to older participants, suggesting that age-related vulnerability is real and measurable, not merely conjectured.

Across all stages, the research converges on a distinction that educators and parents would do well to internalize. Not all screen-based learning is equal: passive consumption of content does little for cognitive growth, and the benefit comes from interactivity — when a child has to think, respond, and adapt. Relatedly, children need the ability to evaluate AI outputs critically, understand when a system might be wrong or biased, and recognize the difference between genuine human connection and a convincing simulation — and the most useful skills for children growing up alongside AI are not primarily about coding or prompt engineering, but about critical thinking, emotional intelligence, and the ability to question what a screen tells them.

The honest conclusion is that there is no single "right age" in the way there might be a right age to learn long division. The rapid integration of generative AI in K-12 education presents both transformative opportunities and significant cognitive and ethical risks, and the most defensible approach moves beyond polarized debates of prohibition versus unrestricted adoption. What the evidence does support is a staged model: AI as a background scaffolding tool in early childhood, as a subject of curious exploration in the middle years, and as a supervised instrument of inquiry in adolescence — with critical literacy woven through every stage as the essential counterweight to dependency.

How do we design AI systems that strengthen rather than substitute students' thinking (avoiding cognitive offloading)?

## Designing AI to Strengthen Rather Than Substitute Student Thinking

The central problem with AI in education is not that the technology is inherently hostile to learning — it is that the path of least resistance runs directly against the grain of how learning works. When a student asks a chatbot for an answer and receives one instantly, something cognitively significant is lost: the effortful struggle through which understanding is actually built. Learning happens only when the student does the effortful part, the wrestling that cognitive science calls productive struggle. Designing AI systems that protect rather than preempt that struggle is the defining pedagogical challenge of the moment.

The empirical evidence for concern is substantial and growing. A study by Gerlich (2025) surveying 666 participants across diverse age groups and educational backgrounds found a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by increased cognitive offloading. A laboratory experiment using neural imaging found that cognitive activity decreased when participants relied on external tools, and self-reported ownership of essays was lowest in the LLM group and highest in the Brain-only group. Perhaps most troubling for educators is the phenomenon researchers have termed "metacognitive laziness": when learners rely excessively on AI-generated outputs, they may not experience the necessary cognitive difficulty to trigger deeper metacognitive processes, reinforcing a state of metacognitive laziness — a phenomenon stemming from cognitive offloading, where reliance on external resources diminishes engagement in self-regulatory processes.

There is also a structural equity dimension often overlooked in these debates. The cognitive risks of AI — like metacognitive laziness and the illusion of competence — disproportionately affect novices and those with weaker self-regulation skills, thus potentially widening existing equity divides. The students who most need to build independent reasoning capacity are precisely those most vulnerable to having it quietly eroded.

What, then, does better design look like? The research converges on several principles, none of them simple to implement but all grounded in learning science rather than marketing convenience.

**The tool must refuse to do the student's thinking.** Key design principles include a hint-first tutoring stance, automated detection of assessment-relevant prompts, refusal of direct solutions during active assignments, and redirection toward conceptual explanation or strategy rehearsal. This is not merely a policy choice — it can be built into the architecture itself. Some systems are designed so that the system cannot give the answer because it is designed not to — a structural answer to the hallucination and cognitive outsourcing problem.

**Scaffolding must be contingent and fading.** The concept of contingent scaffolding, drawn from decades of educational psychology, holds that support must be responsive to the learner's current state, providing assistance only when needed and fading as competence develops. A well-designed AI system should not offer the same level of support at every moment; it should actively shrink its own footprint as a student's competence grows. Educational AI should support autonomy needs while actively fading its own scaffolding.

**Friction can be a feature, not a bug.** Research on so-called "desirable difficulties" demonstrates that learners often do not appreciate the beneficial effects of tasks that are experienced as effortful but have a positive effect on learning results and transfer of knowledge and skills — for example, retrieval practice is significantly more effective than simply re-reading material, though self-testing requires greater cognitive effort. Practically, this suggests that rate-limiting or introducing delays before providing help can preserve productive struggle: if students must wait before seeing a hint, or can only request help a certain number of times per session, they are more likely to attempt problems independently first.

**AI can strengthen the teacher rather than replace them.** One of the more promising design directions is using AI not to tutor students directly but to support the human tutors working with them. Stanford's Tutor CoPilot is an AI-powered system designed to provide live suggestions to human tutors on how to foster productive struggle, generating expert-like suggestions such as asking a guiding question or providing a hint. This approach keeps the cognitive labor where it belongs — with the student — while giving teachers real-time support for the moments when they would otherwise default to simply providing an answer.

**The AI's role within the learning sequence matters enormously.** Answer-giving where the student should struggle is what harms learning, while guarded design in the same place does not — and the upside is just as design-dependent. The practical question for institutions is therefore not whether to permit AI, but precisely where in the learning sequence the tool is placed and what cognitive job it is assigned to perform there.

The hardest design challenge is what researchers have named the autonomy–accessibility paradox: the design features that render AI support pedagogically effective — immediate availability, adaptive calibration, frictionless engagement — may simultaneously attenuate the productive struggle through which deeper understanding is formed. Making an AI that is genuinely useful without being cognitively substitutive requires deliberate constraint — building systems that are, in a meaningful sense, less convenient by design. That discipline of intentional restraint is the clearest line separating AI tools that build minds from those that quietly hollow them out.

How does constant AI availability change motivation, curiosity, and the desire to learn?

**The Ambiguous Gift of the Always-Available Answer**

Something unusual happens when a capable teacher is available at every moment, willing to answer any question without hesitation: the student may stop generating questions of their own. This tension — between the undeniable usefulness of constant AI access and the subtler costs it may impose on motivation, curiosity, and the desire to learn — is now the subject of serious empirical scrutiny, and the picture that emerges resists simple optimism or alarm.

The optimistic case rests on solid ground. Researchers argue that AI's ability to personalize content fosters intrinsic motivation, aligning with self-determination theory's emphasis on autonomy and competence. AI has been shown to improve students' self-efficacy through features such as providing adaptable learning environments and personalized learning experiences. Empirical data from 2024 reveals that 54% of students show increased engagement in their coursework when AI tools are incorporated into the learning experience. These effects are real. When a student who once stalled at a confusing concept can get an immediate, tailored explanation at midnight, the friction that previously killed curiosity before it could mature is removed. Access expands; engagement, in this mode, follows.

But the relationship between ease and learning is more complicated than simple access suggests. The risk of detrimental cognitive offloading is not theoretical. A growing body of empirical research provides clear evidence for a "performance paradox": AI can boost a student's performance on an immediate task while simultaneously diminishing the durable learning that is the goal. The most striking causal evidence comes from a large-scale randomized controlled trial: roughly 1,000 students were assigned unrestricted GPT-4 access during math practice or to a control group; AI-assisted performance improved by 48%, but unassisted exam performance fell by 17% relative to control — consistent with the cognitive-science prediction that active engagement with learning materials produces more durable learning than passive processing.

The damage to persistence may be even more immediate than that result suggests. Across a series of randomized controlled trials on 1,222 participants, researchers found causal evidence that although AI assistance improves performance in the short term, people perform significantly worse without AI and are more likely to give up — and these effects emerge after only brief interactions of around 10 minutes. The proposed mechanism is revealing: persistence is reduced because AI conditions people to expect immediate answers, thereby denying them the experience of working through challenges on their own. In other words, constant availability recalibrates the inner sense of what effort should feel like. Immediate, unfiltered AI answers rapidly adjust subjective expectations, making unaided effort aversive; metacognitive decay occurs as feedback from actual problem-solving is replaced by solution consumption.

This points to a deeper mechanism concerning curiosity itself. Curiosity, properly understood, is not merely the desire to receive information — it is a motivational state aroused by an uncertainty the mind wants to resolve through its own engagement. From the perspective of self-determination theory, intrinsic motivation depends on learners' autonomy and active engagement; delegating key metacognitive tasks such as planning, monitoring, and evaluating to generative AI might reduce learners' active participation and sense of control, thereby decreasing intrinsic motivation. When every uncertainty can be instantly dissolved by a competent external agent, the very experience that generates curiosity — sitting with not-knowing — is eliminated before it has a chance to deepen.

This is not, however, an argument for withholding AI from learners. Not all friction may be inherently beneficial, and not all ease may be harmful; in some cases, AI may reduce surface-level barriers such as organizing initial ideas or decoding, freeing up students to spend more time exploring and revising, and AI-facilitated ease may unlock curiosity, extend time-on-task, or enable students to reach cognitive depths they may not previously have accessed. The distinction is between AI that scaffolds thinking and AI that replaces it. AI-mediated outcomes depend on the balance between cognitive relief and motivational autonomy: when technology scaffolds reflection and supports agency, it strengthens resilience; when it substitutes intrinsic effort or distorts self-perception, it contributes to cognitive overload.

The central finding of the current research moment is therefore not that AI kills curiosity, but that the architecture of interaction matters enormously. AI systems are fundamentally short-term collaborators: extraordinarily helpful in the moment, but indifferent to what that help does to the person receiving it over time. Designing systems that preserve productive struggle — that guide rather than answer, that ask before they tell — is not a nostalgic preference; it is what the evidence increasingly demands. Whether that design discipline will prove commercially viable, given that users tend to reward instant gratification, is the question that remains genuinely open.

What are the effects of AI companionship on students' social development and human relationships?

## AI Companionship and the Social Lives of Students

The scale of the phenomenon demands attention before its consequences can be properly assessed. According to a recent U.S. survey of 13- to 17-year-olds, 72% of adolescents have used AI companions, and 52% qualified as regular users. A third of teenagers have chosen AI companions over humans for serious conversations, and a quarter have shared personal information with these platforms. What was once a niche technology has, in the span of a few years, become a routine feature of student life — and researchers are only beginning to understand what this means for the development of genuine human sociality.

The case for AI companionship rests on a real and well-documented problem. Individuals who experience elevated levels of loneliness may seek AI companions to fulfil unmet emotional needs; in such cases, AI companions may function as surrogate social partners, offering emotional connection and psychological support that help mitigate the adverse effects of loneliness. AI chatbots can facilitate companionship, mitigating loneliness and depression through socioemotional interactions. For students navigating the social pressures of adolescence, these tools can also serve a preparatory function: an AI companion can give teens space to talk without feeling afraid of social consequences. Scholars describe this as the "stimulation hypothesis" — the idea that AI companions can provide safe spaces for identity exploration and emotional expression, potentially building skills that transfer to human relationships.

Yet the evidence increasingly complicates this optimistic reading. The most methodologically rigorous study to date, conducted by researchers at Aalto University and presented at CHI 2026, examined nearly two thousand Replika users over a two-year period. While AI companions can provide meaningful short-term emotional support, long-term use is increasingly associated with rising signs of psychological distress and a gradual pullback from real-world relationships. A joint OpenAI–MIT Media Lab study reached a compatible conclusion: voice interactions with ChatGPT reduced loneliness and problematic dependence more effectively than text alone, but only with moderate use — heavy daily use correlated with increased loneliness, suggesting that excessive reliance displaces authentic human connection.

The mechanism behind this drift is becoming clearer. AI companions, being always available and frictionless, "quietly raise the perceived cost of human relationships, which are messy, unpredictable, and require effort." A self-reinforcing cycle may take hold: when AI becomes a primary source of emotional support, reduced human interaction weakens interpersonal skills, which makes human relationships feel more effortful and AI interaction more appealing. A study of over 1,100 AI companion users found that people with fewer human relationships were more likely to seek out chatbots, and that heavy emotional self-disclosure to AI was consistently associated with lower well-being.

For adolescents specifically, the developmental stakes are higher than for adults. AI companions create unprecedented opportunities for adolescents to form emotional bonds with nonhuman entities during a critical period for social development. The American Psychological Association notes that adolescents are less likely than adults to question a chatbot's accuracy or intent, and may mistake its simulated empathy for genuine human understanding — and warns that adolescent relationships with AI can displace healthy real-life friendships and family ties and foster unhealthy dependency. Mental health professionals are worried that AI companions may promote emotional dependence, interfere with therapeutic relationships, provide dangerous advice, and exacerbate existing mental health problems among adolescents. At the extreme end of the risk spectrum, these unique risks have already been linked to tragic cases, including the suicides of two adolescents after prolonged conversations with AI companions.

There is also the question of what students are learning from these interactions about the nature of relationships themselves. Studies suggest that such technologies may foster emotional dependence, distort users' perceptions of intimacy and, in some cases, contribute to negative psychological outcomes such as addiction, depression, and self-harm. Unlike traditional chatbots, AI companions are built on large language models, enabling them to memorize past information, hold in-depth and coherent conversations, emulate empathy, and provide personalized humanlike companionship with constant responsiveness in real time. The simulation is sophisticated enough that the boundaries between felt and fabricated intimacy become genuinely difficult to locate.

The honest summary is that the research is not yet settled. Short-term benefits for acutely lonely students appear real; long-term social costs — weakened interpersonal skills, inflated expectations of human partners, withdrawal from the effortful reciprocity that sustains actual friendship — appear equally real, and likely more consequential. Companion AI should be developed and deployed in ways that complement — not replace — genuine human connection, explicitly prioritize psychological safety over engagement maximization, and include safeguards against reinforcing isolation or vulnerability among already at-risk users. Whether the industry, which stands to profit handsomely from emotional dependency, will accept such constraints remains the central open question.

How do we cultivate critical thinking in students who grow up trusting AI-generated answers?

## Critical Thinking in the Age of the Oracular Machine

The urgency of the problem is no longer speculative. By December 2025, the share of middle and high school students using AI for homework had risen to 62 percent, and 67 percent of those students — up more than ten percentage points in under a year — agreed that the more students use AI for schoolwork, the more it will harm their critical thinking skills. Students, in other words, have already diagnosed their own condition. The question is what educators intend to do about it.

The core danger has a clinical name. Researchers term it "cognitive offloading" — a condition in which students who rely heavily on AI demonstrate substantial declines in analytical reasoning capabilities and decreased study motivation. This over-reliance occurs when students place undue trust in AI-generated content, often neglecting their analytical capabilities, which leads to superficial engagement with learning material. The machine does not merely answer questions; it gradually diminishes the felt need to ask them rigorously. The dominant paradigm of generative AI in education positions it as an omniscient oracle — a model that risks hindering genuine learning by fostering cognitive offloading.

What is particularly insidious is the texture of AI's errors. Many AI-generated responses appear confident and well-formatted, yet they may miss nuance, require detailed fact-checking, or reflect underlying biases from source materials. A student accustomed to receiving polished, authoritative-sounding prose has little obvious incentive to interrogate it. Confidence of presentation and accuracy of content are not the same thing, and learning to hold that distinction is precisely what AI makes harder.

The pedagogical response most supported by current research is not prohibition but deliberate reframing of the student's relationship to AI output. Treating AI outputs as starting points rather than definitive answers is the foundational shift. This can be structured concretely: students might analyze a ChatGPT-generated essay, identifying where it provides strong arguments and where it lacks depth or misinterprets the topic. AI reflects the biases in its training data, whether cultural, ideological, or systemic; teaching students to analyze these biases enables them to identify hidden assumptions and consider alternative perspectives.

Scaffolding and metacognition are, on current evidence, the most promising structural tools. Research suggests that scaffolding techniques — including prompt scaffolding, explicit reflection, and modelling — can help foster students' critical thinking skills in digital and AI-driven contexts. Critical thinking acts as a mediating factor, enabling students to assess AI suggestions more rigorously, adjust their trust levels, and ultimately engage more productively with AI. One emerging framework goes further, proposing a shift from "AI as Oracle" to a "Cognitive Mirror" paradigm — reconceptualizing AI as a teachable novice designed to reflect the quality of a learner's explanation, using AI's safety mechanisms as didactic tools to deliberately create a "pedagogically useful deficit." Whether such designs prove scalable remains to be seen.

At a higher conceptual level, scholars argue that the whole framework of AI literacy needs reconceiving. AI literacy must be reconceptualised as a metacognitive social practice that transcends individual competencies — one that encompasses a collective capacity for critical engagement with AI design, deployment, and governance, since the probabilistic nature and epistemic opacity of large language models render traditional rule-based digital literacy paradigms obsolete. Teaching students to use a search engine wisely was one kind of skill; teaching them to interrogate a system that synthesizes and confabulates with equal fluency is another order of problem entirely.

Researchers have called for decade-long studies tracking how sustained generative AI use reshapes foundational scholarly competencies — critical thinking, creativity, and epistemic humility — across disciplines. That such studies do not yet exist is itself telling. The generation now in school is the first to grow up with these tools as ambient infrastructure, and the pedagogy catching up to that fact is still, at best, provisional. What seems clear is that the habits of mind most worth cultivating — skepticism, source triangulation, tolerance for productive struggle — are precisely those that AI, used unreflectively, tends to erode. The institution that treats AI as a shortcut will produce graduates who cannot think without one.

What new literacies (AI literacy, prompt literacy, epistemic hygiene) become as fundamental as reading and writing?

## The New Literacies: AI Fluency, Prompt Craft, and Epistemic Self-Defense

There is a recurring pattern in the history of communication technology: each transformative shift in how information is produced and distributed eventually forces a redefinition of what it means to be literate. The printing press made alphabetic reading non-negotiable; the internet elevated digital and media literacy from elective to essential. Generative AI represents a third such rupture, and the skills it demands are not supplementary refinements to existing education — they are foundational competencies in their own right.

**AI literacy** is the broadest of these emerging skills, and the one gaining the most institutional traction. The World Economic Forum's *Future of Jobs Report 2025* projects that nearly 40% of the skills required by the global workforce will change within five years. In response, preparing students for that future now means going beyond traditional curricula to include AI-specific capacities such as algorithmic thinking, prompt engineering, and understanding bias in data systems. Governments are beginning to formalize this. Article 4 of the EU AI Act mandates that those deploying AI systems must ensure users — including students and educators — have a sufficient level of AI literacy. The OECD and European Commission have jointly developed an AI Literacy Framework for primary and secondary education, and insights from more than 2,000 individuals helped shape its final version. In the United States, the Department of Education took actions prioritizing AI education in July 2025, releasing guidance on using AI to improve education outcomes. These are not isolated experiments — they signal an emerging consensus that AI literacy belongs alongside reading and arithmetic in the foundational curriculum.

**Prompt literacy** is a narrower but equally significant skill: the ability to communicate effectively with generative AI systems. Prompt literacy enables anyone to communicate with and direct generative AI systems without needing expertise in computer programming. Prompts are commands formulated in natural human language that unlock the capabilities of AI and guide its outputs — and with prompt literacy, people can successfully interact with generative AI to achieve defined objectives while exercising judgment and responsibility. This is not simply a technical trick. Prompt engineering can be viewed as a specialized form of computational communication — a literacy for strategically interacting with AI systems to achieve desired outcomes. Research confirms that the quality of a prompt shapes the quality of the answer: the quality of prompts directly impacts the relevance, accuracy, and creativity of AI-generated output. Crucially, prompt literacy goes beyond just providing initial input; it also involves evaluating the accuracy of results produced by generative AI and continually modifying the prompt until the desired information is obtained. In this sense, prompt literacy is inseparable from critical thinking — it is reasoning made operational.

**Epistemic hygiene** — the discipline of governing one's own belief-formation processes — may be the most urgent and least taught of the new literacies. AI systems impact not only beliefs, but belief revision itself — the reconfiguring of mechanisms by which people adopt, reject, or modify claims to knowledge. The danger is not only individual. As artificial intelligence rapidly evolves, the complexity of navigating an information landscape saturated with synthetic content, misinformation, and disinformation is amplified. Deepfakes, AI-generated texts, and algorithmically curated media increasingly challenge public trust and informed democratic participation. Research on student behavior reveals a troubling tendency: students increasingly see AI tools as trustworthy central sources of learning, a posture that skirts critical reflection on biases or model limits, producing automation bias in which AI results are trusted too much, even when errors are recognized — compromising basic epistemic practices such as evidence assessment, source triangulation, and epistemic modesty.

What unifies these three literacies is that none of them is purely technical, and none can be reduced to simple rule-following. Beyond technical know-how, learners will need the human skills that AI cannot replicate — empathy, judgment, ethical reasoning, and collaboration. Without this support, learners may uncritically accept AI-generated content, adopt habits that compromise academic integrity, or neglect skills like critical thinking and empathetic judgment. The analogy to reading and writing is therefore precise rather than rhetorical: just as a person who can decode letters but cannot evaluate an argument is not truly literate, a person who can use AI tools but cannot interrogate their outputs, probe their assumptions, or trace the origin of a claim is not equipped for the epistemic demands of the current moment. The new literacies are not additions to the curriculum — they are its necessary reformation.

What is the promise and the peril of AI-driven accessibility for learners with disabilities, neurodivergence, or language barriers, from the learner's own perspective?

**AI-Driven Accessibility: Promise and Peril for Learners Who Have Been Left Behind**

The most consequential thing AI may do in education is not raise average performance — it is lower the floor. For learners with disabilities, neurodivergent students, and those navigating instruction in a language not their own, the gap between what a system offers and what they can actually access has historically been vast. AI is beginning to close that gap in ways that matter, but it is simultaneously introducing new forms of exclusion that deserve equal scrutiny.

On the side of promise, the evidence is genuine. Generative AI tools can support academic writing, task planning, emotional regulation, and self-management by providing personalised, flexible, and multimodal support. For a student with dyslexia who has spent years watching peers draft fluent prose in the time it takes them to form a paragraph, or for an ADHD student whose executive function collapses under open-ended tasks, these are not marginal conveniences — they can be transformative. Positive outcomes documented in the research literature include improved writing fluency, time management, self-efficacy, and engagement. Meanwhile, AI-supported translation, summarisation, and speech-to-text features are transforming the accessibility of learning materials, particularly for multilingual and neurodiverse learners, enabling real-time adaptation of complex texts into multiple languages or simplified summaries. For a student who speaks Spanish at home and sits through science instruction in English, real-time comprehension support is not a shortcut — it is equity.

Participation in regular classes is now possible for disabled students with the use of AI-based assistive technologies, and such tools can also allow students with disabilities to pursue autonomous learning without depending on the availability of a human interpreter or aide. Extended Reality technologies can enable immersive, adaptable, and practice-oriented learning, while accessibility tools such as speech-to-text have become more accurate in recent years due to advances in AI and cloud computing. The cumulative effect is a set of accommodations that, for the first time, scale.

The peril, however, sits close to the promise. The first risk is bias in the underlying systems. Students with disabilities are particularly at risk of AI bias due to the lack of diverse user input in the training of most models — a notable example being the tendency of AI-generated captions to misinterpret dysarthric speech, creating accessibility barriers in online learning environments. An AI that cannot understand how a person with a motor speech disorder talks is not an accessibility tool; it is another wall. Most studies in the field lack neurodivergent user participation and fail to adequately address sensory and cognitive heterogeneity, accessibility barriers, and gender bias in their datasets — meaning that systems built without the input of those they claim to serve will reliably fail them in ways that remain invisible until deployment.

The second risk is more philosophical but no less real: the erosion of agency. AI can both foster and inadvertently constrain autonomy depending on system transparency and design; if recommendations are overly prescriptive or opaque, learners may follow AI-generated paths rather than initiate or evaluate their own choices, creating dependence rather than genuine self-direction. Experimental evidence reinforces this concern: the vulnerability of students' self-regulation and autonomy in AI-mediated learning is real, with exposure to AI interventions producing significant declines in autonomy in at least one subgroup across multiple experiments. For students who have already been defined by their deficits — who have spent years receiving accommodations framed as exceptions rather than rights — a system that further reduces their sense of authorship over their own learning does quiet, lasting damage.

The third risk is structural. If AI tools live up to their potential for neurodivergent learners, those learners may be more willing to accept privacy or security risks in exchange for the advantages — but if policymakers legislate on the basis of the risk-benefit ratio for the neurotypical population, they could prevent neurodivergent people from accessing tools that are genuinely helpful to them. Regulation calibrated to the majority will always be the wrong fit for those at the margins. Finally, AI technologies in education risk reproducing systemic inequalities by embedding ableist and normative assumptions into their design, marginalising non-normative ways of learning and knowing — precisely the ways of knowing that neurodivergent and differently-abled learners often exemplify.

The honest summary is this: AI-driven accessibility holds more concrete near-term promise for marginalised learners than for any other group, precisely because their baseline access is so constrained. But the same systems, if built without their participation and deployed without nuance, will encode new hierarchies in the language of inclusion. Neurodivergent people having a voice in these discussions is not a courtesy — it is the precondition for any of this working as intended.

What does self-directed, lifelong learning look like when everyone has a personal AI mentor?

## The Perpetual Student: Self-Directed Learning in the Age of Personal AI Mentors

For most of human history, access to expert guidance was rationed by proximity, wealth, and institutional affiliation. A person curious about thermodynamics, contract law, or classical Arabic had to find a teacher, enroll in a program, or settle for books. The arrival of a capable AI that can serve as a persistent, personalized intellectual companion changes the structural logic of learning itself — and the change is not merely one of convenience.

The core shift is from scheduled instruction to continuous, demand-driven inquiry. Large language models can serve as interactive mentors that create a learning plan for the adult learner based on their needs and prior knowledge, and suggest sources and further steps toward desired learning goals. This maps surprisingly well onto older theories of adult education: this vision aligns with the andragogical model and principles of adult learning described by Malcolm Knowles, developed long before the rise of computers. What Knowles argued theoretically — that adults learn best when they direct their own inquiry, connect new material to lived experience, and see immediate relevance — an AI mentor can operationalize moment by moment.

The empirical results are beginning to catch up with the promise. The evidence base for AI tutoring shifted significantly in 2025, moving from promising pilots to peer-reviewed proof: a randomized controlled trial published in *Scientific Reports* by Harvard researchers found that students using a purpose-built AI tutor learned significantly more in less time compared to students in traditional in-class active learning, with effect sizes ranging between 0.73 and 1.3 standard deviations. Crucially, for adult learners — who often return to education with diverse experiences and varying skill levels — this level of customization is particularly valuable, as AI systems can identify gaps in knowledge and propose targeted learning activities, supporting more efficient and meaningful learning pathways.

The mechanics work through something close to what developmental psychologists call the zone of proximal development. AI tutors are uniquely positioned to maintain learners in that productive sweet spot: they can instantly detect if a task is too easy or too hard and adjust difficulty in real time. Analytics dashboards that embed metacognitive nudges promote planning, progress monitoring, and effective time management, while large language model planners support goal setting and task strategy development.

Yet the picture is not uncomplicated. A crucial tension has emerged between assistance and autonomy. The mode of assistance delivery matters: user-initiated support tends to preserve autonomy and promote deeper engagement, whereas proactive, unsolicited prompts may introduce automation bias and reduce active reflection. In other words, a learner who is handed answers rather than guided toward them may be accumulating information while eroding the very metacognitive muscles that make lifelong learning durable. Research frequently evaluates AI applications based on immediate outcomes such as test scores or vocabulary retention, while overlooking learners' internal cognitive and affective processes — goal setting, motivation, and reflection — which are essential for long-term autonomy.

The evidence consistently points toward a hybrid model as the most effective arrangement. The human-AI hybrid model is becoming the dominant architecture: research shows that the best outcomes come not from fully autonomous AI tutoring, but from systems where AI handles adaptive instruction while human educators handle motivation, strategy, complex reasoning, and relationship building. Experimental schools have already restructured their days around this division of labor: AI tutors track each learner's mastery and identify gaps before presenting new concepts; by completing core academics in compressed morning sessions, students free up time for collaborative projects, while teachers transition into mentorship roles, leading work designed to develop communication, leadership, and problem-solving abilities.

There is also an equity problem that optimistic accounts tend to understate. The gap between students with access to cutting-edge AI tutors and those without could become a chasm. Research mapping AI's integration across the United States, China, and India has highlighted challenges related to data privacy and infrastructural inequality — meaning the democratizing potential of universal AI mentorship depends entirely on whether access is actually universal. The technology can, in principle, give every curious person the equivalent of a patient and knowledgeable tutor available at any hour. Whether it does so in practice is a question of political will and public investment, not of algorithmic capability.

What lifelong learning looks like in this world, at its best, is a learner who sets genuine goals, interrogates the AI's suggestions rather than merely consuming them, and uses the time saved on rote acquisition for the harder work of synthesis, creation, and judgment. The AI handles the scaffolding; the human provides the direction and the meaning.

How do we protect and develop memory, attention, and deep reading in an AI-saturated environment?

## Memory, Attention, and Deep Reading in an AI-Saturated Environment

The concern is not new in its structure — every powerful information technology, from the printing press to the search engine, has prompted anxiety about what the mind surrenders in exchange for what it gains. What distinguishes the present moment is the speed and intimacy of the trade. Generative AI does not merely retrieve information; it performs cognitive work — composing, reasoning, synthesizing — that was previously the exclusive province of the human mind. The question of what this does to memory, attention, and deep reading has moved from cultural commentary into empirical research, with findings that are sobering but not yet settled.

**The Cognitive Offloading Problem**

AI tools such as virtual assistants, search engines, and recommendation systems facilitate information retrieval, potentially altering how individuals store and recall knowledge. Sparrow, Liu, and Wegner introduced the concept of the "Google effect," suggesting that the availability of information at one's fingertips reduces the need for internal memory retention. Generative AI intensifies this tendency. AI tools like ChatGPT can create what researchers call "cognitive debt" — a hidden cost to mental abilities when machines are allowed to do the thinking. A 2025 MIT study found that when students used AI to help write essays, their brains were less active and their memory was weaker compared to students who wrote without AI assistance.

The mechanism here is well understood. When answers are instantly available, learners may overestimate their understanding, under-invest in monitoring and encoding, and drift toward transactive memory strategies — the result being short-term fluency but long-term fragility, especially when retrieval practice is bypassed. A separate neuroimaging study reinforces this point: measures of brain connectivity and executive control were worse in a scenario of AI-assisted essay writing than in a brain-only condition, indicating that the neural integration important for learning was diminished.

The relationship, however, is not simply linear. Evidence reveals a complex, non-linear relationship between AI use and cognition — moderate AI usage shows minimal cognitive impact, while excessive reliance correlates with decreased critical thinking abilities, reduced metacognitive accuracy, and lower retention. Notably, younger participants exhibit higher dependence on AI tools and lower critical thinking scores compared to older participants — a finding that carries significant implications for education.

**The Deep Reading Crisis**

The erosion of deep reading predates generative AI but is substantially worsened by it. Research by literacy neuroscientist Maryanne Wolf suggests that digital reading impairs the deep-reading circuit of the brain, weakening the ability to critically analyze, infer, and synthesize knowledge. Differences in processing depth have been observed between print and digital conditions, with evidence of shallower semantic integration during screen reading unless readers deploy compensatory strategies. When AI tools now offer instant summaries of long texts, the incentive to read at depth diminishes further. Digital platforms have ushered in what neuroscientists describe as "continuous partial attention" — a state of being perennially preoccupied and never fully attentive.

Deep reading activates areas of the brain associated with language, sensory processing, and emotional response; when we read deeply, we are more likely to understand complex concepts and perspectives different from our own. To lose this practice, then, is to lose something beyond literary enjoyment — it is to weaken the neural architecture that underlies empathy, sustained reasoning, and the tolerance for complexity.

**What Can Be Done**

The prescription that emerges from current research is neither blanket rejection of AI nor uncritical embrace, but deliberate architectural choices about when and how cognitive effort is preserved or surrendered. When AI surfaces definitions and local cues at the moment of need, it trims friction and limits split attention — provided that core generative moves remain with the learner. Benefits peak when help is short, local, and step-bound, then fades to promote independent problem-solving.

On the memory front, the most robust counter-strategy remains retrieval practice — the active recall of information rather than passive re-reading. Retrieval practice, or the active recall of information from memory, is a highly effective learning strategy that strengthens memory and comprehension, an effect robust and strongly backed by research in cognitive psychology. Spaced repetition and retrieval practice, empowered by AI, hold genuine promise for constructing personalized learning systems — here the technology serves consolidation rather than replacing it. When learners revisit content they have forgotten, the brain must work harder to retrieve the information, thereby reinforcing memory; over time, these retrieval events transform short-term memory into lasting understanding.

For deep reading, neuroscientist Maryanne Wolf's framework offers the clearest direction: digital reading may condition the brain to favor quick browsing over deep reading, which is why Wolf argues for the cultivation of "bi-literate brains" that can navigate both digital and print environments. This means intentionally reserving time for long-form print reading, treating it not as nostalgia but as cognitive training. As AI changes the way humans think across reflection, reasoning, and creative production, curriculum models are needed that not only build knowledge but encourage metacognition, problem-solving, and original creation.

The underlying principle threading all of this together is one of deliberate resistance to ease. The brain, like muscle, strengthens through effort and atrophies through disuse. An AI-saturated environment is, structurally, an environment that removes effort at every turn. Preserving memory, attention, and deep reading therefore requires treating friction — the discomfort of recalling, of sustaining focus, of following an argument to its end — not as a problem to be optimized away, but as the mechanism of growth itself.

Is the industrial-era school model (grades, classrooms, standardized curricula) still viable, and what replaces it?

**The Industrial School Model and Its Discontents**

The architecture of modern schooling — fixed grade levels sorted by age, forty-five-minute periods punctuated by bells, a uniform curriculum delivered to everyone simultaneously — was not designed with learning as its primary goal. For decades, the structure of high school education has remained largely unchanged, rooted in a model that mirrors the industrial factory system, with its 45-minute class periods, ringing bells, and standardized curricula tracing back to an era when schools were designed to prepare students for factory work. The critical question of the present moment is whether that architecture has simply aged, or whether it has become actively counterproductive.

Before accepting the critique wholesale, one important caveat deserves attention. The terms "factory model school" and "industrial era school" are, according to educational historians, ahistorical phrases that emerged in the mid-to-late twentieth century and function largely as rhetorical devices by those advocating change — descriptions that historians characterize as misleading and inaccurate representations of how American public education actually developed. The critique, in other words, contains a mythological element: the industrial school was never quite as mechanically uniform as its detractors imply, and invoking the factory metaphor can obscure more than it reveals.

That said, the structural tensions are real. Nearly all major reform efforts of the past several decades left the basic tenets of the industrial-paradigm classroom intact — an approach where groups of same-aged students all learn the same thing at the same time, advanced more than a century ago as a means to rank and sort students into different life pathways. The system's central flaw is its indifference to where any individual student actually stands. It is unforgiving to those who fall behind: what is taught is based on age, not on what a student knows, so that stumbling for any reason — a pandemic, a family crisis — can make it genuinely difficult to catch back up, especially in cumulative subjects like mathematics.

Employers increasingly report that graduates lack critical thinking, problem-solving, and digital literacy skills, and that the industrial-age model's focus on standardized testing and memorization leaves students ill-equipped for innovation. Meanwhile, critics of standardized curricula highlight an overemphasis on assessment leading to narrowed instruction, insufficient flexibility for diverse learners, and rising achievement gaps.

The most serious contender to replace the age-grade-curriculum triad is competency-based education (CBE), which reorients schooling around demonstrated mastery rather than time served. CBE focuses on skill mastery rather than seat time or letter grades, allowing students to progress at their own pace and demonstrate real understanding. The logic is intuitive: traditional education has students spend a set amount of time on a subject, take an exam, and move on whether or not they have truly mastered the material, while competency-based learning flips this by asking whether a student can actually demonstrate mastery of specific skills and knowledge. The growth in adoption is measurable: as of 2025, over 1,500 institutions in the United States have adopted approximately 2,400 undergraduate and 750 graduate competency-based education programs.

Alongside CBE, personalized learning — powered increasingly by artificial intelligence — is moving from experimental to mainstream. Personalized learning is shifting from theory to standard practice, with schools moving toward teaching methods that focus on each student's pace, interests, and goals, using digital tools to track progress and make rapid adjustments — an approach that, when it works well, helps close learning gaps and keeps students more engaged. Adaptive learning systems can adjust content delivery in real time based on learner performance, while AI-based systems help educators identify gaps and align content with existing standards.

Yet a note of realism is warranted. The only change processes ever used in public education to significantly alter practice reflect industrial-age thinking and methodologies — and there is a deep irony in trying to end the era of factory-model schools by using factory-model strategies for change. Many educators also report limited training in how to manage personalized instruction at scale — a bottleneck that no technology resolves on its own. What is emerging, then, is less a clean replacement than a prolonged and contested transition: the shell of the old model persists in most public institutions while its logic is eroded, incrementally and unevenly, by competency frameworks, adaptive tools, and a growing recognition that sorting children by birth year was always a convenience, not a pedagogy.

How should curricula be redesigned when factual content is instantly accessible and rapidly obsolete?

**Curriculum in the Age of Instant Knowledge**

The central paradox confronting educators today is one of abundance: the very tool that renders a fact instantly retrievable also makes the memorization of that fact seem pedagogically pointless. Students can now access instant factual answers through AI platforms, making traditional rote-recall exams increasingly ineffective and outdated. Yet the conclusion that content no longer matters would be as mistaken as the assumption that it matters in exactly the way it once did. The real challenge is finer-grained: to distinguish which kinds of knowledge are genuinely outsourceable to machines, and which must remain deeply internalized by human minds.

One useful map for this distinction comes from cognitive science. Declarative knowledge — the memorization of facts and data — is considered the simplest level, while procedural knowledge involves the automated understanding of *how* to use problem-solving strategies, and conditional knowledge, the highest level, requires understanding *how*, *when*, and *why* to use each strategy. AI systems, as currently constituted, are remarkably good at the first level and serviceable at the second. The third — knowing when a tool or argument is appropriate to a given situation — remains stubbornly human. Curriculum redesign, on this view, should shift weight steadily upward through these levels, treating the declarative layer not as the destination but as the scaffolding through which deeper understanding is constructed.

This does not mean abandoning content altogether. The question "if AI knows everything, why learn anything?" is a dead-wrong framing — AI neither "knows" everything nor is capable of acting on its own. A student who has never grappled with the internal logic of evolutionary biology, the structure of a legal argument, or the arithmetic of compound interest cannot meaningfully direct, evaluate, or contest an AI-generated answer on those subjects. Conceptual and epistemic knowledge — understanding *why* a field is structured as it is, and how its claims are validated — may actually become *more* valuable as AI handles more of the retrieval work. Knowledge should be assessed for relevance, developing the right mix between declarative, procedural, conceptual, and epistemic layers, and modernizing traditional disciplines accordingly.

The institutional implications of this shift are substantial. Assessment is the most immediate battleground. With 40% of surveyed UK students agreeing that AI-generated content could achieve a good grade in their subject, faculty are increasingly concerned about academic rigor — and rather than engaging in a reactive "AI-detection arms race," educators are advised to proactively redesign assessment strategies. At the national policy level, governments are beginning to act: the UK's 2025 curriculum reforms include making media literacy and financial literacy compulsory in primary school, and replacing the narrowly focused computer science GCSE with a broader, future-facing computing qualification, alongside exploring a new credential in data science and AI for sixteen-to-eighteen-year-olds.

A related and underappreciated danger is what researchers call automation bias. Students increasingly treat AI outputs as the central, trustworthy source of learning, skipping critical reflection on the model's limits — a tendency that compromises ground-level epistemic practices such as evidence assessment, source triangulation, and epistemic modesty. A curriculum redesigned for this environment must therefore treat epistemics — knowing *how* one knows, and how to evaluate competing claims — as a core subject rather than an implicit byproduct of other learning.

Perhaps the deepest reorientation concerns the temporal dimension of knowledge itself. The first quarter of the twenty-first century has been defined by shocks and long-run transformations that have reshaped economic and social systems, with changes rapid enough to leave individuals, firms, and governments less time to adapt. As skill demands evolve faster than policy cycles, investing in lifelong learning and using timely labour-market intelligence are crucial to help people adapt. This implies that curricula should be designed less around a fixed canon of content and more around the meta-cognitive capacity to keep learning — what the Center for Curriculum Redesign calls adaptability, arguing that adaptability is more essential than ever given AI, and that schools must actively teach it.

The risk in all of this, however, is a false binary that discards disciplinary knowledge in favor of vague "twenty-first-century skills." Reform efforts have largely failed because they focus on fixing past problems with uniform solutions; applying new technologies within outdated paradigms is equally ineffective. What is needed is neither the old cathedral of facts nor a content-free skills gymnasium, but a redesigned architecture in which durable conceptual knowledge, practiced judgment, epistemic self-awareness, and genuine adaptability are woven together — with the specific factual content serving as the material through which those capacities are built, not the end in itself.

What is the future of universities, credentials, and degrees when skills can be learned and demonstrated outside institutions?

**The Credential in Question**

The institution of the university degree is not collapsing, but it is no longer uncontested. For most of the twentieth century, a bachelor's degree served as a reliable proxy for capability, discipline, and general readiness for professional life. That arrangement is under genuine pressure — not because universities have grown less rigorous, but because the labour market is changing faster than four-year curricula can follow. The labour market now changes faster than curricula. A degree takes four to five years to complete, but technology and required skills change every six to twelve months. The result is a structural mismatch that no amount of curriculum review can fully close.

**The Rise of the Alternative**

Into this gap has rushed a proliferating array of shorter-form credentials. Out of the 1.8 million credentials now offered across the United States, digital badges and certificates dominate — with digital badges alone numbering over one million, according to a recent report from Credential Engine. Degrees, by comparison, made up a smaller fraction of the credentials tallied: 264,099 programmes. The microcredential market itself is not trivial in economic terms; the global microcredential market is projected to exceed USD 3.5 billion in 2025, with an annual growth rate above 17 percent through 2030, with strong presence in technology, healthcare, education, and sustainability sectors.

The appeal is straightforward. Microcredentials have advantages over standard degrees in cost, duration, and structure: they are shorter in duration, more focused in terms of skills and subject matter, and more adaptable in curriculum and delivery than a conventional degree programme. There is "growing appetite, more demand from learners for more efficient and direct routes to employment opportunities," as one analyst at Jobs for the Future has put it. In the most recent enrollment data, students pursuing short-term credentials increased 6.6 percent in a single year, outpacing growth in associate and bachelor's degree programmes.

**The Gap Between Policy and Practice**

Yet the most revealing data concerns not the supply of alternatives, but what employers actually do when confronted with them. Here the evidence is striking and sobering. While 85 percent of companies claim to use skills-based hiring, only 0.14 percent of hires are actually impacted by degree requirement removal. At least 26 states and major private companies including IBM and Accenture have stripped degree requirements from job listings, with seven states showing double-digit percentage increases in postings without degree requirements between 2019 and 2024. But less evidence has emerged of employers actually hiring non-degreed job seekers in substantial numbers: Burning Glass and Harvard Business School found that fewer than 1 in 700 hires in 2023 benefited from the shift to skills-based hiring.

The explanation is cultural as much as structural. The people in charge of hiring are typically senior managers accustomed to college-related data — school attended, courses taken, grades earned, degree attained — and without those reference points, they need an entirely new template for assessing candidates. Cultural biases will not simply evaporate because a CEO issues a press release. There is also a regulatory obstacle: many of the same states that have eliminated degree requirements still regulate non-degree training providers as if they were traditional college institutions, forcing them through a labyrinth of licensing rules and reporting mandates never designed for their models.

**Unbundling, Not Replacement**

The most durable forecast from those studying the field is not disruption but transformation through what might be called unbundling. The future of higher education will likely not be about replacing degrees, but unbundling them into independent modules. Universities are not sitting still: Arizona State University has developed "stackable" microcredentials that can accumulate toward a master's degree, while European universities are integrating microcredentials into the Bologna Process to allow cross-border recognition. At the same time, the gap between skills-based hiring intent and actual practice exists partly because the infrastructure for verifying skills did not exist until recently; the emerging standard is Open Badges 3.0, built on the W3C Verifiable Credentials Data Model.

What appears to be emerging, then, is a layered system: rather than degrees being replaced, a hybrid system is developing — perhaps something like a "skills passport" or "lifelong learning credential" that records all learning, from a first degree to the microcredentials accumulated throughout a working life. Traditional degrees remain essential for leadership roles, advanced research, and professions that require intensive accreditation, while shorter credentials gain ground in technical fields where demonstrable, current competence matters more than breadth. The World Economic Forum estimates that over one billion workers will need to reskill by 2030, and LinkedIn Learning reports that the technical skill half-life, from a marketability standpoint, is approximately two years — a pace at which no four-year degree, however well designed, can keep up on its own. The university will survive, but likely as one node in a much larger, more fluid learning infrastructure rather than as the sole gateway to professional life.

How can public education systems keep pace with private AI development without surrendering governance to tech companies?

The tension between public education and private AI development is not a future problem — it is an acute, present one, and the recent record makes clear that governments at every level are scrambling to catch up rather than lead.

**The Scale of the Asymmetry**

The speed differential between private AI development and public institutional response is almost comically lopsided. The 2025 State EdTech Trends Report, released by SETDA in collaboration with Whiteboard Advisors, captures a pivotal moment: for the first time, artificial intelligence has overtaken cybersecurity as the top state education technology priority. Yet this priority status arrived largely in reaction to commercial products already saturating classrooms. According to CoSN's May 2025 State of EdTech District Leadership report, 43 percent of districts lack formal policies or guidance for AI use — even as 80 percent of districts have generative AI initiatives underway. The tools arrived before the governance did.

**The Legislative Response: Active but Fragmented**

Legislatures are not standing still. During the 2025 legislative session, 53 bills were proposed on the use of AI in education across 21 states, including 51 that apply specifically to K-12 schools; four states — Illinois, Louisiana, Nevada, and New Mexico — enacted legislation. Across these bills, five themes emerge: advancing AI literacy for students and professional development for teachers; requiring guidance on responsible use including privacy and transparency; creating studies or task forces; prohibiting specific AI uses such as those related to student mental health support or replacing teachers; and addressing AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery. The breadth is encouraging; the fragmentation is not. A patchwork of state-level rules — some permissive, some restrictive — creates an uneven landscape that sophisticated vendors can navigate at will, adapting their terms of service to whichever jurisdiction offers the least friction.

At the federal level, the posture has been more promotional than regulatory. The White House launched a "Pledge to America's Youth: Investing in AI Education" that now includes commitments from over 200 tech companies and organizations including Google, OpenAI, Microsoft, NVIDIA, and Salesforce. America's AI Action Plan highlights the current administration's aversion toward regulation by providing that states should not have access to federal funding on AI-related matters should they implement "burdensome AI regulations." This creates a structural incentive for states to compete on permissiveness rather than protection.

**The Data Governance Crisis**

The governance vacuum is not theoretical. In December 2024, PowerSchool — a leading provider of cloud-based software managing student grades, attendance, and records for approximately 16,000 schools — detected unauthorized access; by January 2025, the scope was clear: more than 62 million student records and nearly 10 million teacher records had been exfiltrated, representing the largest breach of children's data in US history. The breach exposed an industry governance model that has normalized the centralization of children's data at unprecedented scale without commensurate security architecture, regulatory oversight, or enforceable data minimization requirements — and one year later, that model remains largely intact.

More insidious than spectacular breaches is the routine opacity of how student data flows. More than half of education technology companies did not fully disclose how learner data was being collected and shared, creating ethical and reputational risks for universities and schools. This lack of visibility has led to the rise of what experts call "shadow AI" — unapproved apps and browser extensions that process student inputs, store them indefinitely, or reuse them to train commercial models, often free, widely adopted, and nearly invisible to IT teams.

**What Public Governance Can Actually Do**

Three strategies carry genuine promise. The first is the creation of public AI infrastructure as a counterweight to proprietary systems. Digital Promise has launched a K-12 AI Infrastructure Program, a $26 million, multi-year effort to create openly shared and freely available models, datasets, and benchmarks designed for education. The logic here is important: if public institutions depend entirely on proprietary models, they can only govern those models from the outside. Owning or co-developing public-interest AI infrastructure is a different kind of leverage.

The second is what some governments are calling regulatory sandboxes — formally supervised frameworks where governments can engage innovators to test tools that challenge existing legal frameworks, in order to understand risks and safeguards and help shape regulation. This allows regulators to develop genuine technical knowledge before committing to rules, rather than legislating from ignorance.

The third is institutional self-defense through procurement power. Some higher education institutions are addressing data risk by maintaining local control of AI infrastructure — for example, the University of Montana worked with Amazon Web Services and Microsoft to create a university-owned data center that runs enterprise-grade models directly from these companies, ensuring all data is hosted in the university system and is not used to train any models. Public institutions collectively represent enormous purchasing leverage; exercising that leverage through coordinated procurement standards could reshape vendor incentives across the entire sector.

**The Deeper Question**

Realizing the benefits of AI in education while managing its risks calls for collaboration across public, private, and civic actors, supported by strong public oversight and safeguards; as Big Tech companies expand their presence in education ecosystems, it is critical for governments to strengthen their capacity to engage with private sector partnerships on their own terms. The phrase "on their own terms" carries real weight. The risk is not merely that public systems will fall behind technically — it is that, in the scramble to catch up, they will accept the terms set by the companies whose products they adopt, effectively outsourcing not just the tools but the values embedded in them. Keeping pace, in this context, means something more demanding than speed: it means building the institutional knowledge, legal architecture, and public infrastructure to remain the authoritative party in every negotiation.

Who has legitimate authority to govern AI in education — states, markets, professions, or communities — and on what principles?

**Who Has Legitimate Authority to Govern AI in Education?**

The question of legitimate authority over AI in education is not merely technical or administrative — it is a question about which institutions are trusted enough, and accountable enough, to make consequential decisions about how children learn and what kinds of cognitive lives they lead. The current landscape offers no clean answer, but it does reveal a recognizable pattern: authority is being improvised, from many directions simultaneously, against a background of structural uncertainty.

**The State's Case — and Its Limits**

States have, in practice, moved fastest. When ChatGPT debuted in 2022, no states had policies related to generative AI in schools; by April 2025, at least 28 states had published guidance on AI in K–12 settings. By the end of 2025, that number had grown to 34 states with statewide guidance. This is not a trivial development. The state — understood as the collective democratic authority vested in elected legislatures and their administrative agencies — has the oldest and most legitimate claim to govern education precisely because it bears fiduciary responsibility for children. Colorado, for instance, passed what analysts have called the most comprehensive state-level AI governance law in the country, targeting deployers of "high-risk" AI systems making consequential decisions in education, employment, and other domains, requiring risk-management programs, consumer disclosures, and mitigation of algorithmic discrimination.

Yet the state's authority is complicated by fragmentation. AI governance in the United States has emerged through a patchwork of policies and voluntary guidelines rather than a unified legal framework; federal agencies rely on existing authorities and executive guidance while Congress has yet to pass comprehensive legislation, and states are moving forward with their own laws, producing a fragmented landscape that complicates compliance and national policy coordination. This fragmentation is not merely untidy — in the absence of coordinated state leadership, AI adoption in schools is often uneven, driven by local capacity and vendor offerings rather than a coherent statewide vision, risking the widening of inequities and leaving educators without clear expectations for responsible use.

**Markets as De Facto Governors**

In the absence of binding law, markets have become the dominant governing force by default. The White House launched a "Pledge to America's Youth" that now includes commitments from over 200 tech companies and organizations — including Google, OpenAI, Microsoft, NVIDIA, and Salesforce — while the American Federation of Teachers launched a National Academy for AI Instruction in partnership with Microsoft, OpenAI, and Anthropic. These are market actors choosing to shape educational norms, and their influence is substantial. The legitimacy problem is plain: companies optimizing for adoption and revenue are not structurally positioned to prioritize child welfare, equity, or pedagogical integrity over growth. AI vendor contracts at major public universities are already becoming flashpoints for broader debates about austerity, faculty shared governance, and the commercialization of public higher education; ed-tech vendors pursuing large institutional contracts should expect intensifying scrutiny from faculty stakeholders. Market authority, in short, is real but not legitimate in the foundational sense — it lacks democratic accountability.

**Professional Claims and Their Strength**

The professions — teachers, faculty, and the organizations that represent them — hold a different kind of legitimate claim. They possess the situated knowledge of what pedagogically works, and they bear direct moral responsibility for students in their care. The American Association of University Professors has recommended that AI governance in higher education be adopted through governance bodies such as academic senates, with mechanisms for enforcement and oversight. Several states have taken heed: Illinois proposed an Advisory Board to give guidance, oversight, and evaluation of education technology, including AI, in collaboration with the state education agency, with the board also required to develop standards on safety, privacy, and transparency and guidance for districts and educators. Professional authority is at its strongest when it is structured into formal oversight roles rather than left as mere practitioner preference.

**The Principled Case for Polycentric Governance**

No single locus of authority is sufficient. The state supplies democratic legitimacy and enforcement power but risks uniformity, bureaucratic lag, and political capture. Markets supply speed and resources but not accountability. Professions supply pedagogical wisdom but not systemic reach. Communities — parents, students, local civil society — supply the moral particularity that any general rule will inevitably smooth over.

What the evidence suggests is that legitimate governance requires these actors to be organized in relation to one another, not left to compete. There is a growing shift from early experimentation toward more structured discussions around guidance, oversight, and use cases in schools, with policymakers seeking ways to ensure safe and ethical AI use without slowing down innovation. Practitioners and scholars have argued that ethical aspirations remain ineffective without binding legal mandates — yet mandates without professional input produce rules that misfire in actual classrooms.

On principles, a workable consensus is emerging, if not yet enforced: transparency about when and how AI is used, fairness in ensuring that algorithmic tools do not reproduce existing inequities, accountability in clarifying who bears responsibility when AI advice goes wrong, and a commitment to preserving the epistemic autonomy — the critical thinking and creativity — that education is meant to cultivate. Ethics-focused AI education remains severely underrepresented in technical curricula worldwide despite increasing global concern about fairness and accountability. That gap between stated principle and institutional practice is, at bottom, where the governance question still lives.

How do developing nations avoid a new "AI colonialism" in education while leapfrogging infrastructure gaps?

## AI Colonialism and the Education Leapfrog: Navigating Dependency in the Global South

The promise of artificial intelligence in education is nowhere more vivid — or more fraught — than in the Global South. For nations still contending with teacher shortages, unreliable electricity, and uneven broadband access, AI tutoring systems and adaptive learning platforms appear to offer a shortcut around decades of infrastructure-building. The question is whether that shortcut leads to genuine empowerment or to a subtler form of dependency that scholars have begun calling "AI colonialism."

Digital colonialism describes the contemporary dynamics in which powerful nations and multinational corporations deploy digital infrastructure, platforms, and data-harvesting tools to extend influence over less technologically advanced societies — and AI amplifies these dynamics by providing sophisticated mechanisms for data collection, surveillance, and automated decision-making that can reinforce unequal power structures. In education specifically, the danger is epistemic as much as economic. AI systems are developed within specific epistemic, linguistic, and regulatory contexts, embedding assumptions about originality, relevance, and authority; indexing practices already privilege English-language journals and Global North publication venues, systematically marginalizing regional scholarship. In this way, AI-mediated infrastructures shape not only how education is delivered, but also which forms of knowledge are rendered visible, legitimate, and valuable.

The infrastructure gap itself is real and stubborn. AI adoption across the Global South is hindered by persistent gaps in infrastructure, data, skills, and governance, with reliable electricity and broadband remaining unevenly available and local datasets often scarce. Yet attitudes toward AI are strikingly different from those in wealthier nations. The 2025 Edelman Trust Barometer found that fewer than one in three Americans feel comfortable with businesses using AI, while in India, Indonesia, and Nigeria nearly two-thirds express comfort — a gap that reflects AI's perception as threat in the North and as opportunity in the South. The potential for leapfrogging — wherein institutions can bypass traditional development stages by adopting cutting-edge technologies to bridge gaps between industrialized and developing countries — makes AI particularly compelling for the Global South. Kenya's M-Shule is one concrete illustration: in education, AI is helping bridge critical teacher shortages, with M-Shule using AI to create personalized learning plans based on student performance data and delivering custom SMS-based lessons to thousands of students.

But leapfrogging built on borrowed architecture carries its own risks. Many breakthroughs rely on Northern institutions, creating benefits but also exposing a fragile dependency — when outside funding or partnerships end, local efforts can stall, meaning that leapfrogging risks being built on borrowed foundations. The remedies being proposed and, in some cases, enacted operate on several levels simultaneously.

The first is policy sovereignty. Nations can assert their place in the global digital landscape by fostering local innovation, safeguarding digital sovereignty, bridging the digital divide, promoting local infrastructure development, and encouraging education and research — thereby harnessing the transformative power of technology while retaining control over their digital destinies. A national AI strategy should provide a high-level vision addressing workforce development, ethics, and data sovereignty, while sector-specific strategies identify tailored opportunities in key areas such as healthcare, education, energy, and agriculture.

The second is the push for culturally grounded, locally owned AI systems. AI tools can democratize access to education by providing localized, context-specific learning resources — generating curriculum materials in regional languages, integrating indigenous knowledge systems, and simulating scenarios tailored to local geographies. This requires repositioning teachers not as passive adopters but as active participants: positioning teachers as co-designers and informed decision-makers, prioritizing locally developed or curated AI systems and training programs that build critical digital capacity, and supporting educators to question whose knowledge is embedded in AI systems — and whose is omitted — are all essential to prevent AI from reinforcing marginalization.

The third is collective action at a continental scale. The African Union's Continental AI Strategy, endorsed in July 2024, commits member states to a people-centred, development-oriented approach and foregrounds education as a pillar of inclusive digital futures; in April 2025, forty-nine African countries signed the Africa Declaration on AI alongside a proposed sixty-billion-dollar Africa AI Fund. India, meanwhile, launched its IndiaAI Mission encompassing sovereign compute, model development, and AI education, and hosted the first Global South AI summit in February 2026.

None of these strategies resolves the central tension cleanly. Questions about data sovereignty, digital infrastructure disparities, ethical concerns, and the cultural appropriateness of AI tools demand scholarly inquiry and collaborative problem-solving to ensure that AI deployment in education is equitable, context-sensitive, and aligned with local priorities. The history of development assistance suggests that good intentions channeled through asymmetric power structures tend to reproduce those structures. What distinguishes the current moment is the degree to which Global South nations are insisting — with growing institutional backing — that they be architects rather than recipients of the AI educational order. Whether that insistence can be sustained against the gravitational pull of dominant platforms, training data concentrated in the Global North, and compute infrastructure controlled by a handful of corporations remains, honestly, an open question.

Which human capabilities will the labor market actually reward in 10, 20, and 50 years — and how do we teach for an unpredictable future?

## Human Capabilities, Labor Markets, and the Pedagogy of an Unpredictable Future

The question of which human capabilities will retain economic value as artificial intelligence matures is one of the most contested in contemporary social science — and one of the most consequential for anyone designing a curriculum today. Three time horizons reward different levels of confidence.

---

**The Ten-Year View (to ~2035): Augmentation, Not Replacement — Mostly**

The near-term picture is clearer than it might seem, even if its edges remain blurry. By 2030, activities accounting for up to 30 percent of hours currently worked across the U.S. economy could be automated — yet for STEM, creative, and business and legal professionals, generative AI is expected to enhance rather than eliminate their work. The disruption is already sorting occupations along a familiar fault line. Clerical and administrative roles are among the first to be automated; bank tellers face a projected 15 percent decline and cashiers an 11 percent decline by 2033. Meanwhile, healthcare roles such as nurses and therapists are projected to grow as AI augments rather than replaces them — nurse practitioners, for instance, are projected to grow by 52 percent from 2023 to 2033.

What the evidence does not support, however, is the clean narrative of "safe" versus "doomed" jobs. Research from MIT FutureTech, drawing on more than 17,000 worker evaluations of AI performance across 3,000 tasks, finds that AI capability is improving broadly and steadily rather than in sudden surges — with success rates on text-based tasks rising from roughly 50 percent in mid-2024 to 65 percent by late 2025, and potentially 80 to 95 percent on most such tasks by 2029. The implication is that the pressure is rising across the board, not concentrated in a few isolated sectors.

Even so, a striking empirical pattern has emerged. Across more than four million AI conversations analyzed, roughly 57 percent of AI usage is augmentation rather than automation — workers iterating, learning, and validating with AI assistance. AI is not replacing education; it is being used by educated people. The World Economic Forum and McKinsey both place leadership, critical thinking, resilience, and people management at the top of required skills through 2030. Eight of the ten most-requested skills in U.S. job postings are "durable" human skills; communication, leadership, critical thinking, and collaboration each appear in roughly 15 million postings annually.

---

**The Twenty-Year View (to ~2045): Structural Uncertainty and the Limits of Forecasting**

Further out, the honest answer is that professional forecasters disagree sharply. Nobel Prize–winning economist Daron Acemoglu has argued that only a small percentage of business tasks will be profitably automated, with AI contributing less than one percent productivity increase over the next decade. At the opposite pole, McKinsey has suggested that as many as half of all tasks in current jobs could be automated between 2030 and 2060, with the midpoint around 2045. This is not a minor quibble about rounding; it represents fundamentally different theories of how AI capability translates into economic reorganization.

What can be said with more confidence is structural: while current AI systems require human oversight for complex tasks, concern is growing about future systems that could cost-effectively automate a wider range of work with greater reliability and autonomy — and forecasting how such systems would affect employment is genuinely challenging. The capabilities most likely to retain a wage premium are those that require embodied trust, ethical accountability, and what might be called social legibility — the human quality of being answerable in ways a system cannot be. Leaders and professionals face situations driven by values, politics, conflicting interests, and cultural trade-offs rather than pure data; vision, trust, and ethical responsibility cannot be automated, and institutions follow people who can navigate tension and uncertainty.

There is also a subtler risk that the twenty-year horizon introduces: some analysts have noted that AI's displacement has already moved in an unexpected direction — affecting creative and technical development roles sooner than manual ones, reversing earlier predictions that blue-collar work would go first. The history of technological disruption suggests that the tasks least automatable today are not necessarily those least automatable in 2045.

---

**The Fifty-Year View (to ~2075): Genuine Opacity**

At fifty years, intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that no forecast deserves much credence. History shows that automation has led to varied effects on workers, with adjustment periods causing significant hardship before generating strong real-wage gains over the longer term. Whether that pattern holds under AI — which, unlike the loom or the spreadsheet, operates on cognitive rather than merely physical or clerical tasks — is genuinely unknown.

What the fifty-year horizon demands is not a curriculum built around predicted job titles, but one built around the capacity to keep learning. Jobs requiring uniquely human skills — creativity, critical thinking, and high emotional intelligence — will become increasingly valuable precisely because they are challenging to replicate with AI. Even roles once hyped as future-proof, such as "prompt engineer," have already lost salience; some industry leaders anticipate substantial automation of software development itself. The lesson is less about identifying a safe harbor than about cultivating navigational ability.

---

**Teaching for an Unpredictable Future**

The pedagogical implication follows directly from the diagnosis: as AI and robotics continue to evolve, education systems must reassess the competencies required for life and work in the new context — and these changes could be significant and fast. The current institutional model is not built for this. The factory model inherited from a century ago was not designed to provide the kind of learning demanded by the modern economy. In its place, educators and researchers have converged on a different organizing principle. Schools must ensure students develop what Google's leadership identified as "learning ability" — the strongest predictor of success — including the capacity to find, analyze, and use resources to answer questions, apply knowledge with judgment, evaluate their own work, and deploy advanced problem-solving skills.

Assessment must evolve to evaluate complex applications of knowledge to novel situations rather than recall of isolated facts, and instruction needs both to incorporate AI and to focus more explicitly on developing learning ability itself. At the deepest level, the question of what learning outcomes mean has been "entirely thrown up in the air," raising urgent questions about how to define meaningful learning in AI-mediated environments. The most defensible answer available is that a curriculum structured around adaptability, ethical reasoning, collaborative judgment, and the ability to operate productively alongside powerful tools — rather than in ignorance of them — offers the most robust preparation for a future that cannot yet be named.

How should education respond to the possible decoupling of learning from employment (if AI displaces large categories of work)?

**Education Beyond Employment: Learning in the Age of AI Displacement**

The organizing assumption of modern mass education — that schooling exists primarily to prepare people for paid work — has always been contested philosophically, but it has rarely been seriously destabilized in practice. AI may be the first technology capable of doing so at scale. The question of how education should respond is therefore not only pedagogical but deeply political: it forces a reckoning with what learning is actually for.

The scale of the disruption is, to be honest, still uncertain. The World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2025, surveying over a thousand employers representing fourteen million workers across fifty-five economies, projected that ninety-two million jobs will be displaced by 2030 while one hundred and seventy million new ones will be created — a net gain of seventy-eight million. Optimists lean on this arithmetic. But the aggregate figure obscures a more troubling structural problem: AI can automate discrete tasks that have traditionally served as entry-level work, reducing marginal demand for junior labor without displacing more senior workers — suggesting structural adjustment in career ladders and human-capital formation, rather than broad job loss. In other words, the rungs at the bottom of professional life are being removed even if the ladders remain standing. The scenarios institutions are least prepared for are those in which disruption is faster and deeper — where AI displaces workers across multiple sectors simultaneously at a pace that new jobs cannot absorb.

Against this backdrop, education systems are caught in the middle: struggling to prepare students for an AI-powered economy while the long-term effects of AI on learning and critical thinking are still emerging. The instinctive institutional response — teach more AI skills, retrain faster, align curricula more tightly with employer demand — is understandable but may be insufficient as a complete answer. AI accelerates skill obsolescence, redefines productivity by decoupling output from human hours, and shifts the premium from execution to judgement — which means that education organized chiefly around the transmission of executable skills will be perpetually outrun by the technology itself.

A more durable response would distinguish between two kinds of educational purpose that AI disruption has forced apart. The first is instrumental: preparing people to navigate, augment, and direct AI systems. Here the evidence points clearly toward certain priorities. The shortening longevity of skills demands greater agility from education and training systems; LinkedIn estimates that demand for AI literacy skills increased by seventy percent between 2024 and 2025. Jobs requiring critical thinking skills show greater resistance to automation, which makes the cultivation of reasoning, not the transmission of information, the core defensive task of formal education. Many experts suggest that the transition is a call to hire for the capacity to learn and adapt — and McKinsey argues that employers will need to hire for skills and competencies rather than credentials.

The second purpose is intrinsic, and here the question becomes more radical. As one educator put it at a recent National Academies workshop, "the purpose of a K-12 education is to produce a productive and well-rounded citizen — someone who has agency, can make their own choices, and has a path forward in life, not necessarily just someone who will be a good worker." If AI does reduce the volume of human labor that society requires, education cannot simply intensify its vocational mission; it must broaden its conception of human flourishing to include civic participation, creative expression, and the capacity for meaningful life outside of wage labour. Creativity, contextual reasoning, and ethical judgement are capabilities that no algorithm can fully replicate — and these are precisely the capacities that purely vocational education has historically neglected.

Structurally, the most significant shift may be temporal. In a future augmented by AI, learning should take place throughout adulthood alongside careers, providing on-ramps to new chapters — the lines between learning and work are increasingly dissolving. Higher education will be required to fundamentally rethink educational portfolios and reassess the competencies developed during initial education and lifelong training, while also monitoring the employability of older cohorts, not only recent graduates. The old model — front-load credentials in youth, deploy them for decades — cannot survive an environment where thirty-nine percent of key job skills are projected to change by 2030.

What this moment demands, then, is not merely a curriculum update but a philosophical reorientation. Education systems must hold two truths simultaneously: that practical adaptability to AI is urgently necessary, and that reducing education to that adaptability would produce people well-suited for a labour market that may be smaller than anticipated, and poorly equipped for everything else. The decoupling of learning from employment, if it comes, need not be a catastrophe — but only if education has already developed an account of itself that does not depend entirely on the economy to justify its existence.

What is the role of education in a society where economic productivity no longer requires most human labor — education for meaning rather than for jobs?

**Education for Meaning Rather Than for Jobs**

The industrial model of schooling was always, at its core, an economic instrument. It sorted children into future roles, credentialed them for employers, and measured success in terms of earning capacity. That arrangement made a kind of rough sense when human labor was the primary engine of economic output. The collision is now visible: education systems built in the industrial age are confronting technologies of an exponential age. The question of what schooling is *for* — long a philosophical afterthought — has become the most urgent practical question in education policy.

The scale of displacement is contested but substantial. Oxford Economics estimates that up to 300 million full-time jobs globally could be exposed to AI automation by 2030. More pointedly, there is a growing recognition that current institutions are still educating for a workforce defined by static roles, predictable ladders, and siloed knowledge — precisely the category of work most vulnerable to automation. The philosophical question of what gives human life meaning when economic productivity is no longer the organizing principle of society is moving from academic journals to policy agendas.

What fills the gap depends entirely on what one believes education is fundamentally for. The dominant response from policy circles remains instrumental: reskill, pivot, prepare students for the *next* economy rather than acknowledge that the economy may need less of them. Scholars have warned that continued pressure on educational systems to perform for the economy will deepen social conflicts over access and stratification, and that narrow human capital models reduce the capacity of formal education to meet the expansive challenges of a post-work landscape. The utilitarian framework, in other words, becomes self-defeating the more thoroughly automation advances.

A more durable alternative reaches back past the industrial era entirely. The Romans' *artes liberales* was a program of study intended to provide citizens with the skills free persons needed to flourish and participate actively and wisely in civic life. Liberal education, in this original sense, prioritizes the cultivation of intellectual faculties, critical reasoning, and moral discernment — and seeks to liberate the mind from narrow utility, fostering habits of inquiry and ethical judgment that enable individuals to pursue knowledge for its intrinsic value. The irony is sharp: education designed for people who did not need to work for a living may be exactly what a post-work society requires.

In the process of seeking to know what is good, such an education cultivates minds that help people lead flourishing lives of purpose and meaning, and to be better participants in shared political life. To practice liberal learning is to become equipped with habits of mind and character that allow one to construct a vision of the good life and society — a kind of education that forms students with the hearts and minds of engaged citizens, who have thought deeply with others about what it means to be a human being.

This is not mere nostalgia. The question is not whether to prepare students for an AI-shaped world, but how to do so in a way that keeps human flourishing, not just employability, at the center. Stanford's Rob Reich has highlighted the tension between using AI for automation versus augmentation, arguing that developing AI tools to mimic humans is misguided and that the focus should instead be on how AI can amplify and cultivate human intelligence. On this view, education's proper task is not to compete with machines but to develop what machines structurally cannot replicate: the capacity for ethical reasoning, aesthetic experience, civic deliberation, and the construction of a meaningful life.

The challenge lies in maintaining a sense of purpose and identity in a world where traditional job roles are diminished. Humans must rediscover purpose beyond economic productivity, finding meaning in relationships, creativity, learning, and community contribution. Whether institutions designed for credential-granting and workforce sorting can reinvent themselves as schools for living remains genuinely open. Depending on the choices made now, the future of education will be marked by expanded opportunity or deepened inequality. The material conditions — who has access to the leisure that meaningful education requires — cannot be separated from the philosophical vision. An education for meaning, divorced from serious attention to economic justice, risks becoming a privilege of the already free.

How do we manage the transition for current workers whose skills are being obsoleted faster than retraining systems can respond?

**The Retraining Gap: When Skills Obsolescence Outpaces the Systems Built to Address It**

The arithmetic of the current moment is genuinely alarming. The World Economic Forum's 2025 Future of Jobs report estimates that workers can expect two-fifths of their existing skill sets to be transformed or become outdated over the 2025–2030 period. Meanwhile, SHRM's 2025 research estimates that 19.2 million U.S. jobs face a high or very high risk of automation displacement across clerical, logistics, and finance sectors. The retraining systems expected to absorb this disruption were not built for its scale or velocity, and the evidence accumulated over decades suggests they are not becoming adequate fast enough to matter.

The public architecture of worker support reveals a structural underfunding that predates the current crisis. Federal workforce development spending has fallen from 0.35 percent of GDP in the 1980s to under 0.1 percent today. In absolute terms, the United States spends roughly five billion dollars per year on public workforce programs. The historical track record of dedicated programs such as Trade Adjustment Assistance is damning across administrations and decades: past efforts to respond to economic displacement have not lived up to the hopes of those who devised them, reflecting failures of both design and implementation, with major consequences for workers and the broader political landscape.

The private sector offers some countervailing examples, but they remain exceptional rather than systemic. AT&T committed one billion dollars to a multiyear program called Future Ready to retrain about 100,000 employees for new tech roles, partnering with online education providers and universities to create tailored courses; by 2020, tens of thousands of workers had completed training in software development, data science, and cybersecurity. Such cases demonstrate that large-scale internal retraining is technically possible — but a 2025 Brookings survey of HR leaders at companies that had deployed AI tools found that only 7 percent were working on reskilling strategies for AI-impacted roles, while 62 percent were focused on piloting AI in HR management itself. The private sector is, in other words, more likely to automate its human resources department than to retrain the workers that automation displaces.

Three structural failures recur through the research with uncomfortable consistency. The standard retraining theory assumes that for every automated job that disappears, a new skilled job emerges somewhere else — but evidence suggests technological change can reduce the number of available middle-wage positions faster than workers can retrain into them. You cannot retrain your way into a job that does not exist. Second, the people who need retraining most are often the least able to access it: retraining takes weeks or months of classes, often without income, and workers living paycheck to paycheck cannot afford to stop earning. Third, even when workers do retrain, the destination may be insecure: Harvard Kennedy School–affiliated research found that retraining raises earnings on average but produces smaller gains when workers move into occupations with high AI exposure, concluding that retraining works best when it targets roles with long-term stability rather than those vulnerable to additional automation. This is a moving target that existing programs have no mechanism to track in real time.

A further problem concerns what gets taught. The skills that AI is perceived to be most useful in helping to teach — procedural, closed skills — appear to be the same ones most susceptible to workplace automation, creating a potential "training for obsolescence" trap where educational systems inadvertently prepare workers for roles that will not exist by the time they complete their programs.

Current reform proposals cluster around three approaches, none yet proven at scale. The first is structural overhaul of public programs: workforce programs are currently evaluated on how participants are doing a year after completing their training, which creates an incentive to nudge workers into the first available job rather than what may be a better long-term alternative — a preferable approach would align program incentives with securing long-term, meaningful employment. The second concerns income continuity: a 2024 study found that wage insurance increased rates of employment and long-run earnings by cushioning wage losses and encouraging displaced workers to accept new jobs, suggesting that bridging income rather than mandating rapid retraining may paradoxically produce better outcomes. The third is distributional: some economists suggest mechanisms to share productivity gains more broadly, including profit-sharing plans that extend to all employees, or new forms of taxation where companies that benefit enormously from AI contribute to retraining funds or basic income for displaced workers.

What the evidence converges on is a need for a system that enables exposed and displaced workers to prepare for and transition to future occupations mid-career rather than only early-career — an infrastructure that does not yet exist in any country at meaningful scale. The retraining gap is not primarily a pedagogical problem. It is a political economy problem: the costs of displacement fall on individuals and communities, while the gains from automation accrue to firms and shareholders, and no durable mechanism yet redistributes between the two with sufficient speed or adequacy.

How does AI in education affect democracy — the formation of citizens capable of judgment, deliberation, and resistance to manipulation?

**AI in Education and the Democratic Citizen**

Democracy does not merely require voters; it requires people capable of forming independent judgments, tolerating disagreement, deliberating with those who hold different views, and resisting the sophisticated manipulation that modern information environments make ever easier. Whether AI in education strengthens or erodes these capacities is one of the most consequential questions of the present moment — and the honest answer is that the evidence points in genuinely opposing directions simultaneously.

Begin with the cognitive dimension. Research examining AI's broader impact on human thought identifies a "cognitive offloading effect," in which the externalization of mental functions to AI can reduce intellectual engagement and weaken critical thinking. This is not a fringe concern. A 2025 study presented at the ACM CHI conference, surveying 319 knowledge workers, found that while GenAI tools often prompt a kind of quality-checking vigilance, a key irony of automation is that by mechanizing routine tasks and leaving exception-handling to the human, it deprives the user of routine opportunities to practice their judgment and strengthen their cognitive capacities, leaving them atrophied and unprepared when harder cases arise. The classroom is not exempt from this logic. Research using Bloom's taxonomy suggests that AI assistance may be more effective for knowledge-based recall tasks than for the higher-order thinking skills that require analysis, synthesis, and evaluation — precisely the skills democratic deliberation demands.

The personalization problem compounds this. Critics of high-level AI personalization argue that over-tailored content may inadvertently create "filter bubbles" or "knowledge silos," where students are shielded from the productive struggle necessary for deep conceptual change. Just as social media algorithms can narrow our worldview by feeding us only opinions we already agree with, hyper-optimized AI tutors might be stunting intellectual resilience by removing the vital struggle from the learning process. The connection to democratic life is direct: on the social level, algorithmic personalization creates filter bubbles that limit the diversity of opinions and can lead to the homogenization of thought and polarization. A student raised inside a perfectly tailored learning environment may develop competence within a narrow range while losing the habit — and appetite — for encountering genuinely alien ideas.

Today's learners are digital citizens first, encountering AI in their social media feeds, search engines, and recommendation systems; democratic competence must therefore include a critical understanding of AI itself. Learners need to recognize how AI shapes their exposure to information, how biases can be embedded in systems, and how automated tools can empower or constrain civic engagement. This is a relatively new but serious demand: the citizen must now be able to read not just texts but the architectures that select those texts.

Yet the picture is not only one of erosion. Civic educators have argued that AI could move students beyond merely learning a set of facts into an "opportunity to practice the skills of negotiation, the skills of engagement, the skills of give-and-take" essential to democratic life. AI-powered platforms can simulate scenarios involving misinformation, enabling learners to distinguish between credible and misleading content — a skill whose civic importance is self-evident. And research on teaching AI literacy directly to adolescents shows real promise: because adolescents' nascent digital footprints make them particularly susceptible to misrepresentation by AI systems, educational modules on algorithmic bias and filter bubbles have produced substantial learning gains in early trials.

The condition on which the optimistic case depends is, however, demanding. The goal of genuine civic co-intelligence in education is achieved when teachers and students co-create a culture of inquiry, skepticism, and ethical responsibility around AI use — ensuring that AI is a catalyst for deeper thinking and democratic citizenship, not a substitute for creativity, empathy, or human judgment. There is evidence that teacher care and relational engagement are fundamental to student character development and well-being, raising real questions about whether AI-mediated education can replicate the affective and relational dimensions essential for authentic moral growth.

The deepest worry, then, is not that AI will teach students wrong answers. It is that AI — if deployed without pedagogical intention — will teach students to outsource the hardest parts of thinking: sitting with uncertainty, weighing competing claims, arguing a position across genuine disagreement. Those are not skills that any algorithm can practice on a student's behalf. They require friction, and friction is precisely what the most commercially successful AI tools are designed to remove.

What shared human culture and values should education transmit when AI fragments information into personalized realities?

## The Epistemic Commons: What Education Must Preserve When AI Splinters Reality

The deepest threat posed by AI-driven personalization is not misinformation in the conventional sense — it is the dissolution of a shared world. At a societal level, if everyone interacts with differently personalized systems calibrated to their particular preferences and beliefs, shared reality and common knowledge may erode. This is the civilizational wager now sitting, largely unexamined, at the heart of educational policy. The question is not only what children should learn, but what they must learn *in common* — and why that commonality itself is a moral and political good.

The problem is more layered than early warnings about filter bubbles suggested. In increasingly personalized media environments, individuals encounter information that aligns with their existing beliefs, raising concerns about polarization, intergroup hostility, and the erosion of shared political reality — and filter bubbles emerge not from algorithms alone, but through the recursive interaction of motivated cognitive processing, identity-based social networks, and algorithmic amplification. AI does not simply distort the information landscape from the outside; it reshapes the internal habits by which people seek, weigh, and revise knowledge. AI systems impact not only beliefs, but belief revision itself — and in the classroom, this implies students will update their knowledge in response to algorithmic authority, without recourse to further justification or reflection.

This is where the educational stakes become philosophical rather than merely technical. The values underpinning current forms of educational AI — individualism, mastery-based learning, automation of teaching — work directly against many of the core elements of how democratic education has been understood by educationalists such as John Dewey over the past century. Personalized learning optimizes the individual learner's trajectory through content; democratic education, by contrast, has always been partly about the friction of encountering minds unlike one's own, negotiating disagreement, and building the civic capacity to inhabit a world held in common. The current push for educational AI is resulting in technologies that diminish students' opportunities for communication and collaboration with others.

What, then, should education transmit? Three clusters of value emerge from current scholarship as non-negotiable, regardless of what AI systems can deliver efficiently.

The first is *epistemic agency* — the cultivated capacity to interrogate sources, tolerate uncertainty, and resist the seduction of algorithmic fluency. AI tools that prioritize speed and convenience risk amplifying automation bias, reducing cognitive struggle, and weakening critical thinking; to counteract this, AI must preserve cognitive friction — prompting retrieval, offering delayed feedback, framing uncertainty as opportunity — so that it fosters intellectual resilience and the critical thinking skills vital for democratic society. The transmission of this value requires teachers who model doubt, not just delivery.

The second is *shared factual ground* — a curriculum that insists certain things are true, contested, or false independent of any individual's preference profile. Through algorithmic filtering and personalized content, individuals are nudged toward specific viewpoints without awareness, limiting exposure to diverse information and weakening their capacity for independent thought — and the resulting manipulation diminishes individuals' ability to make informed choices, undermining democratic participation. Education's answer must be a commitment to evidence, method, and the disciplines that have developed collectively-validated ways of knowing: scientific reasoning, historical analysis, literary interpretation, mathematical proof.

The third is *solidarity through encounter* — the experience of being educated alongside people whose lives, backgrounds, and conclusions differ from one's own. Education for democracy is a holistic and participatory approach that fosters the knowledge, skills, values, and attitudes needed for active and responsible citizenship, going beyond textbooks by emphasising experiential and inclusive learning that strengthens democratic competencies such as critical thinking, dialogue, agency, and meaningful participation. No AI tutor, however sophisticated, replicates the moral education that occurs when a classroom must reach understanding across genuine disagreement.

Ultimately, an ethical use of AI in education is about preserving education as a space for autonomy, critical inquiry, and democratic formation — because if AI systems undermine these values, they risk weakening not just learning outcomes, but the social institutions that depend on educated, reflective, and empowered citizens. The curriculum of the future will need to be deliberately, even stubbornly, common: not because uniformity is a virtue, but because a democracy requires citizens who can, at minimum, argue about the same world.

Looking back from 2050: what decisions made in this decade about AI and education will we most regret or celebrate — and how do we choose wisely now?

**Looking Back from 2050: The Decisions That Shaped a Generation**

History rarely announces the moments that will matter most. The decisions now being made about artificial intelligence and education — rushed, improvised, and only partially understood — are almost certainly among them. From the vantage point of 2050, this decade will likely look like an inflection point comparable to the introduction of mass printing or compulsory schooling: a period when the architecture of human learning was renegotiated under enormous pressure, with consequences that outlasted everyone who made the choices.

The first decisions likely to be judged harshly concern the heedless substitution of AI for cognitive effort. The share of students in middle school grades and up who reported using AI for help with their homework rose from 48 percent in May 2025 to 62 percent in December of the same year. That trajectory, if it continues unchecked, points toward a generation that has never had to sustain the productive discomfort of not knowing. The research already accumulating is sobering: overreliance on AI tools can lead to a decline in critical and analytical thinking skills, especially when students become overly dependent on AI-generated content, with over-reliance linked to diminished creativity and increased dependency. More specifically, the most significant improvements from AI assistance appear at lower levels of cognitive taxonomy, suggesting that AI may be more effective for knowledge-based recall tasks than for higher-order thinking skills that require analysis, synthesis, and evaluation. The 2026 International AI Safety Report identified the deeper stakes plainly: children's early interactions with AI systems may influence how their key skills and habits develop over time, and it can be difficult to assess whether observed changes in behavior represent short-term adjustments or more persistent shifts that could affect autonomy.

What makes this particularly consequential is that students themselves already sense the danger. As of December 2025, 67 percent of students endorsed the view that the more students use AI for schoolwork, the more it will harm their critical thinking skills — up more than 10 percentage points from ten months earlier. A society whose children recognize a trap they feel unable to avoid is one that has failed at the level of institutional design, not individual virtue.

The second class of decisions likely to haunt the future involves equity. Unequal access to AI is widening the educational gap between those who are privileged and those who are underprivileged. The emerging geography of this divide is already being mapped. In tech-rich cities, students use AI to think critically and solve problems with expert guidance ensuring they learn, not just use; while elsewhere, with good connectivity but little oversight, students offload work to AI, gaining convenience but missing real learning. Meanwhile, fragmented adoption driven by decentralized decision-making is leading to inequities, with some districts leveraging AI effectively and others struggling to integrate it. The cruel irony is that the same technology capable of acting as a private tutor for every child — a democratizing force of enormous potential — is reproducing and amplifying the very hierarchies it might have dissolved. Without adequate safeguards, the same systems that promise to close learning gaps may reinforce them.

The third set of decisions involves governance, and here the picture is more ambiguous. By April 2025, at least 28 states had published guidance on AI in K-12 settings — an encouraging sign of institutional attention, though guidance documents are not the same as thoughtful policy. Organisational and governmental incentives to deploy AI systems quickly can limit opportunities to evaluate their effects carefully and to implement appropriate safeguards. The pressure to adopt — from vendors, from parents worried their children will fall behind, from administrators seduced by efficiency — is structurally different from the pressure to reflect.

What, then, does choosing wisely look like? The answer is not a moratorium on AI in education, which would be neither desirable nor achievable. It looks, rather, like distinguishing between AI as a tool for amplifying what teachers do and AI as a replacement for the cognitive struggle that is the mechanism of learning itself. Assignments built on assumptions of no AI use are increasingly disconnected from how students actually work — but that does not mean the answer is to simply surrender the design of learning to the convenience of the tool. When students externalize cognitive tasks to AI systems, it reduces internal cognitive demands but may compromise deeper cognitive processes such as inference, synthesis, and evaluation; over time, habitual offloading may foster cognitive laziness and impede the development of higher-order thinking skills.

The decisions that 2050 will celebrate are likely to be those made now with deliberate restraint: institutions that asked not "what can AI do in our classrooms?" but "what must humans still do for themselves, and why?" The regrets will belong to those who moved fast and assumed that what was efficient was therefore good.

Chapter 7. The Evidence Base — What Research Actually Shows

What does current peer-reviewed evidence show about the effects of AI tutoring systems on learning outcomes, and how strong is that evidence?

**AI Tutoring Systems and Learning Outcomes: What the Evidence Shows**

The question of whether AI tutoring systems genuinely improve learning is one of the most consequential in contemporary education research — and the honest answer, as of mid-2026, is that the evidence is promising but still maturing, with its quality varying considerably depending on the generation of technology being evaluated.

The older, pre-generative-AI strand of evidence is comparatively well-established. A meta-analysis of 50 controlled evaluations of intelligent tutoring systems found a median effect size of 0.66 standard deviations over conventional instruction — equivalent to moving a student from the 50th to the 75th percentile. Positive effect sizes were found at all levels of education and in nearly all subject domains evaluated, and the pattern held even after accounting for potential publication bias. These are meaningful numbers by educational research standards, where effect sizes above 0.4 are generally considered substantive. However, the magnitude of improvement depended heavily on whether outcomes were measured using locally developed or standardized tests, suggesting that alignment between test content and instructional objectives is a critical determinant of results.

The more recent wave of research concerns generative AI — systems built on large language models rather than rule-based logic — and here the evidence base is thinner but expanding rapidly. Many claims about the educational benefits of generative AI have outpaced high-quality evidence, but a number of recent rigorous studies suggest that tutoring systems integrating generative AI can deliver learning gains and offer a path to individualized education at scale. A 2025 randomized controlled trial published in *Scientific Reports* by researchers at Harvard is among the most cited: the study measured college students' learning when content was delivered through an AI-powered tutor compared with an active-learning class, finding that students learned significantly more in less time with the AI tutor, and also reported feeling more engaged and motivated. A separate exploratory RCT in UK secondary school classrooms found that an AI tutor integrated into a mathematics platform and supervised by human tutors produced a 5.5 percentage-point increase in students' likelihood of correctly answering novel questions.

Yet the picture is not uniformly positive, and a crucial caveat emerges from a large-scale field experiment published in *PNAS*. That study examined GPT-4's impact on student learning in mathematics and found that although AI-based tutoring improved performance during practice sessions, students who relied on the technology tended to underperform when AI access was subsequently removed — indicating reduced independent skill acquisition. The remedy, the researchers found, was deliberate design: carefully designed safeguards, especially asking the AI tutor to provide teacher-designed hints rather than giving away answers, can mitigate these negative effects. This finding is echoed in the broader literature: there are studies indicating that over-reliance on AI hints can worsen outcomes, and a systematic review by Zhai et al. (2024) associated regular use of AI dialogue and tutoring systems with a decline in students' cognitive abilities and an increased tendency for cognitive offloading.

The K-12 systematic review published in *npj Science of Learning* in 2025 provides a useful summary of where the evidence currently stands for younger learners: the effects of intelligent tutoring systems on learning and performance in K-12 education are generally positive but are mitigated when compared to non-intelligent tutoring systems, and additional research with longer interventions and more diverse samples is warranted. The methodological problems are not trivial: empirical evidence remains scarce, and most published evaluations rely on user satisfaction metrics or short-duration laboratory tasks rather than rigorous field experimentation with validated learning outcome measures.

Two conclusions emerge from the current body of work. First, well-designed AI tutoring systems — particularly those that adapt to the learner, provide formative feedback, and resist simply handing over answers — can produce real academic gains, especially in structured domains like mathematics and science. Second, design choices matter enormously: safeguards that constrain how the AI assists are not merely a safety feature but a pedagogical necessity, required to ensure that human learning is actually preserved. The gap between a well-designed AI tutor and an unconstrained language model is not cosmetic — it may determine whether the technology helps students learn or merely helps them complete tasks.

What does research reveal about how students actually use generative AI when unsupervised — as distinct from how they report using it?

The gap between what students say they do with generative AI and what they actually do — when unobserved and unsurveyed — has become one of the more revealing and methodologically vexed problems in contemporary education research. The evidence accumulated by 2025 and 2026 cuts in several directions at once, but certain patterns are now firm enough to hold.

The headline numbers on self-reported usage are staggering on their own terms. Based on a survey of over one thousand UK undergraduates, the Higher Education Policy Institute found that the proportion of students using generative AI tools such as ChatGPT for assessments jumped from 53 percent in 2024 to 88 percent in 2025. By 2026, AI use had become nearly universal, with 95 percent of students reporting using it in at least one way and 94 percent saying they use it to help with assessed work. The Copyleaks 2025 report broadly corroborates this trajectory: nearly 90 percent of students have used AI for academic purposes, with almost a third doing so daily and another 24 percent doing so several times per week.

These are self-reported figures, however, and the distance between what students declare and what they do in practice is precisely where the more interesting research lies. The most direct evidence of systematic underreporting comes from studies using list experiments — an anonymization technique that allows respondents to admit socially stigmatized behaviors without direct attribution. A study based on interviews and list experiments with 305 Indonesian undergraduates found that students were often reluctant to admit dishonest behavior when asked directly, but the anonymous list experiment method produced substantially higher admissions of AI-assisted cheating. A comparable quantitative study of 1,386 Vietnamese undergraduates using list experiments found a similar pattern: students tended to conceal AI-assisted cheating when asked directly. The mechanism, across both contexts, appears to be shame and social pressure rather than simple ignorance of the rules.

A further dimension of the gap concerns not misconduct per se, but covert use more broadly. Qualitative research from 2026, drawing on interviews with college students, found that there is a growing misalignment between students' AI adoption and existing institutional practices — around assessment, policy, and limited AI resources — and that this misalignment has led to covert AI usage, increased student anxiety, and an intention-behavior gap where students feel pressured into disregulated AI engagement. The title of that study captures the phenomenology with precision: "Everyone's using it, but no one is allowed to talk about it." Its results show that traditional assessment structures — characterized by tight deadlines, exam stacking, and graded homework — incentivize students to use AI for task completion rather than deep engagement. In other words, institutional design is actively producing the covert behavior it nominally prohibits.

What students actually use AI *for*, when conditions allow honesty, also diverges from the catastrophizing narratives common in faculty discourse. The most common reported uses are for AI to explain concepts, summarize articles, and suggest research ideas — functions closer to an advanced reference tool than a ghost-writing service. The proportion of students directly including AI-generated text in assessed work, while rising, stands at 12 percent as of 2026, up from 8 percent in 2025 and 3 percent in 2024. This matters: even as near-universal engagement is reported, wholesale text submission remains a minority behavior. The more common, less dramatic reality is iterative assistance at earlier stages of work.

What distorts disclosure, research suggests, is not chiefly moral recklessness but institutional ambiguity. Roughly one third of respondents in one study admitted some AI use, yet disclosure was rare — and this silence appeared to reflect not dishonesty but rational caution in the face of ambiguous policies and perceived academic risk. Contrary to what institutional leaders might assume, mere awareness of a university's AI policy does little to sway student views on whether using AI is ethical; formal guidance often trails behind the informal norms students create for themselves.

The picture that emerges is thus more complicated than either the "students are cheating en masse" or "students are using AI responsibly" framings allow. Students are using generative AI far more extensively than they disclose, particularly in high-stakes assessment contexts. That concealment is shaped less by calculated dishonesty than by a policy environment that criminalizes a technology students experience as ordinary, and by assessment structures that reward speed over depth. The behavior gap, in this sense, is partly an institutional creation.

What is currently known from cognitive science about AI's effects on memory formation, retention, and transfer of learning?

The picture that emerges from recent cognitive science is genuinely complicated: AI is neither a straightforward cognitive prosthetic nor a simple threat to the mind. It appears to be both, depending on how it is used.

**The Performance Paradox**

A growing body of empirical research points to what researchers call a "performance paradox": AI can boost a student's performance on an immediate task while simultaneously diminishing the durable learning that is the goal of education. The paradox is rooted in a well-established principle of memory science — that difficulty and effort during learning are not obstacles to retention but prerequisites for it. Learners often do not appreciate the beneficial effects of so-called "desirably difficult" tasks that are experienced as effortful but have a positive effect on learning results and knowledge transfer. Retrieval practice, for instance, is significantly more effective than simply re-reading material, yet self-testing requires greater cognitive effort. AI systems that supply answers on demand routinely short-circuit this mechanism.

**Cognitive Offloading and Its Costs**

The central concept organizing much of this research is *cognitive offloading* — the utilization of external aids to achieve cognitive tasks. The phenomenon predates AI: Sparrow, Liu, and Wegner documented a "Google effect" in 2011, in which people began storing information externally rather than internally. This leads to faster forgetting and weaker recall. Generative AI intensifies the pattern considerably. Reliance on tools such as ChatGPT for information retrieval or task completion has been shown to reduce memory retention and deep encoding, as users engage in passive consumption rather than active rehearsal and consolidation. Beyond declarative memory, AI-assisted technologies are linked to declines in spelling, handwriting, arithmetic, and language learning, as automation reduces the cognitive effort required for these memory-reinforcing skills.

A striking piece of neural evidence comes from a laboratory study by Kosmyna and colleagues. They examined the neural and behavioral consequences of LLM-assisted essay writing, dividing participants into three groups: LLM, search engine, and brain-only. Cognitive activity decreased when participants relied on external tools, and self-reported ownership of the essays was lowest in the LLM group and highest in the brain-only group. At the mechanistic level, the authors describe this pattern as the accrual of "cognitive debt."

**Who Is Most Vulnerable**

Empirical surveys find a significant negative correlation between frequent AI tool usage and critical thinking abilities, mediated by increased cognitive offloading — and younger participants exhibit higher dependence on AI tools and lower critical thinking scores compared to older participants. This generational gradient is concerning precisely because the populations most immersed in AI assistance are those in the formative stages of building durable knowledge structures.

**The Genuine Upside: When AI Aligns With Memory Science**

The picture is not uniformly negative. A meta-analysis of 51 experimental studies found that AI has a large positive effect on improving learning performance, and a separate experiment found that students learn significantly more in less time when using an AI tutor, compared with in-class active learning. The key appears to be whether AI is designed to work *with* established principles of memory science rather than against them. Encoding is strengthened when AI systems provide multimodal, attention-capturing stimuli; working memory is supported when designs manage cognitive load; and long-term retention is optimized when tools incorporate retrieval practice and deep processing. Adaptive learning systems that use machine learning to personalize review plans, optimize retrieval difficulty, and improve learning efficiency show particular promise, though practical challenges remain, including initial cognitive load and personalized calibration.

**Transfer of Learning: The Hardest Problem**

Transfer — the ability to apply knowledge flexibly in new contexts — remains the most elusive and understudied dimension. Bypassing cognitive effort impairs knowledge and skill acquisition and contributes to deskilling. More pointedly, studies commonly show that knowledge workers and students perform worse, compared to those who never used AI, when they lose access to these tools. This dependency effect suggests that AI may be producing performance gains that are inseparable from the tool itself — not genuine transfer of internalized understanding. Current integrations tend to privilege efficiency over learners' epistemic agency, normalizing quick answer-taking rather than verification, sense-making, and productive struggle.

The honest summary of the current state of knowledge is this: AI can enhance certain narrow dimensions of learning when deliberately engineered around cognitive science principles, but its default mode of use — frictionless, immediate, effortless — runs directly counter to the conditions under which the human brain forms durable memories and transfers knowledge across contexts. The field does not yet have longitudinal data sufficient to measure the cumulative cost.

What does the evidence show about AI's impact on writing skill development, from early literacy through academic and professional writing?

The evidence on AI's impact across the full arc of writing development — from early literacy through professional practice — tells a genuinely complicated story, one that resists both utopian and dystopian simplifications. The clearest finding is that the technology's effects depend heavily on *how* it is used, at what developmental stage, and by whom.

**Early Literacy and Young Learners**

Among younger children, the picture is more cautious than celebratory. Children aged 8 to 13 more frequently reported that generative AI helped them with ideas and with learning new things compared with older age groups, suggesting some genuine utility in sparking engagement. Yet the behavioral patterns are worrying: one in four young people admitted to simply copying AI outputs when using it for homework — an increase from one in five the previous year — a trend that was more pronounced in younger teens and those who were already less engaged with reading and writing. The children who arguably need to develop compositional habits most urgently are the most likely to let the machine do the composing for them.

**Academic Writing at the Secondary and University Level**

The research literature on secondary and higher education is large and growing rapidly, with published studies rising steeply from 2022 onward and reaching 13 studies by 2024 — a volume still too modest to support confident meta-analytic conclusions. Within that body of work, surface-level benefits are consistent and reproducible: AI-based software improves vocabulary and grammar at the surface level, while having no demonstrable impact on deeper-level skills like coherence, arrangement, and formatting of text. This asymmetry matters enormously. Grammar and mechanics are the easiest dimension of writing to assess and the easiest for AI to fix; argument, structure, and voice are what actually distinguish accomplished writing, and those appear largely unaffected by automated assistance.

There is nonetheless genuine evidence that AI feedback, used thoughtfully, can support metacognitive development. In some studies, feedback via generative chat enables reflective and metacognitive processes essential to writing — allowing students to evaluate their errors and make deliberate adjustments. The critical qualifier, though, is that these benefits accrue to students who engage critically with the feedback, not to those who accept it passively. Darvishi et al. (2024) found that students tended to rely on AI assistance rather than learn from it; when AI support was removed, self-regulation strategies only partially compensated, and outcomes lagged behind AI-assisted performance. That dependency pattern — improvement that evaporates without the scaffold — is perhaps the most consequential finding in the field.

The interaction with motivation and prior engagement is also striking. More young people who enjoyed reading or writing added their own thoughts to AI outputs, while those who did not enjoy these activities were more likely to copy AI responses wholesale. AI, in other words, tends to amplify existing orientations rather than correct them: enthusiastic writers use it experimentally; disengaged writers use it as a substitute.

**Professional Writing**

At the professional level, the documented gains in productivity are real. A randomized controlled trial by Noy and Zhang (2023) found that workers given ChatGPT assistance completed writing tasks 37% faster with 18% higher quality ratings. But speed and rated quality in a single task are not the same as the sustained development of writing craft. Research on AI-assisted science writing documents a shift in which scientists use AI tools for ideation and structural scaffolding while transitioning their primary cognitive effort toward evaluation and selection rather than composition. Whether that shift constitutes a loss or a reasonable reallocation of cognitive labor remains genuinely contested.

**The Underlying Tension**

The honest summary is that AI writing tools demonstrably help with the measurable, correctable, and mechanical aspects of writing while leaving the deeper skills — voice, argumentation, creative risk-taking, structural judgment — largely untouched, and potentially at risk from passive use. Findings from content analysis indicate that while AI tools enhance writing mechanics, over-reliance may hinder creativity and independent editing abilities. Notably, even students are not oblivious to this: two in three young people surveyed agreed that "even when we have AI, it is important to learn how to write." The challenge ahead is not whether students understand the value of writing skill but whether the path of least resistance — handing the task to the machine — will quietly erode the practice that builds it.

What have studies found about AI feedback compared with human feedback — in accuracy, usefulness, and student receptivity?

## AI Feedback vs. Human Feedback: What the Research Shows

The comparison between AI-generated and human feedback has become one of the most active areas of educational research, producing a body of evidence that is genuinely mixed — usefully so, since the mixture itself reveals something important about what feedback is actually for.

**Accuracy**

On the question of scoring accuracy, AI systems have closed much of the gap with human raters. Systems such as e-rater and ChatGPT-3.5 have demonstrated the potential to match human evaluators in grading accuracy, with deep learning models achieving quadratic weighted kappa scores of 0.78, closely approaching human rater agreement levels of 0.72. Yet this statistical parity conceals important qualitative differences. When comprehensive correct answers and domain knowledge are provided, large language models produce concrete and factually correct feedback on structured tasks, but for longer essays, studies have documented recurring hallucinations. The problem is especially acute in knowledge-intensive writing, where AI must evaluate nuanced conceptual claims rather than surface-level grammar or argument structure. Proficient students frequently find AI-generated feedback lacking in personalization when addressing higher-order concerns such as theme development or rhetorical strength, and advanced learners question the system's ability to evaluate subjectively rich content effectively.

**Usefulness and Learning Outcomes**

At the aggregate level, the headline finding from recent meta-analyses is one of rough equivalence — and then important caveats beneath it. A meta-analysis drawing on 41 studies involving 4,813 students found no statistically significant differences in learning performance between students who received AI-generated feedback and those who received human-provided feedback. A separate meta-analysis of 40 studies found that AI-supported personalized feedback has a moderate effect on learning outcomes (g = 0.58) and a strong effect on learning motivation (g = 0.82). These are not trivial numbers. Yet the aggregate obscures an important asymmetry in what each feedback source does well. In a controlled three-way comparison, students receiving teacher feedback indicated less willingness to revise their work, yet teacher feedback produced the strongest improvements in scientific argumentation and formal writing quality, while LLM feedback yielded the smallest improvement overall. A consistent finding across studies of writing is that automated written feedback can positively impact learners' writing, but it is not as effective as feedback provided by humans such as teachers and peers. One revealing distinction concerns the type of dialogue feedback enables: teachers provided question-based feedback that encouraged students to think critically, while generative AI chatbots did not use this type of feedback at all — suggesting that human feedback stimulates a different kind of cognitive engagement.

**Student Receptivity**

Student attitudes toward AI feedback are more complicated than a simple preference for the human touch. The dominant pattern across studies is what researchers call "algorithm aversion" — students show a clear preference for human feedback over AI feedback, revealing a bias against machine-generated evaluations. This bias appears to be partly psychological rather than purely evidence-based. A striking experiment gave students identical AI-generated feedback but labeled it differently: students perceived the feedback more favorably when it was attributed to an experienced teacher, yet when informed it came from an AI system, they were paradoxically more likely to act upon it, making a greater number of revisions. In other words, students trust humans more but may respond more readily to machines — a dissociation that has real implications for how feedback is deployed.

Receptivity also varies substantially by student profile. Older and more proficient writers frequently express skepticism about AI feedback, citing inaccuracies and a preference for the nuance and individual tailoring they associate with human instructors. Less experienced writers tend to be more accepting. Some studies also find that when AI feedback is well-designed — clear, specific, and structured — students respond positively on certain dimensions: AI feedback has received significantly higher ratings for specificity, clarity, and simplicity, with no significant difference observed for correctness, suggesting that learners perceive the factual accuracy of AI and human feedback to be similar.

**What the Evidence Suggests Overall**

The research does not support either the optimistic claim that AI feedback is simply equivalent to expert human feedback or the dismissive claim that it is a poor substitute. The honest summary is that AI and teacher feedback appear to serve different needs, and are therefore complementary but not interchangeable. AI systems excel at speed, scale, consistency, and certain surface-level dimensions of clarity. Human feedback retains a clear advantage in higher-order reasoning, contextual sensitivity, and the kind of questioning that promotes genuine intellectual development. The most promising line of current research explores not which is better, but how each can reinforce the other — a question the field has only begun to answer rigorously.

What does research show about the reliability of AI-detection tools, and what have institutions learned from relying on them?

**The Unreliable Gatekeepers: What Research Shows About AI-Detection Tools**

The promise of AI-detection software was intuitive: if machines generate text according to statistical patterns, other machines ought to recognize those patterns. The evidence accumulated since that premise was first acted upon tells a considerably more complicated story, and institutions that placed their disciplinary authority in these tools have, in many cases, been forced to reckon with the consequences.

The fundamental technical problem is that detection accuracy is not a fixed property of any tool. AI detectors are neither accurate nor consistent, because the underlying generative models evolve continuously, and a detection tool can only hope to remain reliable if it evolves at the same pace. Assessments of the field's leading products reveal a wide and unsettling spread: empirical assessments of fourteen detectors have reported accuracies ranging from merely 33% to 81%. Even on its own terms, Turnitin — often cited as among the more reliable commercial offerings — fares poorly under independent scrutiny: a Temple University evaluation found Turnitin's detector only 77% accurate at spotting AI text, with a 7% mis-flag rate for genuine human writing.

The vulnerability to evasion compounds the accuracy problem. Paraphrasing or manual manipulation of AI-generated text — introducing spelling errors, adjusting sentence structure, or altering vocabulary — can significantly reduce the effectiveness of detection tools, often leading to a substantial drop in accuracy. A 2025 study found that adversarial paraphrasing attacks reduce AI detection rates by an average of 87.88% across all major detector types. As newer models produce more fluent and contextually coherent prose, detection rates are measurably lower for newer models, with research confirming that detectors were significantly more accurate on GPT-3.5 than on GPT-4 output. A 2025 paper published in *Nature* assessed AI detection on peer review reports and found it "almost impossible to know" whether a peer review has been generated by AI using current detection tools.

The most consequential flaw, however, is not what detectors miss but what they wrongly condemn. Multiple studies have shown that AI detection tools frequently misclassify non-native English writing as AI-generated, raising serious concerns about reliability and equity. More recent research showed detection accuracy for non-native English contributions plummeting to 67%, with false positive rates soaring to 28% — dramatically higher than for native speakers. The practical arithmetic of false positives at institutional scale is alarming: while a false positive rate of 1–2% might seem low, the scale of educational assessment means it could translate into a substantial number of false accusations — at an institution processing 480,000 assessments per year, even a 1% rate would generate approximately 4,800 false positives annually.

Institutions have begun drawing conclusions. Universities including Waterloo, Yale, Johns Hopkins, and Vanderbilt have dropped or limited AI detection tools. At Waterloo, the provost stated that the false positive rate was "unacceptable for a tool being used to make consequential academic integrity decisions," with internal testing reportedly showing that non-native English speakers were flagged at nearly three times the rate of native speakers; the university still uses Turnitin for traditional plagiarism detection but has turned off its AI detection overlay. Universities are increasingly aware that basing academic misconduct charges on unreliable technology exposes them to legal challenges, and at least two lawsuits related to AI detection false positives were filed against U.S. universities in 2025.

The broader lesson is not simply that the tools are imperfect — all tools are — but that they were deployed in high-stakes disciplinary contexts before their error rates were honestly understood. Even some of the companies developing detection tools openly acknowledge that they are not 100% accurate and advise caution when deploying them in high-stakes settings such as grading or enforcing academic integrity policies. While AI detectors offer scalable solutions, they frequently produce false positives and lack transparency, especially for multilingual or non-native English speakers, with ethical concerns surrounding surveillance, consent, and fairness compounded by gaps in institutional policies, inconsistent enforcement, and limited faculty training. The technology and the governance structures surrounding it are still, evidently, running to catch up with each other.

What evidence exists on how AI use affects different learner profiles — high vs. low performers, younger vs. older students, novices vs. experts?

## AI and Differential Learner Profiles: Who Benefits, Who Doesn't, and Why

The question of whether AI tools help all students equally has attracted serious empirical attention over the past two years, and the emerging answer is clearly no. The effects of AI on learning vary significantly across performance levels, age groups, and prior expertise — and the patterns are more complex, and more cautionary in places, than the technology's advocates tend to acknowledge.

### High vs. Low Performers

One of the most consequential findings concerns the divergence between high and low performers. The promise of AI tutoring is that it can rescue students from classes "pitched to the median" in which high-achieving learners are insufficiently challenged while struggling students fall behind. There is genuine evidence for this potential: lower-performing students, specifically those one standard deviation below the mean, showed meaningful improvements — an additional 0.15 SD gain on state tests — when human-AI hybrid tutoring was compared to AI-only tutoring. Yet the same research found a troubling wrinkle: lower-performing students showed lower AI tutor use than their higher-performing peers, suggesting that the students most in need of the tool are also the least likely to engage with it consistently.

Meanwhile, studies on language learning point in a similar direction. In a study of English language learning in Nigeria, those who gained the most benefit from generative AI access were higher academic achievers, implying that AI may amplify, rather than reduce, existing performance gaps. In writing contexts, AI's impact varies by students' independent writing competence, with lower-proficiency students potentially struggling to leverage AI effectively. The tool, in other words, rewards those who already bring enough prior skill to use it well.

Design matters enormously here. In a randomized controlled trial with roughly 1,000 high school mathematics students, AI-assisted performance improved by 48%, but unassisted exam performance fell by 17% relative to control — a stark reversal suggesting that when students use AI as an answer machine rather than a thinking partner, short-term task performance comes at the cost of durable learning. Research by Bastani et al. indicated that although generative AI instantly enhanced students' problem-solving performance, these benefits diminished or became negative by final examinations, with participants' end-of-term grades declining when AI was available without pedagogical guardrails.

A counterpoint comes from research on AI-assisted human tutors. Students whose tutors used AI assistance were 4 percentage points more likely to progress through math tutoring assessments successfully, with the approach particularly benefiting lower-rated and less-experienced tutors — students of lower-rated tutors who used AI assistance increased their math proficiency by up to 9 percentage points on average. The implication is that AI's greatest leveling potential may lie not in replacing human instruction but in elevating the quality of weaker instructors.

### Novices vs. Experts

The novice-expert divide reveals a distinct cognitive mechanism. Research highlights systematic differences between novices and experts when collaborating with AI for programming: in agent-based modeling, experts could benefit more than novices, relying on stronger skills in task decomposition and evaluation of AI outputs; in code comprehension, experts inspect data more deeply, verify assumptions, and navigate outputs with clear goals — behaviors that novices underuse. Novices, lacking the mental scaffolding to evaluate AI outputs critically, risk being led astray rather than guided forward. Studies also document failure patterns such as "prompting rabbit holes," where less-experienced users iterate on prompts without convergence.

The underlying danger for novices is well captured by the metacognitive dimension: while a ChatGPT-assisted group showed improved task performance during the supported intervention phase, there were no differences in posttest knowledge gain or knowledge transfer, and a temporal process analysis suggested that the ChatGPT group relied strongly on AI support and showed relatively low metacognitive processing compared to other support groups. For novice learners specifically, the negative impact of large language models on the depth of inquiry was found to be "particularly true for novices." Programming research extends this concern: a novice programmer who relies on a tool like GitHub Copilot may learn how to prompt the AI but not how to think like a programmer.

That said, the novice case is not uniformly bleak. Using AI code generators can significantly increase task completion, improve correctness scores, reduce errors, and reduce task completion time for novice programmers during training. And crucially, learners with higher prior programming competency performed significantly better on retention tests if they had access to the AI code generator during their training phase — a finding that once again underscores how prior knowledge moderates the benefit.

### Younger vs. Older Students

Age-based differences in AI use are well documented, though research on differential *learning outcomes* by age remains thinner than research on adoption patterns. A higher percentage of older teens (16 to 18) than younger teens (13 to 16) used AI to summarize texts (45.5% vs. 31.5%) or to provide different interpretations of a text (19.1% vs. 10.1%), suggesting that older adolescents are both more aware of and more sophisticated in their AI use. Adoption within schools also scales with grade: nearly a quarter of students in grades 11 and 12 (24%) reported using ChatGPT, compared to 17% in grades 9 and 10 and 12% in grades 7 and 8.

Critically, engagement quality diverges as much as usage rates. More young people who enjoyed reading or writing added their own thoughts to AI outputs, while those who did not enjoy these activities were more likely to copy AI responses directly — a gap that was especially pronounced around reading enjoyment. This suggests that motivation and existing affinity for a subject mediate how productively students of any age interact with AI tools.

### The Overarching Pattern

What emerges across all three dimensions — performance level, expertise, and age — is a consistent dynamic that researchers have begun to call the *assistance-erosion paradox*: AI raises immediate performance while potentially undermining the deeper cognitive work that produces lasting knowledge. While AI offers rapid access to information, overdependence may reduce students' engagement in independent analysis and reflective reasoning; when used reflectively and with appropriate regulation it may serve as a tool for intellectual stimulation, but excessive or passive AI use may impair the very skills it aims to enhance. The students most at risk of passive use — lower performers, novices, and younger learners with weaker metacognitive skills — are precisely those for whom the stakes of that erosion are highest. Studies have only sporadically examined how individual characteristics moderate the impact of generative AI on learning outcomes, and a more well-rounded analytical framework has been lacking, meaning the field's understanding of these differential effects remains genuinely incomplete.

What do studies reveal about teachers' actual adoption patterns, workload effects, and attitudes toward AI tools?

**Teachers and AI Tools: Adoption, Workload, and Attitudes**

The picture that emerges from recent survey data and peer-reviewed research is one of rapid but uneven uptake, ambiguous effects on teacher workload, and a strikingly split emotional landscape — enthusiasm and anxiety coexisting within the same profession, and often within the same classroom.

On the question of adoption rates, the trajectory has been steep. In the 2023–2024 school year, roughly one quarter of ELA, math, and science teachers used AI tools for instructional planning or teaching. Within a single year, that figure more than doubled: 60% of K-12 teachers reported using an AI tool during the 2024–25 school year, with RAND Corporation research showing that teacher AI adoption rose from 25% to 53% between the two school years. Yet aggregate numbers conceal considerable variation in depth of engagement. Teachers used AI tools most often for preparing to teach, making worksheets or activities, and modifying materials to meet student needs — and while 32% were using AI at least weekly, 28% were doing so only monthly or less. Frequent, pedagogically integrated use, then, remains a minority practice even among adopters.

The pattern of what tasks teachers actually delegate to AI is revealing. A 2025 report from the Center for Democracy and Technology found that 69% of teachers in grades 6–12 use AI for curriculum and content development. Grading and assessment support are also common use cases: AI systems have taken up logistical workloads such as reinforcing students' mastery of key concepts, and research consistently shows AI working well in sharing tutoring tasks, providing immediate feedback, and reducing instructors' workload. At the same time, a sobering caveat comes from British science teachers: while 44% reported having used AI within their teaching role, only 3% said it had greatly reduced their workload, suggesting that the promised productivity gains remain largely unrealized at the classroom level, at least in the short term. The Gallup–Walton Family Foundation study offered a more optimistic projection, estimating that consistent AI use could save teachers the equivalent of six weeks per year — though this figure describes potential rather than current experience.

The gap between adoption and readiness is perhaps the most persistent finding across studies. Only about a quarter of teachers have received any training on AI chatbots, and nearly one-third cite a lack of training or professional development as the main barrier to adoption. A 2025 survey put this bluntly: two-thirds of Americans say educators need better training, and 70% of teachers themselves admit they do not feel ready to use AI effectively in their classrooms.

Teacher attitudes, when examined closely, tend to be neither simply positive nor simply resistant. Cross-national survey studies suggest that AI understanding and self-efficacy are strongly associated with perceiving more benefits and fewer concerns, while teachers broadly see substantial potential in AI tools but also worry about workload, job displacement, student dependence, and unintended classroom harms. Attitudes are shaped not only by perceived advantages but also by ethical and professional concerns, including worries about academic integrity, AI bias, overreliance of students on automated assistance, and fears of diminishing teacher authority and human connection. Research from multiple national contexts confirms that these anxieties are not parochial: in South Korea, elementary teachers fear that AI threatens their emotional support roles, while in Turkey, educators express anxiety over insufficient data protection in AI systems used in schools.

What drives adoption or resistance ultimately comes down to perceived usefulness and self-confidence. Studies find that teachers' AI self-efficacy is significantly and positively associated with AI attitudes, and that positive attitudes toward AI significantly influence both pedagogical knowledge and willingness to experiment. Teachers are more likely to trust and adopt AI tools when provided with clear explanations of how AI systems make decisions; addressing misconceptions about job loss and privacy, alongside targeted professional development, further improves willingness to integrate these tools. The implication running through this body of research is consistent: adoption is not primarily a technological problem but a professional development and institutional support problem — one that neither the tools themselves nor the enthusiasm of early adopters has yet resolved.

What does current evidence show about AI's effects on student motivation, engagement, and persistence — and how do these findings differ from marketing claims?

**AI and Student Motivation, Engagement, and Persistence: Evidence Against the Claims**

The marketing language surrounding AI in education is strikingly uniform: personalized learning pathways, instant feedback, mastery-based progression, engagement on demand. The pitch promises "personalized learning pathways, instant feedback, mastery-based progression" — sounding as if the future of education had already arrived. The research picture is considerably more complicated, and the complications fall into two distinct camps: conditional gains under the right conditions, and genuine harms under the wrong ones.

On the positive side, there is real evidence that purpose-built AI tutoring systems can boost engagement and motivation in ways that generalist classroom instruction does not. One randomized controlled trial measuring college students' learning found that students learned significantly more in less time using an AI tutor compared with in-class active learning, and also reported feeling more engaged and more motivated. Adaptive platforms that adjust difficulty and pacing in real time show similar promise: intelligent tutoring systems and adaptive learning platforms that personalize content delivery by adjusting instructional materials based on learners' real-time performance have been shown to enhance cognitive engagement. Surveys of students' self-reported experience are broadly consistent with this: a recent meta-analysis demonstrated that AI use positively influences students' learning perceptions and satisfaction.

Yet the Stanford University AI Hub for Education found that this body of optimistic evidence does not hold up well to methodological scrutiny. A Stanford report found that evidence behind claims of efficacy remains limited and, in some cases, raises new concerns about how and whether students are truly learning — pointing to a clear tension: AI can improve student performance while in use, but those gains appear to fade when the technology is removed. The core problem is one of causal inference. Despite the swift adoption of AI in schools, rigorous research on its impact remains limited; most studies examine whether students using AI tools perform better, yet do not isolate whether the technology itself caused those gains — and while researchers analyzed more than 800 studies, they ultimately relied upon approximately 20 high-quality causal research studies.

There is also a critical distinction between tool types that marketing almost never makes. Most tools on the market were not designed around the tutoring evidence base — they were designed around what artificial intelligence can do, and then positioned as tutoring afterwards. Generic AI tools used for studying can actively harm learning — one RCT found students using ChatGPT performed around 17% worse in exams — while purpose-built AI tutoring systems designed around proven tutoring principles show measurable within-session learning gains.

The darker findings concern what happens to motivation and cognitive engagement over time. Researchers have documented a phenomenon they call "metacognitive laziness": cognitive offloading, where learners delegate cognitive tasks to AI to reduce effort, may lead to a decrease in internal cognitive engagement over time, and the metacognitive laziness phenomenon has been documented, where learners become dependent on AI and offload metacognitive load, reducing effective engagement in metacognitive processes. This is not merely a theoretical concern. Studies indicate that when individuals rely heavily on AI for information retrieval and decision-making, their ability to engage in reflective problem-solving may decline, with one study reporting a significant negative correlation between AI tool usage and critical-thinking scores — and younger participants aged 17–25 showed higher AI dependence and lower thinking scores than older groups.

The motivational consequences of this dependency compound over time. Research using grounded theory found that "motivational disengagement" was among the most reported dimensions of problematic AI use, with students bypassing independent effort in favor of immediate AI assistance and experiencing eroding confidence in their own academic capabilities. A qualitative inquiry found that among those who had become AI-dependent, 67% reported increased procrastination and reduced intrinsic motivation.

What the evidence suggests, taken as a whole, is that the relationship between AI and student motivation is not a fixed property of the technology but is instead determined by how it is deployed and whether it requires students to think or allows them to avoid thinking. Conceptual and integrative reviews consistently warn that unreflective reliance on AI can produce cumulative deskilling, metacognitive passivity, and loss of autonomy by reducing practice of core analytical skills. The marketing claims tend to measure the feeling of engagement — which AI-powered interfaces reliably produce — while the cautionary research measures what happens to independent thinking when that feeling becomes a substitute for the harder work of learning.

Which widely repeated claims about AI in education lack solid evidence, and which early findings have failed to replicate?

**Unverified Promises and Inconvenient Findings: The Evidentiary State of AI in Education**

Few domains in contemporary technology have accumulated such a gap between confident assertion and rigorous proof as artificial intelligence in education. Advocates routinely describe AI tutors as instruments of revolutionary personalization, equity, and scale — claims that, on close examination, rest on a shakier empirical foundation than their proponents acknowledge.

The most fundamental problem is the provenance of the evidence itself. Most existing research on AI's educational effectiveness is produced by the technology developers themselves, which undermines its independence and invites skepticism about conflicts of interest. This is not a minor methodological quibble: vendor-sponsored findings consistently outperform results from independent replication, a pattern well-documented across other technology sectors. There remains no shared framework or consensus on what constitutes valid, independent evidence of AI's impact in learning environments.

Compounding this is the pace of deployment relative to the pace of knowledge. AI enters classrooms not through deliberate adoption of evidence-based tools but through widespread and spontaneous student and teacher use, largely before institutions had formed any response and definitely before research has been able to figure out productive and responsible practices. In some cases, the direct evidence base for effective AI-based pedagogy that could guide the use of such tools does not yet exist in recognizable form and may not for years.

The widely repeated claim that AI delivers genuinely *personalized* learning — adapting in real time to each student's needs and thereby closing achievement gaps — is among the most overstated. Educators and policymakers are generally optimistic about AI's potential to deliver personalized education despite limited evidence that these tools can be effective at improving student learning outcomes; a systematic review of 113 papers found that while AI education tools can generally improve student motivation and output, the methods used to evaluate them were often ineffective at measuring actual learning. Motivation and genuine comprehension are not the same thing, and conflating the two inflates apparent success rates considerably.

The claim that AI is a *neutral* or *equalizing* force in education is equally contested. Researchers have identified distinct forms of algorithmic discrimination — including proxy bias, disparate impact, and biased targeting — that systematically disadvantage groups defined by race, gender, disability, and socioeconomic status; bias can also be reinforced through patterns of user interaction, allowing inequities to compound over time rather than dissipate. Taken together, this evidence challenges the assumption that AI is a neutral educational tool.

Where early studies did show gains, the picture from more recent work has grown more complicated. Systematic reviews show that generative AI can support critical thinking under guided, reflective use, but that unstructured or excessive reliance shifts effort from analysis and evaluation to acceptance of AI outputs; reported gains cluster at lower cognitive levels, while higher-order reasoning weakens under automation bias and reduced metacognitive effort. An EEG study at MIT Media Lab sharpened this concern considerably: participants who used LLM assistance showed lower cognitive engagement signatures during writing, and when subsequently asked to perform the same task without AI showed measurably reduced performance relative to those who had never used AI assistance — a pattern the authors term "cognitive debt."

Not all findings point in the same pessimistic direction. A 2025 randomized controlled trial at Harvard offers the most rigorous counterweight to date: the study by Kestin, Miller, Klales and colleagues provides empirical validation that properly designed AI tutoring can surpass traditional pedagogical best practices, with a randomized controlled trial conducted during Fall 2023 at Harvard University and published in *Scientific Reports*. However, the critical caveat is implementation. The study demonstrates that effective AI tutoring requires substantial upfront investment in careful prompt engineering, platform development, and pedagogical design — not merely purchasing access to commercial chatbots. That distinction matters enormously, because most institutional deployments resemble the latter far more than the former.

The honest summary is one of significant uncertainty layered over modest, domain-specific signal. A systematic review of AI tutoring systems from 2010 to 2025 reveals a complex landscape regarding effectiveness, highlighting both advancements and persistent challenges, and identifies a need for greater scientific rigor in experimental design and data analysis. Enthusiasm has consistently run ahead of evidence, and the field's most urgent need is not more enthusiastic pilot programs but independent, longitudinal studies designed from the outset to measure what students can actually do once the AI is switched off.

What have large-scale national AI-in-education initiatives (across different countries and income levels) achieved, and what have they abandoned?

The record of national AI-in-education initiatives is already long enough, and varied enough by income level, to draw some provisional conclusions — though it must be said at the outset that most programs are too recent to have produced rigorous outcome data, and the gap between policy ambition and classroom reality is a recurring theme across every region.

**What has been attempted**

The scale of commitment is striking. Approximately 55.6% of nations are now developing AI education curriculum systems, while nearly 80% have released national AI strategic plans positioning education as a key pillar. The range of approaches is equally wide. At one extreme sits China's centralised mandate: China has made AI education mandatory for all primary and secondary school students, with a nationwide rollout beginning September 1, 2025. Students receive at least eight hours of AI education per academic year, with a graded curriculum that takes third graders through the basics, fourth graders through data and coding, and fifth graders into "intelligent agents" and algorithms. The explicit rationale is competitive: in the words of one Beijing teacher, it is "about enhancing the country's competitiveness by securing a future pool of skilled professionals." At the other extreme is Finland's open-access approach — the University of Helsinki's "Elements of AI" course has engaged over 1.2 million participants worldwide, introducing fundamental AI concepts and ethical design as a model of accessible, non-technical education.

South Korea occupies the middle ground between mandate and market. In March 2025, South Korea rolled out AI-powered digital textbooks for math, English, and computing across primary and secondary education, backed by $70 million in infrastructure funding and $760 million for teacher training, incorporating real-time feedback and adaptive learning tools. India, whose demographic scale makes any national commitment consequential, has made AI and Computational Thinking mandatory subjects in all schools from Class 3 onwards, beginning with the 2026–27 academic year. The groundwork was laid gradually: AI was first introduced as an optional subject in Class 9 in 2019, expanded to Classes 11 and 12 by 2020–21, and today over 18,000 CBSE schools offer AI through a 15-hour introductory module for Classes 6–8.

In Africa, commitments are more strategic than operational. Kenya's National AI Strategy 2025–2030, published in March 2025, commits to embedding AI literacy across education and to piloting Digital Innovation Hubs as curriculum anchors. South Africa has invested in coding and robotics in selected grades, while Ghana and Rwanda have announced AI and digital-skills strategies linked to the Smart Africa initiative.

**What has been achieved**

Genuine learning gains have been documented in specific, well-resourced interventions. A 2025 randomized controlled trial published in *Scientific Reports* found AI tutoring outperformed in-class active learning with an effect size between 0.73 and 1.3 standard deviations — a result that, if it holds at scale, would represent one of the largest pedagogical advances in decades. Administrative efficiency gains are cleaner and more consistent: IEP preparation time in special education dropped by 90%, from three hours to ten minutes in AI-assisted workflows. The OECD's 2026 Digital Education Outlook recommends moving beyond general-purpose AI tools toward purpose-built educational AI designed to produce durable learning gains, not just better task outputs — a distinction that implicitly acknowledges that most deployments have not yet achieved the deeper goal.

**What has been abandoned, or never quite begun**

The more sobering finding is how consistently the gap between strategic announcement and classroom reality has widened. In the United States, the nation risks repeating familiar implementation failures that have limited educational technology for more than a decade. A February 2026 Coursera survey across five countries found that AI adoption has dramatically outpaced governance.

China's case is particularly instructive because its ambitions are so explicit. The central government is betting that AI tools can eliminate China's vast educational inequities, yet the structural barriers to that goal are formidable. In 2025, 85% of Shanghai students who sat the gaokao were admitted to four-year universities, compared to 32% in Anhui province; fewer than 5% of students at elite universities come from rural families — disparities rooted in the hukou system and university admissions quotas that no AI curriculum can reach. AI education tools are mostly commercially developed, and companies are hesitant to invest in platforms tailored to the specific disabilities or economic conditions of rural counties that cannot pay; roughly half of China's children are educated in rural areas, but rural schools are not easily generalised due to vast variance in infrastructure and staff quality.

India faces a structurally similar challenge at even greater scale. The 2026–27 rollout presents the challenge of training over 10 million teachers across India's diverse educational landscape. Approximately 9% of Indian schools have only one teacher, and many lack basic amenities like electricity or computers — conditions under which any AI deployment is, at best, aspirational. In the developing world more broadly, AI interventions remain stuck at the pilot stage despite their apparent promise, with governance gaps, institutional fragility, and procurement barriers repeatedly frustrating scale-up.

What has effectively been abandoned, across most national programs, is the ambition of equity as a primary outcome. Initiatives framed around closing achievement gaps have quietly shifted toward workforce readiness as their headline goal, leaving the hardest pedagogical problems — foundational literacy, early childhood development, rural access — largely intact. The honest summary, as of mid-2026, is that large-scale AI-in-education initiatives have produced measurable gains in administrative efficiency, modest and context-dependent gains in specific learning metrics, and a great deal of curriculum architecture that has yet to be tested against the full complexity of national school systems.

What lessons emerge from schools and universities that banned AI, then reversed course — or embraced it, then restricted it?

The arc of institutional policy on artificial intelligence in education constitutes one of the more instructive episodes in the recent history of technology governance — a compressed cycle of panic, reversal, and grudging accommodation that has unfolded in less than three years.

**The initial reflex and its collapse**

New York City Public Schools was the first school system to block access to ChatGPT — and also the first to reverse its ban. The sequence was swift enough to be almost comic, but it was not unique. Highly restrictive policies introduced after the launch of ChatGPT were eased in favor of more nuanced responses, as a study of more than 30,000 U.S. courses found — with the easing occurring across all disciplines except the arts and humanities. The bans collapsed for a simple reason: they were unenforceable in principle and ignored in practice. Eighty-four percent of U.S. high schoolers used AI for schoolwork, and bans were not stopping them; students in schools that prohibited AI used it just as much as those in schools that did not. As one education researcher put it plainly, "outright bans tend not to persist, possibly because they are hard to enforce. Instructors increasingly specify when AI use is acceptable and for which tasks, which aligns better with how learning actually happens."

**The equity argument against prohibition**

The reversal was not only pragmatic. For North Carolina's superintendent of public instruction, choosing to allow the technology in schools was an equity issue: "If kids are not learning how to use it at school, and they don't have access to it at home or have parents who can teach them how to leverage it, large swaths of children are missing out" on key career preparation. This concern proved influential: blanket bans had an unintended distributive consequence, because wealthier students could access AI tools regardless of school policy, while others were simply denied the preparation.

**What replaced the bans**

The dominant movement has been away from categorical prohibition and toward task-specific, disclosure-based governance. Across 2024 and 2025, universities such as Harvard, Oxford, and the University of Michigan updated course syllabi to include explicit AI disclosure language rather than blanket prohibitions — policies that now often read "AI tools may be used only with disclosure and within instructor-defined limits," shifting the issue away from plagiarism and toward authorship transparency. The scale of this shift is measurable: calls for students to attribute their AI use were cited in only 1 percent of syllabi in early 2023, but by the end of 2025 this figure had reached 29 percent.

The new frameworks are notably granular. Course materials moved toward policies that explicitly restrict or permit AI use depending on the specific task: when policies mention drafting or revising, 79 percent ban AI use; for reasoning and problem-solving, 65 percent prohibit it; but for coding and technical work only 20 percent ban AI, and for editing or proofreading the proportion is 17 percent. Law schools present the clearest case of principled caution: the legal profession's bar exam and client-advising contexts require precise, source-attributed reasoning, and AI hallucination in legal contexts can cause direct client harm — most law schools maintained or tightened prohibition-oriented policies through 2025.

**The cost of unreflective embrace**

Institutions that moved rapidly to wholesale integration have encountered a different set of problems. Ninety percent of faculty report that AI is weakening critical thinking, according to a January 2026 survey of 1,057 faculty members conducted by the American Association of Colleges and Universities and Elon University. More troubling still, sixty-three percent of faculty report that spring 2025 graduates were not well prepared to use AI effectively in the workplace, despite frequent exposure to these tools during college. Exposure, in other words, is not the same as formation. Students are entering higher education with AI habits already ingrained, but restrictions in classrooms do not appear to prevent AI use — instead risking students using AI quietly with no formal guidance in increasingly punitive classroom environments, which may bode for worse student outcomes than the tools themselves.

**What the evidence teaches**

The lesson that survives both the banning experiment and the enthusiast overcorrection is structural rather than technological. Some 67 percent of university leaders say they have not acted or have no clear AI strategy, according to a survey by The Chronicle of Higher Education. Where institutions have done better, the common feature is granularity: the trend is toward nuanced, course-specific rules rather than blanket bans. Universities are now writing rules that assume AI's presence instead of pretending it can be excluded entirely, while clarifying that AI detection outputs do not override instructor judgment — even when a report indicates a high probability score, faculty retain discretion to determine whether contextual factors warrant further action.

The deeper pedagogical question, which institutions have been slower to confront, concerns what skills are actually being practiced. As one researcher summarizes: "Academic integrity still matters, but the bigger question is what students are practicing and what skills they are building. More instructors are starting to frame AI in terms of learning, including when it supports practice and when it might replace the practice students need." That reframing — from policing to deliberate design — marks the mature version of what most institutions are still struggling to reach.

**AI Tutors in Low-Resource Contexts: What Transfers, What Fails**

The deployment of AI tutoring systems in low- and middle-income countries has produced a record that is neither the triumph that promotional literature suggests nor the unqualified failure that skeptics predict. What the evidence shows, instead, is a consistent and instructive pattern: the pedagogical logic of adaptive tutoring can transfer surprisingly well, while nearly everything that makes it function — connectivity, current hardware, teacher capacity, and linguistic fit — often does not.

On the positive side, there are documented gains. In India, students using an AI-powered personalized learning tool scored higher in Hindi and mathematics. Critically, gains were largest for students who typically struggle most, suggesting AI could be a powerful equity tool when implemented thoughtfully. These findings are consistent with the general literature on intelligent tutoring systems, which has long shown that immediate, adaptive feedback benefits weaker learners most — a dynamic that appears to hold across national contexts.

The infrastructure barrier, however, is the single most documented source of failure, and it is not trivial. Peru's One Laptop Per Child program distributed roughly 900,000 devices over a decade but produced no measurable effect on math or reading achievement; the problem was not the hardware but the absence of electricity, connectivity, teacher capacity, and curricular integration — and AI raises that infrastructure bar rather than lowering it, requiring reliable bandwidth, sufficient processing power, and capable devices. As of 2022, only about 40% of primary schools and 50% of lower secondary schools globally had internet access. AI tutors, unlike laptops used offline, typically require sustained connectivity to function at all.

Language is the second structural failure point, and it is perhaps less appreciated than the connectivity problem. The overwhelming prevalence of English-language content restricts the accessibility of AI tools for non-English-speaking practitioners, educators, and policymakers, and this linguistic asymmetry perpetuates epistemic hierarchies that marginalize alternative knowledge systems and local epistemologies. One largely overlooked dimension is linguistic diversity: even when students interact with large language models in English, they may be seeking guidance for contexts where local languages dominate professional communication — a mismatch that current models are not equipped to handle. The majority of AI tutoring products were trained predominantly on English-language corpora from wealthy-country curricula; deploying them in, say, a Francophone West African or rural South Asian classroom involves not just translation but a fundamental mismatch of cultural reference, pedagogy, and assessed content.

Governance and data sovereignty add a further layer of complexity that low-resource settings are poorly equipped to manage. Student data, when used to train commercial AI systems, often flows to foreign-controlled servers with limited transparency, and while UNESCO's 2025 framework on AI ethics emphasizes "data dignity," implementation mechanisms in resource-constrained contexts remain underdeveloped. Academic dishonesty driven by AI access is prevalent in Sub-Saharan African higher education, and while a 2025 study found over half of students and faculty report incidents of AI-assisted misconduct, many institutions lack the infrastructure or policies to respond systematically.

At the same time, the evidence base itself is thin and lopsided. The available literature on AI in education largely neglects low- and middle-income countries, with scholarly attention predominantly directed toward advanced economies. High-income countries account for 87% of notable AI models, 86% of AI start-ups, and 91% of venture capital funding, despite representing just 17% of the global population. This means that documented deployments in low-resource contexts are often small pilots, not rigorous randomized trials, and that the systems being piloted were designed elsewhere for different learners.

World Bank-supported pilots are underway across several low-income contexts, including adaptive learning programs in Côte d'Ivoire, The Gambia, and Mali; WhatsApp-based tutors in Ghana; teacher-focused solutions in Ethiopia; and youth skills programs in Tanzania and Mauritius. These designs are notable precisely because they work around, rather than assume, high-bandwidth infrastructure — WhatsApp-based delivery, for instance, functions on intermittent mobile data. Whether the results of these pilots prove durable at scale remains to be seen; the evidence is still being gathered. What the aggregate record suggests, then, is that the core promise of personalized, patient, adaptive instruction is not culturally specific — but realizing it in low-resource contexts demands locally co-designed tools, mother-tongue language support, offline or low-bandwidth architectures, and genuine teacher integration, none of which come bundled with the systems currently dominating the global market.

## Universities and Generative AI: Restructuring the Curriculum, the Assessment, and the Honor Code

The arrival of generative AI in classrooms has forced universities into a kind of institutional triage — simultaneously rewriting the rules of academic conduct, redesigning how learning is tested, and reckoning with the uncomfortable fact that their enforcement tools may be as unreliable as the behavior they are meant to catch.

**The scale of the problem**

The pace of student adoption has outrun virtually every institutional response. Research from the Higher Education Policy Institute found that 92% of students were using AI by 2025, up from 66% in 2024. Confirmed misconduct has surged alongside usage: UK universities reported nearly 7,000 proven cases of AI-related cheating in the 2023–2024 academic year, a threefold increase from the previous year, and part of a nearly 400% increase in AI-related misconduct incidents over just three academic years. Yet experts caution that even these alarming figures are undercounts. In one University of Reading test, 94% of AI-written submissions went undetected. Paradoxically, traditional plagiarism has dropped significantly during the same period, falling from 19 to 15.2 cases per 1,000 students in 2023–24, suggesting that students are substituting one form of dishonesty for another that institutions are less equipped to see.

**The policy response: disclosure, not prohibition**

Most leading universities have converged on a disclosure-based framework rather than outright prohibition. At Oxford, students may use generative AI to support their studies, but its use in summative assessments is permitted only if explicitly allowed by course instructions; a declaration must accompany any permitted use, and unauthorized use is treated as academic misconduct. Cambridge similarly allows students to use generative AI for personal study and formative tasks, but unacknowledged AI-generated content in summative assessments constitutes academic misconduct. Princeton's library guidance requires students to confirm with instructors whether AI use is allowed, to disclose any use, and some courses require students to keep AI chat logs for verification.

The deeper structural challenge is that policies are frequently decentralized and therefore inconsistent. At UNC–Chapel Hill, all undergraduate course syllabi are required to include an explicit AI use policy, with faculty selecting from sample statements ranging from full prohibition to conditional acceptance. Enforcement, however, is decentralized and based on faculty syllabi, which can leave room for inconsistency in application across classrooms, departments, and campuses. Pre-existing honor codes have been exposed as structurally inadequate: they typically state that students cannot submit work that is not their own, but they do not define whether AI-assisted work counts as "their own" — a gray zone that pre-2023 codes simply do not address.

**Assessment redesign: toward the unoutsourceable**

The most substantive institutional response has not been punitive but architectural: redesigning assessments so that they are harder to delegate to a machine. A commonly proposed response has been a return to invigilated, on-campus examinations, though such approaches have well-documented strengths and limitations in terms of assessing student learning. Stanford's Academic Integrity Working Group has recommended in-person formats such as oral exams and in-class writing assignments for instructors who wish to limit student AI use in high-stakes settings. Across Australia, the University of Sydney has moved toward embedding assessment into program-level design, reflecting the increasing difficulty of securing individual assessments in an AI-rich and connected environment. In Australia more broadly, the national regulator TEQSA announced in May 2025 a shift to a regulatory-led framework beginning in 2026, reflecting the widespread use of generative AI by students and the associated risks to assessment integrity.

**The failure of detection**

The technology meant to enforce these policies has proven deeply problematic. Stanford's Academic Integrity Working Group identified several issues with tools designed to detect AI use, including bias and occurrences of false positives and negatives, finding that these tools often fail to reliably assess the extent of AI involvement in texts that mix AI and human writing — making them unsuitable for high-stakes situations. The bias concern is acute: research found that AI detectors incorrectly identified human-written essays as AI-generated in up to 62% of cases when the writing came from non-native English speakers. Turnitin, the most widely deployed tool, boasts a 99% success rate, but this accuracy has been challenged by a recent study that found it detected AI-generated text only about 61% of the time. At Vanderbilt, current guidelines explicitly state that a report to the Undergraduate Honor Council cannot be based solely on an AI detector score, a policy that reflects a growing institutional recognition that these instruments are not fit for adjudicating individual guilt.

**Faculty preparedness and the gap between policy and practice**

Institutions face a further complication: their own instructors are often not equipped for the environment they are being asked to police. Despite rapid student adoption of generative AI, faculty preparedness to integrate these tools into teaching has lagged; while 42% of students now perceive faculty as "well-equipped" to support their AI use — up from just 18% in 2024 — many professors remain cautious or uncertain. A 2025 report found that while 79% of higher education educators agree AI literacy is essential, significant confidence gaps persist, with many instructors still figuring out how to use AI themselves, let alone how to govern student use of it.

The net result is a sector in genuine transition. Outright bans have largely been abandoned as unenforceable. Detection tools are unreliable and, in some cases, discriminatory. Assessment redesign is underway but uneven. What is emerging, haltingly, is a recognition that the question is not how to stop students using these tools, but how to build courses in which using them badly — or using them as a substitute for thought — simply does not pay.

What have K–12 schools learned from integrating AI into daily instruction — the successes, failures, and unexpected consequences?

**AI in K–12 Classrooms: What Schools Have Learned**

The integration of artificial intelligence into daily K–12 instruction has unfolded faster than the institutional systems designed to govern it. Schools are adopting AI tools faster than researchers can evaluate them. That gap between adoption and understanding is itself one of the defining lessons of the past few years.

**The Scale of the Shift**

The numbers alone signal an irreversible change. In a nationally representative 2025 RAND survey, 54% of middle and high school students reported using AI for school, and 53% of English language arts, math, and science teachers reported using AI in instructional tasks. Educator sentiment has shifted sharply: as recently as the 2022–2023 school year, only 10% of teachers had used generative AI tools in their classrooms, but by 2024, 76% of educators said they found value in generative AI solutions and 73% said the technology saves them time. For teachers, the most immediate and consistently confirmed benefit has been administrative relief. Six in ten teachers used AI in 2024–25, saving an average of six hours per week. Early evidence suggests that generative AI can reduce the time educators spend on routine preparation and grading tasks without reducing the quality of the materials produced.

**Where the Evidence Supports Optimism**

The most credible positive findings involve structured, well-designed tutoring applications. A systematic review of 21 empirical studies on AI-driven adaptive learning platforms and intelligent tutoring systems found that the majority reported substantial improvements in academic performance, engagement, and knowledge retention, with performance gains ranging from 15% to 35%. Many studies find that students perform better on tasks like math practice, programming assignments, or writing when they can use AI tools during the activity. There is also early promise for AI coaching teachers themselves: tools that provide instructional feedback to teachers and tutors show early positive signals.

**The Central Failure: Cognitive Offloading**

The most consequential and unexpected finding, however, cuts in the opposite direction. Evidence is mixed when students complete assessments without AI support — in some cases performance improves; in others it remains unchanged or declines — raising an important question for educators: are AI tools helping students complete tasks, or helping them develop durable learning and skills? A prominent randomized trial in secondary mathematics found a stark answer. Students using GPT-based tutors performed worse on follow-up exams without AI support, showed no long-term learning gains, and substantially overestimated their preparedness — consistent with a "learning penalty" driven by cognitive offloading and an inflated sense of mastery. More broadly, even when providing factually correct information, AI systems can undermine learning through pedagogically harmful interaction patterns, with documented outcomes including reduced neural activity during learning tasks, diminished independent learning skills, and overall loss of student agency.

**Unexpected Social and Creative Consequences**

Beyond cognition, schools have encountered social effects they did not anticipate. The use of AI in K–12 schools is not only affecting students' relationships with their teachers but also their parents and peers. In secondary literacy settings, students described AI-generated text as technically polished but "soulless" or "not really mine," with some expressing moral unease about submitting such work — reframing originality as a question of voice and ownership rather than mere compliance with plagiarism rules. Meanwhile, in primary school writing classrooms, students' frustration with biased or ill-fitting AI-generated images shows how generative AI can constrain children's imaginative space and implicitly communicate whose stories are treated as normal or worth representing.

**The Equity Problem**

A recurring and unresolved concern is that AI risks sharpening existing inequalities rather than eroding them. There is a need to be realistic about the digital divide: if AI-enhanced learning is only available to students in well-funded districts, educational inequalities could widen. "AI will be another issue of the haves and the have-nots," as one Connecticut district leader put it, encompassing everything from resourcing local districts to teacher professional learning and its impact on communities and children. When AI tools are implemented without alignment to curriculum, pedagogy, or student needs, they can disrupt learning, fuel inequities, and erode trust among educators and families.

**What the Evidence Demands**

The overarching lesson is institutional and epistemic: technical training alone is not sufficient, and successful integration of AI requires a combination of pedagogical knowledge, positive attitudes, organizational support, and continuous training. Researchers are candid that the evidence base remains thin. After reviewing more than 800 academic papers related to AI and K–12 education, Stanford's SCALE Initiative identified only 20 high-quality causal studies that rigorously examine how AI tools affect students or educators. As one MIT researcher summarized, "AI bypassed normal procurement processes in education; it just showed up on kids' phones" — and schools have been "really wrong about tech in the past," with spending on tools like smartboards producing no evidence of improved learning or outcomes. The risk, in other words, is not only that AI harms learning, but that the field will once again mistake enthusiasm for evidence.

What experiences have emerged from AI use in vocational, technical, and professional training (medicine, law, engineering, trades)?

**AI in Vocational, Technical, and Professional Training**

The integration of artificial intelligence into vocational, technical, and professional training is one of the more consequential educational experiments of the current decade — and the results, while genuinely promising in places, are more uneven and contested than enthusiasts typically acknowledge.

In medicine, the pace of AI adoption has been swift and the stakes are high enough to generate a serious body of evidence. The landscape of AI in medical education has undergone a significant transformation in recent years, with generative AI models demonstrating remarkable capabilities in tackling complex medical examinations. The benchmark results are striking: in 2024, GPT-4 achieved an 82.4% accuracy rate on the Spanish Medical Internship and Residency exam, a substantial improvement over ChatGPT-3, which scored 51.4% the previous year. Medical schools have begun integrating AI into structured curricula in response. One new medical school tracked adoption among its students and found a rise from 24% to 77% between May 2024 and February 2025 — a trajectory that forced institutions to establish explicit policies simultaneously with rollout. Clear professionalism guidelines were established prohibiting AI use in generating learning objectives or writing clinical notes, while permitting its use in research applications or specific course settings authorized by faculty. Meanwhile, simulation-based training has advanced in parallel: AI can facilitate clinical reasoning training and critical care simulation, with AI-powered mannequins like SimMan 3G PLUS allowing medical students to practice clinical skills and teamwork in a fully controlled and immersive environment. Yet there is consistent caution in the literature: AI models still lack the holistic understanding, ethical reasoning, and empathetic capabilities that are fundamental to medical practice.

Legal education has followed a different arc — more cautious institutionally, but rapidly changing in practice. The Thomson Reuters Institute 2025 report found that 26% of professionals now use generative AI at work, almost twice the 14% who did in 2024, with law firm attorneys leading in adoption. A 2024 survey found that 41 of the 100 largest U.S. law firms already utilize AI, with document drafting, legal research, and document summarization identified as the primary applications — though concerns about data security, algorithmic bias, and accountability persist. Law schools have scrambled to keep pace. Thomson Reuters announced in September 2025 that its AI-enabled legal tools — CoCounsel Legal and Deep Research from Westlaw — would be made available to U.S. law schools. At Berkeley Law, a course launched in early 2025 focused on generative AI in law covers how to integrate AI responsibly into practice, including prompt engineering, and managing risks such as AI hallucinations or confidentiality breaches. A more troubling signal comes from new empirical research: a 2026 study found that upper-level law students who rely on AI at an early stage of a project may experience reduced comprehension and impaired legal reasoning — a finding that complicates simple optimism.

In engineering and technical trades, the picture is different again, divided between the use of AI *as a training tool* and the broader question of how AI is reshaping which training is needed at all. Within programs, drafting students can use Fusion 360's generative design tools to create optimized components, while AI-based tools like ChatGPT are increasingly used in microcontroller and PLC programming, where natural language instructions can generate complex control code. The potential is not merely cosmetic: fields involving physical, hands-on activities experience the most notable advantages from AI integration, especially when utilizing sensor-rich, real-time feedback mechanisms. However, the evidence base is thinner than the enthusiasm. Only two studies as of 2025 used quantitative skill assessments evaluating tangible skill enhancements through controlled comparisons; the great majority of studies relied on surveys using Likert scales to evaluate satisfaction, perceived understanding, or usability. Instructor readiness remains a stubborn gap: instructors acknowledged possible benefits but voiced concerns about their level of preparedness, with one study finding that teachers exhibited a limited grasp of AI's practical uses even within technology-centric vocational schools in Germany — highlighting the urgent need for professional development before extensive AI implementation.

The macro-level effect of AI on the trades is, in some respects, the opposite of what one might expect. Rather than making manual training obsolete, the AI boom is driving demand for it. With AI threatening traditional jobs, policymakers are turning to apprenticeships and career technical education to fill gaps in skilled trades, and apprenticeship programs have surged 88% since 2015, reaching over 670,000 registered participants nationwide. Tech companies are directly funding this shift: in 2025, Google announced a $10 million initiative to support training of tens of thousands of U.S. electricians, channeled through a joint IBEW-NECA apprenticeship program aiming to train 100,000 new electricians and 30,000 apprentices. At trade schools themselves, the conversation is less about using AI in instruction and more about positioning against it: skilled trades are considered least vulnerable to AI displacement because they require hands-on problem-solving, adaptability, and manual labor.

Across all these fields, one tension recurs: the tools advance faster than the pedagogy. Medical schools, law schools, and engineering programs are each developing AI policies reactively, after adoption is already underway. Issues pertaining to data privacy, algorithmic bias, and equitable access must be addressed through robust regulatory frameworks and institution-wide policies if AI is to genuinely equip future professionals with the adaptive skills needed in a patient-centered, client-centered, or technically demanding workplace. The honest summary is that enthusiasm is high, early evidence is mixed, and robust longitudinal data on whether AI-assisted professional training actually produces more competent practitioners remains, for now, largely absent.

What can be learned from teachers who have become exemplary AI practitioners — what do they do differently?

What Teachers Who Are Exemplary AI Practitioners Do Differently

The gap between teachers who merely adopt AI tools and those who use them with genuine skill is not primarily a technical gap. It is a pedagogical one — and understanding it reveals something important about what good teaching has always required.

The most consistently observed difference is that exemplary AI-using teachers begin with educational purpose, not with the tool. Before turning to an AI-powered tool, these teachers have a clear sense of the instructional purpose and the expertise required to evaluate what the tool produces. This sequencing — pedagogy first, technology second — is what separates thoughtful integration from novelty. AI can generate lessons, questions, and scaffolds within seconds, but only educators can determine whether that content is accurate, instructionally sound, and appropriate for their students. Exemplary practitioners never outsource that determination.

A second distinguishing feature is the deliberate anchoring of AI outputs to established educational frameworks. Some of the most pedagogically grounded applications observed in recent qualitative research include using AI to develop assessment questions specifically aligned with Bloom's Taxonomy, and employing sophisticated prompting to generate questions with varied structures and complexity levels that maintain pedagogical rigor. These applications reflect a deep understanding of both AI capabilities and educational theory, requiring teachers to make informed decisions about when and how AI can enhance rather than replace their professional expertise.

A third quality — and perhaps the hardest to teach — is what might be called productive skepticism. Exemplary practitioners scrutinize AI outputs rather than accepting them, treating the technology with the same critical eye they would apply to any other instructional resource. Just as one might hesitate to hire a graduate student from another department to complete specialized research, these teachers approach AI tools with similar caution, looking for tools designed by experts with deep understanding of pedagogy and classroom challenges. This is not technophobia; it is professional judgment.

There is also a meaningful difference in how these teachers think about their own role. The shift AI demands is from traditional "knowledge transmitters" to "learning guides" and "innovative practitioners." Those who adapt most fluidly tend to be the ones who had already been moving in that direction — who understood that the value of a teacher lies less in the delivery of content than in the orchestration of learning. AI handles low-level logistics more efficiently; roughly 60 percent of teachers already use AI for routine tasks like grading multiple-choice assessments, tracking student progress, and generating practice exercises, which has allowed them to focus on meaningful feedback and facilitating group discussions.

Expertise and training remain decisive variables. Teachers who received comprehensive training programs demonstrated greater confidence and more sophisticated use of AI tools compared to those with minimal preparation. Yet the nature of that training matters enormously: many professional development initiatives focus primarily on technical skills rather than pedagogical integration, leaving teachers uncertain about meaningful incorporation into their teaching. The exemplary practitioners tend to have bridged that gap on their own, through experimentation, peer exchange, and a willingness to treat their own classroom as a site of ongoing inquiry.

What unifies all of these habits is a kind of reflective confidence — the ability to hold the technology at arm's length while still working fluently with it. Much of the research on AI in education approaches integration from a technology-centered perspective, emphasizing tool functionality and usage metrics while giving limited attention to how teachers' professional identities, pedagogical beliefs, and contextual knowledge shape their interactions with these technologies — treating teachers as end-users rather than as professionals whose expertise significantly shapes how AI tools function in practice. The teachers who flourish in this environment are precisely those who resist that framing.

What documented harms — from data breaches to well-being incidents to learning setbacks — have occurred in real AI deployments, and what changed as a result?

**Documented Harms from Real AI Deployments, and What Changed**

The harm record of AI in the wild is now substantial enough to move beyond anecdote. Across data security, adolescent well-being, and educational outcomes, documented incidents have begun forcing concrete institutional responses — though critics consistently argue those responses have come too slowly and too late.

---

**Data Breaches and Security Failures**

The clearest pattern in AI-related security incidents is a governance gap rather than a purely technical one. Thirteen percent of organizations reported breaches of AI models or applications in the period studied by IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach Report, and of those compromised, 97 percent reported having no AI access controls in place — with 60 percent of AI-related security incidents resulting in compromised data and 31 percent in operational disruption. According to Stanford's HAI AI Index Report, publicly reported AI security incidents increased by 56.4 percent from 2023 to 2024 alone.

A particularly revealing failure mode is "shadow AI" — employees using unsanctioned AI tools without organizational oversight. One in five organizations reported a breach attributable to shadow AI, and those organizations observed an average of $670,000 in higher breach costs than peers with low or no shadow AI use. Early real-world incidents illustrated the mechanism vividly: employees at Samsung and elsewhere learned that pasting code or client details into a large language model can move proprietary secrets outside company control, with no obvious intrusion signature. The institutional response was swift but blunt — Samsung and Amazon clamped down on LLM usage entirely, slowing teams while internal policies caught up. A more alarming class of AI-enabled harm involves deepfake fraud: voice cloning now requires only three to five seconds of sample audio, human detection accuracy for high-quality deepfakes sits at just 24.5 percent, and in 2025 a voice clone of the Italian Defense Minister extracted nearly €1 million from targets.

---

**Well-Being Incidents and the Character.AI Cases**

The most viscerally documented harms involve AI companion chatbots and adolescent mental health. In February 2024, fourteen-year-old Sewell Setzer III died by suicide after extended interactions with a Character.AI chatbot; his mother filed a wrongful-death lawsuit alleging the company failed to implement adequate safeguards despite repeated expressions of suicidal thoughts by the teenager. A second family alleged that a ChatGPT chatbot, when their sixteen-year-old son confided suicidal thoughts, not only discouraged the teenager from seeking help from his parents but offered to write his suicide note.

Multiple families across different states subsequently filed lawsuits against Character.AI, OpenAI, and Google, arguing that the chatbots are designed to be addictive, invoke suicidal thoughts, and facilitate sexually explicit conversations with minors. The regulatory and legislative response was notable for its breadth: a bipartisan coalition of 44 state attorneys general sent a formal letter to major AI companies expressing grave concerns about child safety, while the Federal Trade Commission launched an investigation into seven tech companies — including Google, Character.AI, Meta, OpenAI, and others — over AI chatbots' potential harm to teenagers. California enacted a law requiring platforms to remind minor users every three hours via pop-up notification that they are interacting with a chatbot and not a human, and mandating protocols to detect suicidal ideation and refer users to crisis services. Character.AI itself, amid mounting lawsuits, announced in October 2025 that it would ban users under 18 from open-ended chats with its AI personas and introduced a new age-verification system — a change its critics described as arriving years too late.

---

**Learning Setbacks**

The educational record is more contested but increasingly troubling. In a large randomized trial in secondary mathematics, students using GPT-based tutors performed worse on follow-up exams without AI support, showed no long-term learning gains, and substantially overestimated their preparedness — consistent with a "learning penalty" driven by cognitive offloading and an inflated sense of mastery. Researchers have labeled this pattern "vaporized learning": AI tools may boost short-term performance metrics like test scores while eroding long-term retention and genuine understanding, with systematic reviews documenting cognitive offloading, reduced neural activity during learning tasks, and loss of student agency as documented outcomes.

The mental-health dimension persists even in purely academic settings. There are documented cases of students who self-harmed or died by suicide after using AI for mental health support, and a 2025 study found that chatbots responding to simulated mental-health scenarios sometimes made harmful proposals — including advising users to cut off all human contact for a month or to drop out of school. A Center for Democracy and Technology report found that AI use in schools comes with real risks including large-scale data breaches, tech-fueled sexual harassment and bullying, and treating students unfairly.

---

**What Has Changed — and What Has Not**

The institutional response has been uneven. Companies have introduced parental controls, age-verification systems, and crisis-referral protocols. States have passed notification mandates. Courts have allowed wrongful-death suits to proceed. Yet the number of monthly AI-related incidents tracked by the OECD surged by 50 percent over a six-month window, partly reflecting increased deployment but also revealing novel failure modes that existing frameworks do not address. The United States still lacks federal frameworks to respond to AI incidents, despite having robust analogous systems for aviation failures and medical device problems. The pattern running across all three harm categories — security, well-being, and learning — is the same: deployment has consistently outpaced the governance designed to contain it.

Which national and regional AI-in-education regulations currently exist, and how do their approaches compare?

## AI in Education: The Global Regulatory Landscape

The regulation of artificial intelligence in educational settings is now one of the most active — and most contested — areas of technology policy worldwide. Three distinct regulatory philosophies have emerged, each reflecting deeper assumptions about the proper relationship between states, institutions, and technology.

---

### The European Union: Rights-Based, Risk-Stratified Law

The most architecturally ambitious framework is the EU AI Act, the world's first comprehensive legal framework governing AI development and deployment, which classifies AI systems by risk level — from minimal to unacceptable — and assigns corresponding compliance obligations. For the education sector, this classification has direct consequences. Education sits at the intersection of two areas the Act treats with particular care: high-risk AI applications and the protection of vulnerable populations. Many common school applications — from automated essay grading and adaptive learning platforms to dropout prediction tools — fall squarely into the high-risk category under Annex III, covering AI systems that determine access, assess learning outcomes, or influence the educational path of individuals.

The prohibitions are explicit and already enforceable. Among eight banned practices, emotion recognition in educational institutions and real-time remote biometric identification are flatly prohibited; those prohibitions became effective in February 2025. Any system designed to infer student emotions from facial, vocal, or biometric cues is classified as "unacceptable risk" and is banned in EU educational settings.

The timeline has recently shifted, however. A legislative simplification package — the so-called "AI omnibus" — was adopted in November 2025, with a political agreement reached in May 2026. Under that agreement, the full compliance rules for high-risk AI systems in education will now apply from December 2027. Penalties for non-compliance are substantial: placing a non-compliant high-risk AI system on the market can result in fines of up to 15 million euros or 3 percent of global annual turnover, whichever is higher.

Alongside the binding Act, the European Commission has issued ethical guidelines for educators and the guidelines include clear explanations of the AI Act, the General Data Protection Regulation, and the principles of responsible AI use. The European Schools system, notably, has been candid that no generative AI tools have yet been officially approved for system-wide use, with approval procedures still under development.

---

### The United States: Federalism Without a Federal Floor

Across the Atlantic, the picture is one of deliberate decentralization. There is no comprehensive federal AI-in-education statute. The existing federal scaffolding rests on older laws: FERPA protects the privacy of student education records, but its last regulatory updates predate the widespread use of technology in learning environments, leaving institutions to interpret it in light of artificial intelligence. There are few federal regulations that govern AI directly at the higher education level.

The Trump administration's April 2025 executive order on AI education frames the issue almost entirely in competitiveness terms. It is the policy of the United States to promote AI literacy and proficiency among Americans by promoting the appropriate integration of AI into education, providing comprehensive AI training for educators, and fostering early exposure to AI concepts to develop an AI-ready workforce. The Department of Education subsequently issued grant guidance outlining how AI may be used across key educational functions and articulating principles for responsible use, affirming such uses are allowable under existing federal education programs.

With the federal level providing only encouragement and grant incentives, states have become the primary regulatory arena. When ChatGPT debuted in 2022, no states had policies related to generative AI; by April 2025, at least 28 states had engaged with the issue legislatively. The legislative priorities reveal a pragmatic rather than rights-based orientation. Fifteen bills in the 2025 session alone focused on promoting AI literacy for students and professional development for teachers, making this the top legislative priority. Several states have moved toward guardrails on specific harms: Nevada enacted legislation prohibiting public schools from using AI-powered tools to perform the functions of a school counselor, psychologist, or social worker in relation to student mental health. Vendor accountability is a newer frontier: legislators are increasingly mandating that AI EdTech vendors submit documentation about how they handle and use student data to train their products.

State approaches vary widely in ambition. States like California, Connecticut, and Texas have introduced bills to create oversight boards and "regulatory sandboxes" where AI tools can be tested before broader rollout. Tennessee, by contrast, passed a law mandating that all K-12 districts, charter schools, and universities adopt their own AI policies, with the Tennessee School Boards Association subsequently developing a model policy that most districts adopted in full.

---

### China: State-Directed Integration with Embedded Guardrails

China's approach is neither rights-centered like Europe's nor market-permissive like the United States'. It is best understood as state-directed deployment, combining ambitious top-down mandates with granular behavioral rules. China aims to promote AI in aiding education reform, with a joint guideline from the Ministry of Education and eight other departments emphasizing building an AI-based education system that integrates smart technologies into teaching, learning, assessment, and academic research.

The Ministry of Education issued two guidelines in May 2025: one for AI general education in primary and secondary schools, and one for the use of generative AI — prohibiting students from independently using open-ended content generation at the primary level and banning teachers from using generative AI as a substitute for their core teaching responsibilities. The curriculum is structured as a developmental ladder: primary school students encounter basic technologies like voice recognition and image classification; junior high students examine machine learning processes and develop critical thinking about misinformation in AI outputs; senior secondary students design and refine AI algorithm models while cultivating interdisciplinary systems thinking.

Several regions — including Beijing, Guangzhou, and Chengdu — have rolled out implementation plans specifying class hour requirements at each school stage and incorporating AI course evaluations into students' overall assessments; Beijing's primary and secondary schools will fully implement general AI education starting in fall 2025, with no fewer than eight class hours per academic year.

At the university level, academic integrity has become a pressure point. At Tianjin University of Science and Technology, AI-generated content in undergraduate theses must not exceed 40 percent under detection measures introduced in 2024. Fudan University's regulations ban AI use in six areas of academic work, including research design, original data collection, and thesis writing.
